# Social influence and conformity

## Definition

**Social influence and conformity** names the body of social-psychology theory and experiment concerned with how other people change what an individual believes, judges, or does. The empirical core was laid down by three classic paradigms. Sherif's autokinetic experiments ([[sherif-1936-psychology-social-norms]]) showed that when a stimulus is genuinely ambiguous — a stationary point of light that appears to drift in a dark room with no frame of reference — individuals tested together converge from idiosyncratic personal estimates onto a shared group norm that then persists, even carrying over to later solitary sessions; consensus emerges bottom-up and is internalized. Asch's line-judgment paradigm ([[asch-1955-opinions-and-social-pressure]]) showed the converse case: on an *unambiguous* perceptual task, a unanimous but wrong majority drives a substantial fraction of people to publicly endorse a manifestly incorrect answer, with conformity rising as the majority grows to three or four and collapsing the instant a single dissenting ally breaks unanimity. Deutsch and Gerard ([[deutsch-gerard-1955-normative-informational-influence]]) then drew the field's foundational conceptual cut, distinguishing **informational** influence (accepting others' responses as evidence about reality) from **normative** influence (going along to gain approval or avoid disapproval), and showed the two channels dissociate — making responses anonymous attenuates the normative component while leaving an informational residue.

Around these experiments sits a wider theoretical apparatus that the source notes also cover. French's formal theory of social power ([[french-1956-formal-theory-social-power]]) is the earliest explicitly mathematical treatment, modeling a group as a directed influence graph and proving that iterated power-weighted averaging drives opinions to consensus. French and Raven's bases of social power ([[french-raven-1959-bases-of-social-power]]) decompose influence by its source — reward, coercive, legitimate, referent, expert — predicting that different bases produce qualitatively different (surveillance-dependent versus internalized) change. Festinger's social comparison theory ([[festinger-1954-social-comparison-processes]]) supplies the mechanism that comparison, and hence influence, is biased toward *similar* others and generates pressures toward uniformity, while his cognitive dissonance theory ([[festinger-1957-cognitive-dissonance]]) explains why beliefs also resist updating once committed. Lewin's field theory ([[lewin-1951-field-theory-social-science]]) frames the whole subject as motion of a person within a "life space" under a field of psychological forces. Later strands extend the picture in two directions that any influence model must confront: minority influence, where a numerically small but *consistent* dissenting minority can durably convert a majority ([[moscovici-lage-naffrechoux-1969-minority-influence]]), and social identity, where influence is structured by in-group/out-group boundaries rather than applied uniformly ([[tajfel-turner-1979-integrative-theory-intergroup-conflict]]). The modern synthesis organizes the heterogeneous phenomena around the goals of accuracy, affiliation, and positive self-concept ([[cialdini-goldstein-2004-social-influence-review]]), catalogues the practical levers — social proof, consistency, authority, liking, reciprocity, scarcity ([[cialdini-2007-influence-psychology-persuasion]]) — and quantifies the Asch effect across cultures through meta-analysis ([[bond-smith-1996-culture-and-conformity]]).

## Why it matters here

This literature is an empirical and interpretive benchmark for [[SocialPhysics]], not a set of phenomena already recovered by one functional. The current theorem-first record [[belief-inertia-2026-07-12-theorem-first-revision]] proves continuous-time DeGroot only for fixed symmetric coupling under the primary unweighted product Fisher metric, and for nonuniform reversible coupling only after adding the $\rho$-weighted metric or equivalent agent-specific rates. It gives a restricted anchored Friedkin--Johnsen equilibrium and a soft bounded-confidence analog; it does not derive general directed French/DeGroot influence, conformity, minority conversion, or cascade phenomena. Sherif, Asch, and Festinger therefore supply comparison targets for likelihood strength, network topology, and similarity selectivity rather than validation of the model ([[french-1956-formal-theory-social-power]], [[asch-1955-opinions-and-social-pressure]], [[festinger-1954-social-comparison-processes]], [[Opinion dynamics]]).

The honest accounting separates machinery from analogy. The Deutsch--Gerard informational/normative split motivates keeping evidential coupling, anchoring, and attention regularization conceptually distinct. Festinger's dissonance is not identified with the loss Hessian or kinetic mass: for $\dot\delta=-\eta G^{-1}H_F\delta$, fixed $G$ and $\eta$ make larger positive stiffness reduce matched-force displacement and speed linear relaxation. Slower revision needs a separate mobility, damping, kinetic, or slow-state mechanism ([[Belief inertia]], [[Belief perseverance and confirmation bias]]). Lewin, French--Raven, Cialdini, Tajfel--Turner, and minority-influence studies remain intellectual lineage, calibration targets, or extension prompts. They do not supply theorem-level identities for the current gauge-VFE equations.

## Details

The experimental backbone divides along stimulus ambiguity, and this division is the cleanest map into the VFE terms. Sherif used genuinely ambiguous stimuli, where convergence is informational and durable, corresponding to high observation variance and a weak likelihood term so the coupling gradient dominates and the consensus is internalized as a stable basin ([[sherif-1936-psychology-social-norms]]). Asch used unambiguous stimuli, where conformity is largely normative and public — the participant knows the right answer but yields anyway — and the central quantitative regularities are that conformity rises with majority size and then plateaus past three or four, that unanimity is essential (one ally collapses it), and that the correct answer is obvious in controls (error under one percent) so the social pressure is isolated from perceptual difficulty ([[asch-1955-opinions-and-social-pressure]]). Deutsch and Gerard converted Asch's interpretive mixture of "distortion of judgment, action, and perception" into the operational two-channel decomposition, showing that anonymity (removing the gaze that drives normative pressure) reduces but does not eliminate conformity, leaving the informational residue, and that committing privately to a judgment before exposure attenuates the normative pull ([[deutsch-gerard-1955-normative-informational-influence]]). Schematically an agent's expressed judgment is a precision-weighted compromise between a private perceptual estimate $o_i$ and a social signal $\bar{x}_{-i}$, with the relative weight set by stimulus ambiguity and response visibility — the same trade-off the self-coupling $\alpha\,\mathrm{KL}(q_i\|p_i)$ versus belief-coupling $\sum_j\beta_{ij}\mathrm{KL}(q_i\|\Omega_{ij}q_j)$ balance encodes.

The formal lineage runs from French through DeGroot to the restricted VFE comparison. French represents influence as a row-stochastic power matrix $W$ with the update $\mathbf{x}^{(t+1)}=W\mathbf{x}^{(t)}$ ([[french-1956-formal-theory-social-power]]). The gauge-VFE theorem matches only the fixed symmetric primary-metric case, or a nonuniform reversible case after the additional stationary-measure weighting; frozen coupling alone is not enough for a general directed identity. French and Raven's heterogeneous power bases and Festinger's social comparison and dissonance theories therefore motivate source-dependent weights, similarity kernels, and measurement targets rather than results inherited by the model ([[french-raven-1959-bases-of-social-power]], [[festinger-1954-social-comparison-processes]], [[festinger-1957-cognitive-dissonance]]).

The boundary cases are where the program is tested and the prose theories add structure. Moscovici, Lage, and Naffrechoux's blue-green study showed a *consistent* two-confederate minority calling blue slides green could both shift a fraction of majority responses and durably lower the majority's blue-green perceptual threshold, while an inconsistent minority produced nothing — isolating behavioral consistency, not numbers, as the active variable and challenging any purely size-weighted model ([[moscovici-lage-naffrechoux-1969-minority-influence]]). Tajfel and Turner's social identity theory shows that mere categorization can produce in-group favoritism and accentuation effects even without material conflict, so alignment is group-structured rather than uniform ([[tajfel-turner-1979-integrative-theory-intergroup-conflict]]). Lewin's field theory remains a qualitative force-field metaphor ([[lewin-1951-field-theory-social-science]]). Cialdini, Bond, and Smith provide phenomenology and quantitative benchmarks for influence strength, majority-size saturation, and cultural moderation, not validation that one functional recovers those phenomena ([[cialdini-goldstein-2004-social-influence-review]], [[cialdini-2007-influence-psychology-persuasion]], [[bond-smith-1996-culture-and-conformity]]). These results specify empirical targets and boundary cases for [[SocialPhysics]] rather than consequences of its current theorem set.
