# Social influence and conformity

## Definition

**Social influence and conformity** names the body of social-psychology theory and experiment concerned with how other people change what an individual believes, judges, or does. The empirical core was laid down by three classic paradigms. Sherif's autokinetic experiments ([[sherif-1936-psychology-social-norms]]) showed that when a stimulus is genuinely ambiguous — a stationary point of light that appears to drift in a dark room with no frame of reference — individuals tested together converge from idiosyncratic personal estimates onto a shared group norm that then persists, even carrying over to later solitary sessions; consensus emerges bottom-up and is internalized. Asch's line-judgment paradigm ([[asch-1955-opinions-and-social-pressure]]) showed the converse case: on an *unambiguous* perceptual task, a unanimous but wrong majority drives a substantial fraction of people to publicly endorse a manifestly incorrect answer, with conformity rising as the majority grows to three or four and collapsing the instant a single dissenting ally breaks unanimity. Deutsch and Gerard ([[deutsch-gerard-1955-normative-informational-influence]]) then drew the field's foundational conceptual cut, distinguishing **informational** influence (accepting others' responses as evidence about reality) from **normative** influence (going along to gain approval or avoid disapproval), and showed the two channels dissociate — making responses anonymous attenuates the normative component while leaving an informational residue.

Around these experiments sits a wider theoretical apparatus that the source notes also cover. French's formal theory of social power ([[french-1956-formal-theory-social-power]]) is the earliest explicitly mathematical treatment, modeling a group as a directed influence graph and proving that iterated power-weighted averaging drives opinions to consensus. French and Raven's bases of social power ([[french-raven-1959-bases-of-social-power]]) decompose influence by its source — reward, coercive, legitimate, referent, expert — predicting that different bases produce qualitatively different (surveillance-dependent versus internalized) change. Festinger's social comparison theory ([[festinger-1954-social-comparison-processes]]) supplies the mechanism that comparison, and hence influence, is biased toward *similar* others and generates pressures toward uniformity, while his cognitive dissonance theory ([[festinger-1957-cognitive-dissonance]]) explains why beliefs also resist updating once committed. Lewin's field theory ([[lewin-1951-field-theory-social-science]]) frames the whole subject as motion of a person within a "life space" under a field of psychological forces. Later strands extend the picture in two directions that any influence model must confront: minority influence, where a numerically small but *consistent* dissenting minority can durably convert a majority ([[moscovici-lage-naffrechoux-1969-minority-influence]]), and social identity, where influence is structured by in-group/out-group boundaries rather than applied uniformly ([[tajfel-turner-1979-integrative-theory-intergroup-conflict]]). The modern synthesis organizes the heterogeneous phenomena around the goals of accuracy, affiliation, and positive self-concept ([[cialdini-goldstein-2004-social-influence-review]]), catalogues the practical levers — social proof, consistency, authority, liking, reciprocity, scarcity ([[cialdini-2007-influence-psychology-persuasion]]) — and quantifies the Asch effect across cultures through meta-analysis ([[bond-smith-1996-culture-and-conformity]]).

## Why it matters here

This literature is the human-behavior evidence base that the SocialPhysics program ([[SocialPhysics]]), founded on the manuscript [[belief-inertia]], is meant to recover and explain. The connection is strongest at the level of the coupling terms in [[Multi-agent variational free energy]]. When agents carry Gaussian beliefs and disagreement is the gauge-transported divergence $\mathrm{KL}(q_i \,\|\, \Omega_{ij}[q_j])$ weighted by softmax attention $\beta_{ij} = \mathrm{softmax}_j(-\mathrm{KL}(q_i\|\Omega_{ij}[q_j])/(\kappa\sqrt{K}))$, the first-order (overdamped) gradient flow $\dot\mu_i \propto -\partial F/\partial\mu_i$ reduces in the linear-Gaussian limit to French's iterated power-weighted averaging, with $\beta_{ij}$ playing the role of French's exogenous power matrix $W_{ij}$ ([[french-1956-formal-theory-social-power]], [[Opinion dynamics]]). Sherif's autokinetic convergence is the same flow run from heterogeneous initial beliefs under a *weak* observation-likelihood term (high stimulus variance), where the social-coupling gradient dominates and beliefs collapse to a shared $\mu$ — and the durability of his internalized norm is the empirical signature of consensus becoming a stable fixed point rather than a transient compromise. Asch supplies the complementary calibration: the social term $\beta_{ij}\mathrm{KL}(q_i\|\Omega_{ij}q_j)$ must be able to override a *sharp* likelihood $\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(o\mid x)]$, and the unanimity-and-ally phenomenology is a falsifiable prediction about the topology of the coupling graph — one dissenting neighbour, an alternative coupling target, should restore independence ([[asch-1955-opinions-and-social-pressure]], [[Echo chambers and polarization]]). Festinger's "influence decreases with belief distance" is precisely what the softmax-of-KL kernel encodes, giving the attention construction genuine mechanism-level grounding ([[festinger-1954-social-comparison-processes]]).

The honest accounting separates machinery the functional actually uses from adjacent context. The Deutsch-Gerard informational/normative split maps cleanly onto distinct VFE terms — informational influence is the evidential belief coupling (neighbours absorbed as observations would be), while normative influence is closer to prior-conformity anchoring or the attention-entropy regularizer $\tau\,\beta_{ij}\log(\beta_{ij}/\pi_{ij})$ — and this is exactly the justification the manuscript needs for keeping coupling and prior terms conceptually distinct; this link is strong. Festinger's cognitive dissonance grounds belief perseverance through a high self-coupling weight $\alpha\,\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)$, but it must be flagged that dissonance is a *static* stiffness (a large $\alpha$, a stiff spring), not the inertial-mass machinery of the underdamped ansatz $M\ddot\mu + \gamma\dot\mu + \nabla F = 0$ ([[Belief inertia]], [[Belief perseverance and confirmation bias]]); conflating static resistance with belief momentum would be the kind of loose analogy to avoid. Lewin is intellectual lineage rather than an equation source — he supplies the picture of behavior as motion in a force field, the prose ancestor of both the overdamped flow and the underdamped force balance, while the VFE functional supplies the actual potential $F$, the metric/mass, and the equations of motion ([[Hamiltonian belief dynamics]]). French-Raven and Cialdini are interpretive and calibration context: the bases of power and the levers of persuasion (authority, liking, expert, referent) imply that real coupling weights are heterogeneous and asymmetric ($\beta_{ij}\neq\beta_{ji}$), which the bare symmetric softmax-of-divergence does not by itself supply, so they motivate extensions rather than describe current machinery. Tajfel-Turner motivates the *structure* of coupling — in-group assimilation and out-group differentiation map onto block structure in the $\beta$ graph and the emergence of [[Community detection and modularity|communities]], [[Echo chambers and polarization|echo chambers]], and coarse-grained [[Meta-agents and hierarchical emergence|meta-agents]] — but it motivates the structure, not a specific energy term. And minority influence is honestly an *open challenge*: a naive size-weighted averaging flow washes out a small minority, whereas a consistent, confident minority carries low-$\Sigma$ high-precision beliefs (and, in the underdamped reading, large effective inertial mass) that could resist being averaged away and drag the majority over time — whether the functional yields durable *conversion* rather than transient compliance is a concrete falsifiable test, not an established result ([[moscovici-lage-naffrechoux-1969-minority-influence]]). The connection to [[Collective active inference]] is natural, since the same variational substrate frames conformity as agents minimizing surprise over a shared generative model.

## Details

The experimental backbone divides along stimulus ambiguity, and this division is the cleanest map into the VFE terms. Sherif used genuinely ambiguous stimuli, where convergence is informational and durable, corresponding to high observation variance and a weak likelihood term so the coupling gradient dominates and the consensus is internalized as a stable basin ([[sherif-1936-psychology-social-norms]]). Asch used unambiguous stimuli, where conformity is largely normative and public — the participant knows the right answer but yields anyway — and the central quantitative regularities are that conformity rises with majority size and then plateaus past three or four, that unanimity is essential (one ally collapses it), and that the correct answer is obvious in controls (error under one percent) so the social pressure is isolated from perceptual difficulty ([[asch-1955-opinions-and-social-pressure]]). Deutsch and Gerard converted Asch's interpretive mixture of "distortion of judgment, action, and perception" into the operational two-channel decomposition, showing that anonymity (removing the gaze that drives normative pressure) reduces but does not eliminate conformity, leaving the informational residue, and that committing privately to a judgment before exposure attenuates the normative pull ([[deutsch-gerard-1955-normative-informational-influence]]). Schematically an agent's expressed judgment is a precision-weighted compromise between a private perceptual estimate $o_i$ and a social signal $\bar{x}_{-i}$, with the relative weight set by stimulus ambiguity and response visibility — the same trade-off the self-coupling $\alpha\,\mathrm{KL}(q_i\|p_i)$ versus belief-coupling $\sum_j\beta_{ij}\mathrm{KL}(q_i\|\Omega_{ij}q_j)$ balance encodes.

The formal lineage runs from French through DeGroot to the VFE flow. French represents influence as a row-stochastic power matrix $W$ with the update $\mathbf{x}^{(t+1)} = W\,\mathbf{x}^{(t)}$ and proves that on an appropriately connected influence digraph the iteration converges to a power-weighted average of the initial opinions ([[french-1956-formal-theory-social-power]]); this is the direct precursor of DeGroot consensus and therefore of the overdamped limit the belief-inertia functional recovers, with the caveat that $W$ is fixed and exogenous whereas $\beta_{ij}=\mathrm{softmax}(-\mathrm{KL}/\tau)$ is belief-dependent, so the correspondence is exact only in the frozen-coupling limit. French and Raven's five bases refine what those weights represent — reward and coercive power produce surveillance-dependent compliance that decays when monitoring stops, while legitimate, referent, and expert power produce internalized durable change — implying coupling weights that are heterogeneous and source-dependent rather than uniform up to belief distance ([[french-raven-1959-bases-of-social-power]]). Festinger's social comparison theory supplies the psychological reason the weights fall off with distance (comparison is biased toward similar others; pressure toward uniformity grows with similarity and group cohesiveness; groups narrow their comparison range and reject the most divergent members when uniformity fails) — the seed of both the softmax kernel and the polarization regime ([[festinger-1954-social-comparison-processes]]) — while his dissonance theory adds the resistance-to-update side, where inconsistency among committed cognitions is aversive and biases the agent against large belief moves ([[festinger-1957-cognitive-dissonance]]).

The boundary cases are where the program is tested and the prose theories add structure. Moscovici, Lage, and Naffrechoux's blue-green study showed a *consistent* two-confederate minority calling blue slides green could both shift a fraction of majority responses and durably lower the majority's blue-green perceptual threshold (a latent conversion detectable on a later private after-task), while an inconsistent minority produced nothing — isolating behavioral consistency, not numbers, as the active variable and standing as a challenge to any purely size-weighted model ([[moscovici-lage-naffrechoux-1969-minority-influence]]). Tajfel and Turner's social identity theory shows, via the minimal-group experiments, that mere categorization into "us" and "them" suffices to produce in-group favouritism and accentuation effects (exaggerated within-group similarity and between-group difference) even absent any material conflict, so alignment is group-structured rather than uniform ([[tajfel-turner-1979-integrative-theory-intergroup-conflict]]). Lewin's field theory provides the unifying metaphor through $B = f(P, E)$ — behavior as the resultant of psychological forces in a life space, with quasi-stationary equilibria held by opposing driving and restraining forces — the qualitative ancestor of both VFE regimes ([[lewin-1951-field-theory-social-science]]). The contemporary syntheses then serve as anchors and validation rather than load-bearing theory: Cialdini and Goldstein's accuracy/affiliation/self-concept framework parallels the informational/normative split and is the review-of-record for grounding the claim that one functional recovers classical influence phenomena ([[cialdini-goldstein-2004-social-influence-review]]); Cialdini's six principles are a phenomenology checklist of levers — social proof as a descriptive-norm analogue of belief coupling, consistency as a cousin of prior anchoring — against which to test the coupling terms ([[cialdini-2007-influence-psychology-persuasion]]); and Bond and Smith's meta-analysis of 133 line-judgment studies across 17 countries converts the Asch effect into quantitative effect sizes, giving the saturating dependence of conformity on majority size as a target for how the aggregate weight $\sum_j\beta_{ij}$ should scale, and the collectivist-versus-individualist contrast as a candidate calibration for the global coupling temperature or prior-conformity strength ([[bond-smith-1996-culture-and-conformity]]). Taken together these works place the SocialPhysics coupling terms within the broader [[Statistical physics of social systems and collective behavior]] program, supplying the empirical regularities the overdamped flow must reproduce and the boundary cases — minority conversion, group-structured polarization — that probe its limits.
