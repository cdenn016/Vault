# Mean-field games and continuum limits

## Definition

**Mean-field games (MFG)** and the broader family of **continuum (mean-field, kinetic, hydrodynamic) limits** are the mathematics of what a population of interacting agents becomes when the number of agents is sent to infinity. The unifying idea is that an individual agent in a large symmetric population has negligible effect on the aggregate, so each agent can be treated as responding not to every other agent but to the *statistical distribution* of the whole population. Two distinct but related programs sit under this heading. The first is mean-field games proper, founded independently and almost simultaneously by Lasry and Lions on the analytic side ([[lasry-lions-2007-mean-field-games]]) and by Huang, Malhamé and Caines in the systems-and-control community ([[huang-malhame-caines-2006-nash-certainty-equivalence]]): here each agent is a rational optimizer choosing a control to minimize its own cost, coupled to the others only through the population density, and the equilibrium is a self-consistency (Nash) fixed point in which the distribution that agents best-respond to is exactly the distribution their optimal play generates. This produces a coupled forward–backward system — a backward Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman equation propagating each agent's value function and a forward Fokker–Planck equation transporting the population mass. The second program is the **continuum limit of interacting-particle (alignment/aggregation) models**: starting from agent-based ordinary differential equations such as flocking ([[cucker-smale-2007-emergent-behavior-flocks]]) or opinion alignment, one passes through a kinetic (Vlasov-type) mesoscopic description to a macroscopic hydrodynamic PDE for density and mean state ([[carrillo-fornasier-toscani-vecil-2010-particle-kinetic-hydrodynamic-swarming]], [[degond-motsch-2008-continuum-limit-self-driven-particles]]). The mean-field-games and the interacting-particle threads meet in the gradient-flow / variational formulation, where the continuum dynamics are realized as steepest descent of a free-energy functional in the Wasserstein metric over probability measures ([[carrillo-craig-yao-2019-aggregation-diffusion-equations]]).

## Why it matters here

This concept is the rigorous mathematical home of the **thermodynamic-limit (continuum) side** of the SocialPhysics program — the passage from a finite population of [[Multi-agent variational free energy|VFE agents]] to a continuum belief field that [[belief-inertia]] gestures at and that [[Meta-entropy]] is meant to make precise through configuration counting. The honest assessment is that this is positioning canon and a methodological toolbox, not machinery the belief-inertia functional currently invokes: every paper below sits at varying distances from the program's actual derivations, and the link strength must be stated plainly rather than dressed up as identity.

The closest genuine structural correspondence is to the **Lasry–Lions forward–backward system**. The forward Fokker–Planck equation is exactly the evolution one would write for a belief-density field, and the backward HJB equation is the variational/optimality side that the program's free-energy minimization would supply; the program's mean-field continuum should be positioned explicitly against this pair, and the open question worth asking is whether a VFE-derived, gauge-transported coupling satisfies a Lasry–Lions-type **monotonicity condition** that would guarantee a *unique* consensus equilibrium rather than multiple polarized ones ([[lasry-lions-2007-mean-field-games]], [[cardaliaguet-2013-notes-on-mean-field-games]]). Equally direct is the **micro-to-macro consistency closure** of the Nash Certainty Equivalence principle ([[huang-malhame-caines-2006-nash-certainty-equivalence]]): each agent optimizes against an aggregate that its own optimized behavior must reproduce, the same self-referential fixed point the program needs when it claims a rigorous representative-belief-agent limit. The caveat, repeated across the MFG sources, is that mean-field games are built on optimal control with an *exogenous* cost and a Hamiltonian, whereas the program's dynamics descend from a *free-energy gradient flow* with [[Gauge transformation|gauge-transported]] divergences; the correspondence is structural (forward density transport plus backward optimality, plus a consistency closure) rather than a term-by-term identity.

Two further connections touch the program's first- and second-order directions, but remain methodological templates. The current finite-agent theorem gives fixed symmetric DeGroot under the primary unweighted Fisher flow; a nonuniform reversible transient needs $G_\rho$ or agent-specific rates, and the restricted anchored stationary equilibrium is metric-independent. It does not derive a mean-field, kinetic, Wasserstein, or hydrodynamic limit. Aggregation-diffusion gradient flows ([[carrillo-craig-yao-2019-aggregation-diffusion-equations]]) therefore supply tools for a future population-density derivation rather than a continuum image already established by the VFE functional. Cucker–Smale flocking ([[cucker-smale-2007-emergent-behavior-flocks]]) and the Degond–Motsch closure ([[degond-motsch-2008-continuum-limit-self-driven-particles]]) are likewise analogies and candidate techniques for a separately derived kinetic extension. The intention-field construction of Degond, Liu, Merino-Aceituno, and Tardiveau ([[degond-liu-merino-tardiveau-2017-intention-field-social-interaction]]) is a useful target object, not a result presently obtained by [[belief-inertia]].

## Details

**The mean-field-game equilibrium.** Lasry and Lions characterize the continuum game by the coupled forward–backward MFG system,
$$
\begin{aligned}
-\partial_t u - \nu\,\Delta u + H(x, \nabla u) &= f(x, m), \\
\partial_t m - \nu\,\Delta m - \operatorname{div}\!\bigl(m\,\partial_p H(x,\nabla u)\bigr) &= 0,
\end{aligned}
$$
with the value $u$ specified at the terminal time and the density $m$ at the initial time. The HJB equation gives each agent's optimal feedback through $\nabla u$; the Fokker–Planck equation pushes the mass forward under that optimal drift; and the **Lasry–Lions monotonicity condition** — the coupling $f(x,m)$ increasing in $m$, penalizing crowding — yields uniqueness of the equilibrium. The pedagogical lecture notes from Lions' Collège de France course ([[cardaliaguet-2013-notes-on-mean-field-games]]) are the standard entry point: they give the heuristic $N\to\infty$ decoupling of a symmetric game, the rigorous PDE existence arguments, the explicit statement and use of the monotonicity condition, and — most relevant to the program — the *potential (variational) MFG* case, where the forward–backward system is the Euler–Lagrange optimality condition of a single convex functional over densities, mirroring the program's free-energy-functional stance. The engineering-side origin frames the same content through the **Nash Certainty Equivalence** ([[huang-malhame-caines-2006-nash-certainty-equivalence]]): for $N$ linear-quadratic-Gaussian agents minimizing $J_i = \mathbb{E}\int_0^\infty e^{-\rho t}[(x_i - \Phi(\bar x))^2 + r u_i^2]\,dt$ with $\bar x$ the mass average, the decentralized feedback obtained by best-responding to a posited aggregate is an $\varepsilon_N$-Nash equilibrium with $\varepsilon_N \to 0$, a rigorous representative-agent reduction. The probabilistic re-casting of the whole theory by Carmona and Delarue ([[carmona-delarue-2018-probabilistic-theory-mean-field-games]]) replaces the HJB/Fokker–Planck pair with McKean–Vlasov forward–backward SDEs whose coefficients depend on the law of the solution, develops the **master equation** on Wasserstein space $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^d)$, and proves with quantitative rates that finite-$N$ Nash equilibria converge to the mean-field solution; this two-volume monograph is the reference shelf for *how to make such a limit rigorous*, adjacent to rather than embedded in the program's current gradient-flow-plus-meta-entropy derivations.

**The interacting-particle tower.** The second thread builds the same micro-to-macro passage for alignment and aggregation models. The canonical second-order particle model combines self-propulsion/friction with a pairwise interaction potential ([[carrillo-fornasier-toscani-vecil-2010-particle-kinetic-hydrodynamic-swarming]]),
$$
\dot x_i = v_i, \qquad
\dot v_i = (\alpha - \beta\,\lVert v_i\rVert^2)\,v_i - \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j\ne i}\nabla U(\lVert x_i - x_j\rVert),
$$
whose mean-field limit is a Vlasov equation $\partial_t f + v\cdot\nabla_x f + \nabla_v\cdot(F[f]\,f) = 0$ and whose hydrodynamic limit yields Euler-type equations for density and mean velocity, producing mills, flocks, and double mills. The Cucker–Smale model ([[cucker-smale-2007-emergent-behavior-flocks]]) is the cleanest rigorous instance: with symmetric distance-weighted communication $a_{ij} = H/(1 + \lVert x_i - x_j\rVert^2)^{\beta}$, the velocities converge to a common limit for *any* initial data when $\beta < \tfrac12$ (unconditional flocking) and only conditionally for $\beta \ge \tfrac12$, proved by tracking spatial and velocity variances as a Lyapunov pair — a proof technique worth importing for any belief-consensus theorem. Motsch and Tadmor ([[motsch-tadmor-2014-heterophilious-dynamics-consensus]]) carry the alignment principle $\dot x_i = \sum_j a_{ij}(x_j - x_i)$ consistently across particle, kinetic, and hydrodynamic scales, and establish the counterintuitive **heterophily** result: locally normalized, *non-symmetric* weights $a_{ij} = \phi(\lVert x_i - x_j\rVert)/\sum_k \phi(\lVert x_i - x_k\rVert)$ — which depend on agent $i$'s own neighbourhood and are therefore the structural parallel of the program's asymmetric, row-normalized softmax coupling $\beta_{ij}$ — make consensus *more* robust, and emphasizing far (disagreeing) neighbours strengthens it further. This is a concrete prediction worth re-examining under gauge transport: does emphasizing large transported $\mathrm{KL}(q_i \| \Omega_{ij} q_j)$ help or hinder consensus once the softmax temperature $\tau = \kappa\sqrt{K}$ controls attention concentration. The curved-state-space closure problem is solved by Degond and Motsch ([[degond-motsch-2008-continuum-limit-self-driven-particles]]): for orientations on the sphere $\mathbb{S}^{n-1}$ the standard hydrodynamic closure fails because the alignment operator conserves nothing usual, and the fix is a *generalized collisional invariant* tailored to the operator, yielding closed macroscopic equations for density $\rho$ and mean direction $\Omega$ with the projector $(\mathrm{Id} - \Omega\otimes\Omega)$ keeping $\Omega$ on the sphere — exactly the kind of construction a belief-field closure on the GL(K) manifold would require.

**The variational / gradient-flow bridge.** The two threads converge in the aggregation-diffusion picture ([[carrillo-craig-yao-2019-aggregation-diffusion-equations]]), where the continuum equation
$$
\partial_t \rho = \nabla \cdot \bigl( \rho\, \nabla [\, U'(\rho) + W * \rho \,] \bigr)
$$
is the Wasserstein gradient flow of the free energy $\mathcal{E}[\rho] = \int U(\rho)\,dx + \tfrac12\iint W(x-y)\rho(x)\rho(y)\,dx\,dy$, with the convex diffusive part $U$ competing against the concentrating nonlocal kernel $W$ to decide whether mass disperses, settles into a steady state, or blows up. This is the closest continuum analogue to the program's free-energy-functional viewpoint, and the structural map (coupling $\leftrightarrow$ attraction, entropy $\leftrightarrow$ diffusion) is genuinely illuminating, with the caveat that porting it requires a Wasserstein-gradient-flow formulation over measures on a curved manifold with gauge transport in the interaction kernel. The single most on-target derivation is the intention-field paper ([[degond-liu-merino-tardiveau-2017-intention-field-social-interaction]]): from a stochastic agent model in which each individual carries an intention $\omega$ gently pulled toward local consensus, a grazing-collision (small-jump, high-frequency) limit produces a Fokker–Planck equation $\partial_t f + \dots = \nabla_\omega\cdot[D\nabla_\omega f + (\omega - \Omega[f])f]$ for the intention distribution, which is then closed into an evolution equation for the macroscopic mean-intention field whose equilibrium manifold is analyzed for the loss of stability of the uniform state and the appearance of structured (non-uniform) opinion states. The grazing-collision route is directly analogous to how a population of small, frequent $\beta_{ij}$-weighted belief updates would aggregate into a continuum equation for the mean belief, and the equilibrium analysis is exactly the consensus-versus-structured-state characterization the program needs — supplying the derivation pattern and the analysis style, though not a ready-made belief-field PDE, since the program's beliefs are Gaussian tuples on a GL(K) manifold coupled through gauge-transported KL divergences rather than a scalar intention under plain attraction. Taken together, these works locate the program's continuum ambitions squarely within the broader [[Statistical physics of social systems and collective behavior]] tradition: a rigorous, multiscale account of when many coupled agents organize into consensus, structured equilibria, or persistent fragmentation.
