# Relativized a priori

## Definition

The **relativized a priori** is the philosophy-of-science thesis, originating with the early Reichenbach and sharpened by Michael Friedman, that scientific knowledge rests on principles that are *constitutive* of objectivity yet *historically revisable*. It splits Kant's single notion of the a priori into two senses that Kant had fused. Kant's a priori (in the [[kant-1781-critique-pure-reason|Critique of Pure Reason]]) carried two marks: it is *necessary and unrevisable*, and it is *constitutive of the object of experience* — the form that makes objective empirical knowledge possible at all (Euclidean space and the causal principle being his exemplars). Reichenbach, in *Relativitätstheorie und Erkenntnis a priori* ([[reichenbach-1920-relativity-apriori|1920]]), argued that general relativity had retained the second mark while destroying the first: a coordinating framework of geometry and physical principles is still required before measurement can have determinate content, but *which* framework is correct is decided by the progress of physics and can change. The a priori becomes constitutive-but-relative-to-a-theory. Friedman's *Dynamics of Reason* ([[friedman-2001-dynamics-of-reason|2001]]) develops this into a layered account in which each mature theory carries a stratum of constitutive principles (paradigmatic mathematics plus coordinating physical principles) that fix what counts as an object and an experiment for that theory, and scientific revolutions are precisely transitions between such constitutive frameworks rather than mere accumulations of fact within one.

## Why it matters here

This concept supplies the epistemological license for one of the more philosophically loaded moves in [[participatory-it-from-bit]]: the reading of an agent's slow generative-model geometry as a *Kantian form of intuition* that is nonetheless learned, fungible, and gauge-dependent. The manuscript identifies an agent's experienced "ambient space" with the structural-tier pullback metric $G^{(s)}_i$, not with an instantaneous prior — exactly the constitutive-yet-revisable role the relativized a priori names. Because each agent's geometry is the pullback of a Fisher-fibre metric along its own [[Gauge transformation|gauge]] frame (see [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]]), the framework already builds in *frame-relativity* of the constitutive structure: different agents, like different theoretical eras, presuppose different conditions of objectivity from the same noumenal substrate. The participatory program thus reads theoretical frameworks themselves as conventions open to collective revision, with a "Kuhnian revolution as collective gauge transformation" gloss that maps [[kuhn-1962-structure|Kuhn's]] paradigm shifts (and the no-loss, structure-preserving reconstruction of them given by [[hoyningen-huene-1993-reconstructing|Hoyningen-Huene]]) onto a coordinated re-fixing of frames across the agent population. This positions the model between [[Structural realism]] (what survives a revolution is structure) and a conventionalist reading (what changes is constitutive choice).

## Details

The relativized a priori is best contrasted with two neighbours. Against naive empiricism it insists there is no theory-neutral observation: a constitutive layer must be in place before evidence bears on hypotheses. Against Kantian apriorism it denies that this layer is fixed once for all. Friedman's distinctive contribution is the *layered* picture — ordinary empirical laws are confirmed against a backdrop of coordinating principles, which are themselves answerable not to direct evidence but to a higher tribunal of inter-framework rational continuity. The link to [[kuhn-1962-structure|Kuhn]] is double-edged: Kuhn supplies the historical phenomenon of incommensurable paradigm change, but Friedman resists Kuhn's relativism by demanding rational bridges between frameworks, and [[hoyningen-huene-1993-reconstructing|Hoyningen-Huene's]] reconstruction shows Kuhn's own view is more structured than the relativist caricature. In the model's idiom, the "collective gauge transformation" reading is attractive but carries the same caveat flagged for the consensus metric in [[participatory-it-from-bit]]: a genuinely frame-independent (revolution-invariant) structure, if it exists, must be recovered by averaging over realised frames and is regulator-dependent over a non-compact group, so the analogy is structural rather than a derivation.
