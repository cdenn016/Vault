# Horizon thermodynamics

Horizon thermodynamics is the program, originating with black-hole thermodynamics and extended to general causal horizons (e.g. Rindler horizons seen by accelerated observers), that assigns a temperature and entropy to any horizon and treats gravitational field equations as thermodynamic equations of state. The **horizon entropy** is proportional to the horizon area, $S = A/(4G)$ (Bekenstein-Hawking). Treating local Rindler (causal) horizons as carrying this area-entropy and the Unruh temperature, Jacobson derived the Einstein field equations from the Clausius relation $\delta Q = T\,dS$, recasting gravity as an equation of state rather than a fundamental force ([[jacobson-1995-einstein-equation-of-state]]). In Padmanabhan's complementary formulation, Einstein's equations near a horizon take the form of the first law ($T\,dS = dE + P\,dV$), so gravity is emergent/thermodynamic rather than fundamental. This observer- and horizon-relative reading of spacetime thermodynamics connects to the participatory, information-theoretic view of physics central to the it-from-bit program.
