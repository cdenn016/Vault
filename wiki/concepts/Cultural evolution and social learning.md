# Cultural evolution and social learning

## Definition

**Cultural evolution** is the research program that treats culture — the body of socially transmitted information held in brains and expressed in behavior and artifacts — as a genuine Darwinian inheritance system running in parallel to genetic inheritance. Its premise is that culture exhibits the Darwinian triad of variation, selection, and inheritance, so the formal toolkit of evolutionary biology (mutation, selection, drift, migration) can be ported to model how cultural variants change in frequency across a population over time ([[mesoudi-2011-cultural-evolution]]). The field's founding mathematical statements deliberately borrowed the recursion-equation machinery of population genetics: Cavalli-Sforza and Feldman built the first quantitative theory of cultural transmission, modeling an offspring trait as a weighted combination of its cultural parents' values plus noise and deriving linear recursions of the form $p_{t+1} = a\,p_t + b$ for trait frequencies ([[cavalli-sforza-feldman-1981-cultural-transmission]]), and Boyd and Richerson cast dual-inheritance (gene-culture coevolution) theory in explicit dynamical models that ask, for each plausible psychology of social learning, what population-level dynamics it produces ([[boyd-richerson-1985-culture-evolutionary-process]], [[richerson-boyd-2005-not-by-genes-alone]]).

**Social learning** is the individual-level mechanism that supplies the inheritance channel: acquiring information by observing or interacting with others rather than by asocial trial and error. The decisive empirical and theoretical refinement of the last two decades is that social learning is not indiscriminate imitation but is governed by *strategies* — evolved, conditional rules specifying *when* to copy and *whom* to copy ([[laland-2004-social-learning-strategies]], [[rendell-2011-cognitive-culture]]). The transmission biases that structure this menu fall into three families: *content (direct) bias*, adopting intrinsically attractive variants on their merits; *frequency-dependent bias*, of which **conformist transmission** is the canonical case, adopting the common variant at a greater-than-proportional rate ([[henrich-boyd-1998-conformist-transmission]]); and *model-based bias*, copying individuals who display markers of success or **prestige** ([[henrich-gilwhite-2001-evolution-of-prestige]]). A parallel tradition, the "epidemiology of representations" or cultural-attraction view, denies that transmission is faithful copying at all: each act of transmission is a reconstructive inference pulled toward shared cognitive **attractors** ([[sperber-1996-explaining-culture]], [[henrich-boyd-2002-modeling-cognition-culture]]). The cumulative product of these mechanisms — the "collective brain" — is adaptive capacity that resides in the interconnected population rather than in any single mind, scaling with population size, connectivity, and transmission fidelity ([[henrich-2016-secret-of-our-success]]).

## Why it matters here

This literature matters to the SocialPhysics program in two distinct ways, and the strength of the connection differs sharply between them. The first is genuine machinery-level lineage for the *overdamped recovery* claim of [[belief-inertia]]. Cavalli-Sforza and Feldman's linear blending recursion $p_{t+1} = a\,p_t + b$ is structurally the population-level analogue of DeGroot averaging and Friedkin-Johnsen anchoring ([[Opinion dynamics]]), and its transmission operators are precisely the population limit that the pairwise gauge-transported coupling $\beta_{ij}\,\mathrm{KL}(q_i \,\|\, \Omega_{ij}[q_j])$ of [[Multi-agent variational free energy]] instantiates at the level of individual Gaussian beliefs. The vertical/horizontal/oblique distinction among transmission channels maps onto the structure of the coupling graph and attention kernel. This is real lineage, with the honest caveat that the classical models are scalar-trait and noise-free whereas the VFE beliefs carry full covariance and gauge frames $\Omega_{ij} = \exp(\phi_i)\exp(-\phi_j)$.

The second connection concerns the *structure of the attention/coupling kernel*, and here the literature functions as a specification of what the dynamics must reproduce rather than as imported mathematics. The social-learning-strategies taxonomy enumerates exactly which neighbors an agent should weight and under what conditions: "copy-when-uncertain" maps directly onto precision-weighted updating — an agent with broad covariance $\Sigma_i$ should place more weight on transported neighbor beliefs — which is the behavior the coupling term produces when its gain scales with the agent's own posterior uncertainty ([[laland-2004-social-learning-strategies]], [[rendell-2011-cognitive-culture]], [[Precision weighting]], [[Fisher information metric]]). The Social Learning Strategies Tournament supplies the same target empirically: the winning "copy-the-best, discount-the-stale" policy is what the softmax weights $\beta_{ij} = \mathrm{softmax}_j(-\mathrm{KL}/(\kappa\sqrt K))$ ought to recover when neighbors are weighted by the quality of their information ([[rendell-2010-why-copy-others]]). Prestige bias is the asymmetric, status-concentrated allocation of learning attention a heterogeneous $\beta_{ij}$ must be able to represent ([[henrich-gilwhite-2001-evolution-of-prestige]]). The relevance here is strong but specificational: these are behaviors the kernel should recover under appropriate weightings, not terms the functional derives.

Two further links touch the program's most distinctive claims. Conformist transmission's cubic adoption rule $p' = p + D\,p(1-p)(2p-1)$ is the frequency-dependent nonlinearity that *separates genuine social influence from linear averaging*; its bistable structure (stable fixity at $p=0,1$, unstable interior at $p=1/2$) is precisely what a coupling kernel must produce to break the contractivity of pure averaging and generate the metastable belief basins of [[Echo chambers and polarization]] ([[henrich-boyd-1998-conformist-transmission]], [[boyd-richerson-1985-culture-evolutionary-process]]). And the cultural-attraction reduction — that as attractor strength grows, a continuous reconstructive model collapses to a discrete replicator with a few stable states — is the cleanest formal analog in this batch of the [[Belief inertia]] premise that beliefs are reconstructed-under-priors, not transmitted verbatim: strong prior self-coupling $\alpha\,\mathrm{KL}(q_i \,\|\, p_i)$ collapses continuous Gaussian dynamics toward a small set of attractor states, recovering discrete consensus from continuous beliefs ([[henrich-boyd-2002-modeling-cognition-culture]], [[sperber-1996-explaining-culture]]). The collective-brain thesis ([[henrich-2016-secret-of-our-success]]) is framing-level motivation for [[Meta-agents and hierarchical emergence]] and [[Collective active inference]] — anthropological evidence that coupled belief-updating yields capability no single agent holds — rather than machinery the equations use.

## Details

The field's spine is the application of the Darwinian triad to socially transmitted variants, with biased and unbiased transmission playing the roles of selection and drift ([[mesoudi-2011-cultural-evolution]]). The two founding formalisms differ in emphasis. Cavalli-Sforza and Feldman ([[cavalli-sforza-feldman-1981-cultural-transmission]]) foreground the *channel* of transmission: vertical (from parents), horizontal (among peers, yielding logistic contagion-like spread), and oblique (from unrelated elders), showing that the route information takes through a population — not just the trait's content — controls its rate and pattern of diffusion, and that culture equilibrates much more slowly than biological inheritance because each learner draws on many cultural parents. Boyd and Richerson ([[boyd-richerson-1985-culture-evolutionary-process]], [[richerson-boyd-2005-not-by-genes-alone]]) foreground the *bias* in the learner's decision rule, classifying social learning into direct/content, indirect/model-based (prestige), and frequency-dependent (conformist) families, and arguing that these biases are themselves adaptive shortcuts whose population aggregates generate cumulative adaptation, stable between-group differences, and predictable maladaptive runaways.

The conformist case is the sharp one. Sampling $n$ models, an unbiased (DeGroot-like) learner adopts a variant at frequency $p$ with probability $p$, whereas a conformist learner adopts it with probability
$$
p' = p + D\,p(1-p)(2p-1), \qquad D > 0,
$$
which has stable fixed points at $p=0$ and $p=1$ and an unstable interior equilibrium at $p=1/2$ ([[henrich-boyd-1998-conformist-transmission]]). This bistability amplifies small majorities to fixation within groups and, against migration, maintains the persistent inter-group variation that cultural group selection requires. It is the nonlinearity that no purely linear (averaging) model can produce, and the formal signature the polarization phenomenology of the program needs.

The "strategies" turn reframes the learner as a boundedly rational decision-maker selecting among information sources. Laland ([[laland-2004-social-learning-strategies]]) organizes the rules into "when to copy" (copy-when-uncertain, copy-when-asocial-learning-is-costly, copy-when-dissatisfied) and "whom to copy" (copy-the-majority, copy-successful, copy-older/experienced, copy-kin), arguing selectivity beats indiscriminate copying because unconditional imitation propagates stale or maladaptive information. The follow-up review ([[rendell-2011-cognitive-culture]]) audits this against controlled experiments, finding robust support for state-dependent reliance on social information (uncertainty and the cost of asocial learning reliably increase copying), partial support for frequency-dependent conformity, and evidence for payoff and model-based biases. The Social Learning Strategies Tournament ([[rendell-2010-why-copy-others]]) tested the same logic computationally: in a multi-armed-bandit world where agents chose among INNOVATE, OBSERVE, and EXPLOIT, the winning entry copied almost exclusively, succeeding because demonstrators preferentially EXPLOIT their highest-payoff behaviors, so what a copier observes is automatically pre-filtered toward high-value actions — and because *how* one copies (recency-discounted payoff weighting) mattered more than *how much*.

Prestige bias gives the model-based family a concrete mechanism: once humans became reliant on copying, selection favored a psychology that identifies competent models and *freely confers deference* on them in exchange for learning access, producing a self-reinforcing concentration of attention on a few high-skill individuals — an information-goods market distinct from coercive dominance ([[henrich-gilwhite-2001-evolution-of-prestige]]). Finally, the transmission/attraction debate addresses the unit of inheritance. Sperber ([[sperber-1996-explaining-culture]]) argues transmission is never faithful copying but transformation toward cognitive attractors, so a "cultural type" is a cluster of similar reconstructions held in place by perceptual, memory, and inferential biases. Henrich and Boyd ([[henrich-boyd-2002-modeling-cognition-culture]]) reconcile this with the selectionist account by an explicit limit: representing a variant as a continuous value inferred from a noisy observation plus the pull of an attractor, they show that as attractor strength grows the continuous-representation model *reduces to a discrete replicator* in which a few attractor states behave like faithfully copied discrete variants — so cumulative culture survives arbitrarily lossy individual copying, and discrete "memes" are an emergent description of a continuous, reconstructive process. Across all of these mechanisms the through-line for the program is that population-level cultural inheritance is the macroscopic face of coupled, biased, precision-sensitive belief updating — the subject of the [[Statistical physics of social systems and collective behavior]] theme to which this concept belongs.
