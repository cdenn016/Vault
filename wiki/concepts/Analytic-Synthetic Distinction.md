# Analytic-Synthetic Distinction

The analytic-synthetic distinction divides statements into the analytic — true by virtue of meaning alone (e.g. 'all bachelors are unmarried') — and the synthetic — true by virtue of how the world is. Quine's 'Two Dogmas of Empiricism' (1951) attacks this distinction as one of the two dogmas of empiricism, arguing that no non-circular account of analyticity (synonymy, definition) can be given and that, instead, all statements face the tribunal of experience collectively as a single 'web of belief'. This holistic, no-sharp-boundary picture connects to the program's view of belief systems as graded, interdependent, and revisable under evidence rather than partitioned into the empirically immune and the empirically testable.
