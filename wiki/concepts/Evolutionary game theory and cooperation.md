# Evolutionary game theory and cooperation

## Definition

**Evolutionary game theory** (EGT) is the study of strategic interaction in populations where success is measured not by an agent's rational deliberation but by reproductive or imitative fitness: high-payoff strategies spread, low-payoff strategies die out, and the analyst asks which strategy configurations are stable under this Darwinian dynamic. The field's founding move, systematized in Maynard Smith's monograph ([[maynard-smith-1982-evolution-theory-games]]), replaces the rational optimizer of classical game theory with selection acting on heritable strategies, and replaces the Nash equilibrium with the **evolutionarily stable strategy** (ESS): a strategy that, once common, cannot be invaded by any rare mutant. Formally $s^*$ is an ESS if for every alternative $s$ either $E(s^*,s^*) > E(s,s^*)$, or $E(s^*,s^*) = E(s,s^*)$ together with $E(s^*,s) > E(s,s)$ — a Nash condition plus an uninvadability refinement. The population-level dynamics underlying ESS is the replicator equation $\dot x_i = x_i[(Ax)_i - \phi]$, with $\phi$ the mean fitness; the textbook synthesis of selection, mutation, the replicator and quasispecies equations, and the finite-population Moran process is Nowak's *Evolutionary Dynamics* ([[nowak-2006-evolutionary-dynamics-book]]).

The most studied problem in EGT is the **evolution of cooperation**: how can a behavior that is costly to the individual but beneficial to others survive selection, when in a well-mixed population defectors always out-earn cooperators? The canonical answer for repeated interaction is reciprocity. Axelrod and Hamilton ([[axelrod-hamilton-1981-evolution-of-cooperation]]) showed in iterated-prisoner's-dilemma tournaments that the simple reciprocal rule tit-for-tat — cooperate first, then mirror the opponent — wins and is collectively stable once the "shadow of the future" (the continuation probability $w$) is large enough. Nowak's review ([[nowak-2006-five-rules-cooperation]]) organizes the whole literature into five mechanisms, each a single cost-benefit threshold: kin selection ($r > c/b$), direct reciprocity ($w > c/b$), indirect reciprocity via reputation ($q > c/b$, [[nowak-sigmund-2005-indirect-reciprocity]]), network/spatial reciprocity ($b/c > k$), and group (multilevel) selection. Each introduces an assortment that lets benefits flow preferentially to fellow cooperators.

A large branch of the field studies these games on **structured populations** rather than well-mixed ones. Nowak and May ([[nowak-may-1992-spatial-chaos]]) placed pure cooperators and defectors on a lattice with local play and local imitation and found that spatial structure alone — with no memory, reciprocity, or strategy — sustains cooperation through cluster formation. Lieberman, Hauert and Nowak ([[lieberman-hauert-nowak-2005-evolutionary-dynamics-on-graphs]]) generalized the Moran process to arbitrary weighted directed graphs, proving the isothermal theorem (a graph reproduces the well-mixed fixation probability iff incoming and outgoing edge weights balance at every vertex) and exhibiting graphs that amplify or suppress selection. Ohtsuki and colleagues ([[ohtsuki-hauert-lieberman-nowak-2006-simple-rule-graphs]]) derived the compact condition $b/c > k$ for cooperation on a degree-$k$ graph, an analytic bridge between kin selection and graph theory. Santos and Pacheco ([[santos-pacheco-2005-scale-free-cooperation]]) showed that degree heterogeneity — the scale-free distributions of real social networks — is by itself enough to make cooperation dominant across the parameter range of both the prisoner's dilemma and the snowdrift game. The physics-facing reviews tie all of this to statistical mechanics: Szabó and Fáth ([[szabo-fath-2007-evolutionary-games-on-graphs]]) map evolutionary games on graphs onto non-equilibrium spin systems, and Perc and colleagues ([[perc-jordan-rand-wang-boccaletti-szolnoki-2017-statistical-physics-cooperation]]) treat the cooperator fraction as an order parameter undergoing phase transitions as game and structural parameters vary.

## Why it matters here

This concept is the cooperation-side counterpart to the consensus and opinion phenomena the SocialPhysics program models, and its relevance is real but should be stated with care. EGT is **adjacent intellectual-neighborhood context, not machinery the belief-inertia VFE functional actually uses**. None of these works carry information geometry, a Fisher/precision tensor, natural-gradient flow, gauge transport $\Omega_{ij} = \exp(\phi_i)\exp(-\phi_j)$, or variational free energy; their dynamics are discrete strategy updates (imitation, death-birth, Moran replacement), not first- or second-order descent on a statistical manifold. The current theorem-first status in [[belief-inertia-2026-07-12-theorem-first-revision]] proves only fixed symmetric DeGroot under the primary metric, nonuniform reversible DeGroot under an additional $\rho$-weighted metric, and a restricted anchored Friedkin--Johnsen equilibrium. [[Bounded confidence]] is only a soft analog. EGT cooperation, fixation, and cascade phenomena are benchmarks and structural analogies, not derived limits of [[Multi-agent variational free energy]].

Within that honest scope the parallels are genuine and worth recording. The strongest is **structure-to-outcome**: EGT's central empirical lesson is that the topology of the interaction graph decides collective outcomes — Nowak–May's lattice clusters, Lieberman–Hauert–Nowak's amplifiers and suppressors of selection, Ohtsuki's $b/c > k$, and Santos–Pacheco's hub-driven cooperation are all maps from coupling topology to whether a trait spreads or fixates. This is exactly the question the program asks of its agent-coupling graph and its softmax attention weights $\beta_{ij}$: which coupling structures drive belief-carrying agents toward shared (consensual) configurations and which leave persistent disagreement and [[Echo chambers and polarization|fragmentation]]. The $b/c > k$ rule and the isothermal/amplifier dichotomy are the kind of closed-form structural template the program would like to derive in its own VFE setting, not results it inherits. A second parallel is **multi-scale**: Nowak's group (multilevel) selection prefigures the program's meta-agent coarse-graining, in which tightly coupled clusters of agents become higher-scale agents ([[Community detection and modularity]]). A third, looser one is reputation: indirect reciprocity introduces belief-about-others (image, reputation) as the state driving behavior ([[nowak-sigmund-2005-indirect-reciprocity]]), conceptually adjacent to the program's representation of agents by belief distributions and to [[Collective active inference]], though reputation is a scalar score updated by observation rather than a Gaussian tuple $(\mu,\Sigma,\phi)$ under gauge-covariant VFE.

The cleanest interface is methodological. Szabó–Fáth and especially Perc et al. recast cooperation as emergent critical phenomena — order parameters, phase diagrams, universality classes, an update-noise "temperature" — which is precisely the [[Sociophysics]] stance the program adopts for belief dynamics, and the temperature/order-parameter framing resonates with the program's [[Meta-entropy]] and thermodynamic-limit arguments. The juxtaposition in Nowak's textbook of deterministic infinite-population (replicator, [[Replicator dynamics]]) and stochastic finite-population (Moran) regimes is conceptually relevant to the same thermodynamic-limit interest. These are shared questions and a shared vocabulary, not a shared functional.

## Details

The substantive content divides into the equilibrium concepts, the cooperation thresholds, and the network results. On equilibria, the ESS criterion of [[maynard-smith-1982-evolution-theory-games]] is the dynamically meaningful refinement of Nash that the field is built on, and it sits atop the replicator dynamics that [[nowak-2006-evolutionary-dynamics-book]] consolidates alongside the Moran fixation probability $\rho = (1-1/r)/(1-1/r^N)$ for a mutant of relative fitness $r$ in a population of size $N$. This juxtaposition of deterministic and stochastic regimes within one framework is the book's signature, and it makes explicit when mean-field replicator reasoning applies and when finite-size fluctuations dominate.

On cooperation thresholds, the iterated prisoner's dilemma of [[axelrod-hamilton-1981-evolution-of-cooperation]] establishes that reciprocity stabilizes cooperation when the discounted stream of future mutual reward outweighs the one-shot temptation, roughly $w \geq (T-R)/(T-P)$ for the payoff ordering $T > R > P > S$. The five-rules synthesis ([[nowak-2006-five-rules-cooperation]]) shows that every known mechanism reduces to a single comparison of benefit to cost scaled by a structural parameter: relatedness $r$ for kin selection, continuation $w$ for direct reciprocity, reputation-knowledge probability $q$ for indirect reciprocity, graph degree $k$ for network reciprocity, and a group-size term for multilevel selection. Indirect reciprocity ([[nowak-sigmund-2005-indirect-reciprocity]]) is the reputation-based member, with condition $q > c/b$ and the additional finding that naive image scoring is exploitable, so that stability against defectors requires higher-order assessment (the "leading eight" norms that judge an action in light of the recipient's own standing).

On networks, the progression is from demonstration to theorem to closed form. [[nowak-may-1992-spatial-chaos]] is the existence proof that population structure alone sustains cooperation: on a lattice with deterministic local imitation, cooperator clusters whose interiors harvest mutual-cooperation payoffs persist against boundary exploitation for a range of the temptation parameter $b$, producing fluctuating but statistically stationary coexistence rather than the all-defect fixation of the mean-field case. [[lieberman-hauert-nowak-2005-evolutionary-dynamics-on-graphs]] supplies the general theory of fixation on weighted directed graphs, with the isothermal theorem identifying exactly which graphs preserve the well-mixed $\rho$ and the amplifier/suppressor classification (star and superstar graphs driving fixation of any advantageous mutant toward one). [[ohtsuki-hauert-lieberman-nowak-2006-simple-rule-graphs]] reduces network reciprocity to the inequality $b/c > k$ (valid for $k \ll N$ under death-birth updating), so that sparser, more locally clustered graphs favor cooperation and dense graphs recover the no-cooperation mean-field limit — a Hamilton-rule analogue in which degree plays the role of inverse relatedness. [[santos-pacheco-2005-scale-free-cooperation]] then shows that the heterogeneity of real networks overrides the game: on Barabási–Albert scale-free graphs, cooperative hubs accumulate large payoffs and seed robust cooperative clusters, making cooperation dominant even in the snowdrift game that erodes it on regular structures. The two physics reviews close the loop to statistical mechanics — [[szabo-fath-2007-evolutionary-games-on-graphs]] mapping two- and multi-strategy games (prisoner's dilemma, snowdrift, stag hunt, rock-paper-scissors cyclic dominance) on lattices and complex networks onto non-equilibrium spin and reaction-diffusion systems with update noise as temperature, and [[perc-jordan-rand-wang-boccaletti-szolnoki-2017-statistical-physics-cooperation]] surveying spatial reciprocity, coevolution of strategy and structure, reward and punishment, and public-goods games through phase diagrams and critical-phenomena language, treating $\langle\rho_C\rangle$ as an order parameter with transitions in Ising and directed-percolation universality classes. Together these supply the order-parameter / phase-transition apparatus that the broader theme of the [[Statistical physics of social systems and collective behavior]] shares with the belief-dynamics program, even though the microscopic law (strategy updates) differs from VFE gradient flow.
