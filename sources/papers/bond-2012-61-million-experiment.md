# A 61-million-person experiment in social influence and political mobilization

> [!info] Citation
> Bond, R. M., Fariss, C. J., Jones, J. J., Kramer, A. D. I., Marlow, C., Settle, J. E., & Fowler, J. H. (2012). *A 61-million-person experiment in social influence and political mobilization*. Nature, 489(7415), 295-298. doi:10.1038/nature11421.

## TL;DR
On the day of the 2010 U.S. congressional elections, Facebook users were randomly assigned to see an "I Voted" social message displaying the faces of friends who had voted, an informational-only message, or no message. The social message measurably increased real-world voting (validated against public voter records) and self-expressed voting, and the indirect effect propagating through *close-friend* ties to people who never saw any message exceeded the direct effect on those who did. The study is the largest field experiment in social influence and the clearest demonstration that political behavior transmits through specific social ties, not merely through exposure to messaging.

## What it establishes
The design is a population-scale randomized controlled trial with behavioral ground truth from validated voting records, which removes the homophily-versus-influence confound that plagues observational network studies. The direct mobilization effect of the social message was small per user but, aggregated, produced hundreds of thousands of additional votes; the indirect peer-to-peer effect — friends of treated users voting more even without treatment — was concentrated almost entirely in *close* friendships and, summed over the network, was larger than the direct effect. Tie strength, not mere connection, governed transmission: weak ties showed negligible behavioral spillover.

## Relevance to this research
This is real-world evidence that influence flows along specific, strong edges and that network-mediated transmission can dominate direct persuasion — supporting the model's premise that collective belief change is governed by inter-agent coupling structure rather than by independent priors acted on in isolation. The finding that close (strong) ties carry the behavioral effect is consistent with treating high-weight attention edges $\beta_{ij}$ as the channels of belief propagation. The relevance is adjacent empirical context: it validates the coupling premise but does not bear on the belief-inertia machinery or the gauge structure specifically.
