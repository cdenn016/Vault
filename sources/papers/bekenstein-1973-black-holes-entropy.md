# Black Holes and Entropy

> [!info] Citation
> Jacob D. Bekenstein (1973). "Black holes and entropy." *Physical Review D* 7(8): 2333–2346. DOI: [10.1103/PhysRevD.7.2333](https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevD.7.2333).

## TL;DR

Bekenstein proposes that a black hole carries an entropy proportional to the area of its event horizon, $S \propto A$ (later fixed to $S = A/4$ in Planck units by Hawking's temperature calculation). This was the first claim that an *information* quantity (entropy) is encoded in a purely *geometric* quantity (horizon area), and the origin of the holographic intuition that the information content of a region is bounded by its boundary area rather than its volume.

## Problem & setting

Without horizon entropy, dropping matter into a black hole would appear to violate the second law of thermodynamics by hiding the matter's entropy. Bekenstein restores the second law by attributing entropy to the hole itself, monotonically increasing with horizon area.

## Method

Information-theoretic and thermodynamic arguments — a generalized second law in which the sum of ordinary entropy outside the hole plus horizon-area entropy never decreases — together with dimensional analysis fixing the proportionality up to a constant.

## Key results

The area–entropy law $S = A/4$ (in $G = c = \hbar = k_B = 1$ units) and the generalized second law. This is the bedrock identity behind the Ryu–Takayanagi formula and every entropy-bound formulation of the holographic principle.
