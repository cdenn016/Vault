# A Relativistic Theory of Consciousness

> [!info] Citation
> Lahav, N. & Neemeh, Z. A. (2025). "A Relativistic Theory of Consciousness (shortened version)." Originally published in *Frontiers of Psychology* (May 2022).

## TL;DR
Lahav and Neemeh propose that phenomenal consciousness is a relativistic phenomenon analogous to velocity in special relativity: a cognitive system either has or lacks phenomenal consciousness *with respect to some observer*, not absolutely. This dissolves the hard problem by showing that the apparent privacy of qualia follows from observer-dependence rather than from any immaterial or illusory nature. Zombies are ruled out by the equivalence principle applied to cognitive frames of reference: any two cognitive systems that agree on all measurements are physically equivalent and must share the same phenomenal status.

## Problem & setting
The hard problem of consciousness (Chalmers 1996) arises from an explanatory gap between third-person, functional descriptions of neural computation and the first-person phenomenal character of experience (qualia, eidetic structures). Both illusionism (qualia are cognitive illusions) and naturalistic dualism (qualia are irreducible non-physical primitives) tacitly assume phenomenal consciousness is an *absolute* property — an object either has it or not, full stop. This shared absolutist assumption generates the paradox of phenomenal judgment: a functional zombie can produce all the same phenomenal judgments as a conscious agent, yet purportedly lacks any phenomenal experience, making the relationship between functional and phenomenal consciousness deeply puzzling.

## Method
The paper introduces two conceptual moves drawn from relativistic physics. First, it replaces the absolutist assumption with the **relativistic principle**: for many properties, object O has property P or ¬P only *with respect to some observer X*, so there is no observer-independent fact of the matter. Second, it applies the **equivalence principle** (analogous to Einstein's equivalence between uniformly accelerated frames and gravitational fields): if a conscious human (Alice) and a sophisticated AI zombie (ALICE) agree on every possible measurement — behavioral reports, phenomenal judgments, internal cognitive states — then the relativistic principle entails they are governed by identical physical laws, hence are physically equivalent. Because phenomenal consciousness is (broad) physical, this equivalence transfers it from Alice to ALICE, eliminating zombies.

The **cognitive frame of reference** is defined by the dynamics of the cognitive system: the first-person frame is the position *within* the system's own dynamics, the only vantage from which phenomenal judgment-representations have direct causal power; the third-person frame is any external observer position, where those same representations appear only as neural firing patterns. The full paper (Lahav and Neemeh 2022, *Frontiers of Psychology*) develops a mathematical formalization including transformation equations (analogous to Lorentz transformations) mapping phenomenal structures between cognitive frames of reference.

## Key results
The main theoretical results are as follows. (1) Zombies are physically impossible in a relativistic universe: any cognitive system capable of producing phenomenal judgment-representations must have phenomenal consciousness in its own first-person frame of reference. (2) There is a formal equivalence (isomorphism) between functional consciousness (the capacity to generate phenomenal judgments) and phenomenal consciousness (qualia and eidetic structures). (3) Qualia are not genuinely private; they only *appear* private because accessing them requires a non-trivial transformation into the appropriate cognitive frame of reference. (4) Both illusionism (privileging the third-person frame as absolute) and naturalistic dualism (treating qualia as fundamentally private and nonphysical) are shown to be errors stemming from the same false absolutist premise.

## Relevance to this research
This paper is directly relevant to the **participatory realism** and **observer-relative ontology** themes in the VFE / active inference research program. Several specific connections arise. The relativistic equivalence principle for cognitive frames of reference resonates with the gauge-theoretic framing in the GL(K) attention work, where gauge freedom corresponds precisely to the impossibility of privileging any absolute reference frame for belief representations. The first-person / third-person cognitive frame distinction maps onto the internal vs. external observer split central to active inference and predictive coding: the free-energy principle describes how a system maintains a boundary (Markov blanket) that distinguishes self-measurements from external measurements, which is structurally parallel to the first-person / third-person cognitive frame distinction here.
