# Evolutionary Games on Graphs

> [!info] Citation
> Szabó, G., & Fáth, G. (2007). *Evolutionary Games on Graphs*. Physics Reports 446(4–6):97–216. DOI: [10.1016/j.physrep.2007.04.004](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.physrep.2007.04.004).

## TL;DR
This tutorial-length Physics Reports review presents evolutionary game theory on structured populations for a physics audience, framing it throughout as a branch of non-equilibrium statistical mechanics. It systematically catalogues two-strategy and multi-strategy games (prisoner's dilemma, snowdrift/hawk-dove, stag hunt, rock-paper-scissors and other cyclic dominance games), the spatial structures they are played on (lattices, small-world, scale-free, and other networks), and the microscopic update rules (imitation, Fermi/Glauber, best response, death-birth). It then analyzes the resulting collective phenomena — ordering dynamics, domain growth, phase transitions, and the spatiotemporal patterns of competing associations — using the conceptual toolkit of statistical physics.

## What it establishes
The review's contribution is a unifying mapping: evolutionary game dynamics on graphs are placed in correspondence with non-equilibrium spin systems and reaction-diffusion processes, so that update noise plays the role of temperature, the cooperator density is an order parameter, and transitions between cooperative and defective phases fall into known universality classes (Ising, directed percolation) for appropriate dynamics. Cyclic-dominance games (rock-paper-scissors) are shown to produce self-organizing spirals and traveling waves, while pairwise games on networks exhibit clustering-driven coexistence. It is the standard physicist's reference connecting strategy dynamics to statistical mechanics.

## Relevance to this research
Written for physicists, it maps evolutionary game dynamics on structured populations onto non-equilibrium statistical mechanics — exactly the methodological stance of the SocialPhysics program. It is a strong reference because it supplies the statistical-physics toolkit (spatial games, update rules as effective spin dynamics, ordering and coarsening, phase transitions) that the program reuses for collective belief dynamics on graphs, including the temperature/noise–order-parameter framing that resonates with the program's meta-entropy and thermodynamic-limit treatment of consensus. The microscopic models are strategy-update spin systems rather than VFE flows, so it furnishes methodology and intuition rather than the program's specific functional.
