# The Theory of Relativity and A Priori Knowledge

> [!info] Citation
> Reichenbach, Hans (1920). *Relativitätstheorie und Erkenntnis Apriori*. Berlin: Springer. English translation: *The Theory of Relativity and A Priori Knowledge*, trans. and ed. Maria Reichenbach (Berkeley: University of California Press, 1965).

## TL;DR

Reichenbach's early monograph splits Kant's notion of the *a priori* into two senses — "necessarily true for all time" and "constitutive of the object of knowledge" — and argues that general relativity refutes the first while vindicating the second. The constitutive principles that make a domain of experience objective (its coordinating principles, e.g. the geometry presupposed to give physical content to measurements) are *a priori* yet *relative to a framework* and revisable as science advances. This is the origin of the *relativized a priori*, later revived by Michael Friedman.

## Problem & setting

Kant held the Euclidean geometry of space and the principles of Newtonian physics to be synthetic a priori — necessary preconditions of any possible experience. General relativity, by adopting non-Euclidean geometry coupled dynamically to matter, appears to refute this. Reichenbach's problem is to salvage what is right in Kant: that experience requires constitutive framework principles to become objective at all, even if those principles are no longer fixed and eternal.

## Method

Philosophical analysis of the structure of physical theories. Reichenbach distinguishes *axioms of connection* (empirical laws) from *axioms of coordination* (the constitutive principles that coordinate mathematical concepts to physical reality and thereby first constitute the object of knowledge). The latter are the genuine a priori — presupposed, framework-constituting — but they are theory-relative and can be replaced (Euclidean geometry by the variable geometry of GR). The "necessary and eternal" reading of the a priori is jettisoned; the "constitutive" reading is relativized.

## Key results

- **Two senses of a priori.** Constitutive-of-the-object vs. necessarily-true-forever; only the former survives relativity.
- **Relativized a priori.** Constitutive principles are framework-relative and revisable — a priori relative to a theory, not absolutely. See [[Relativized a priori]].
- **Coordinating principles.** Objectivity requires principles that coordinate formalism to experience; these are presupposed, not derived from data.

## Relevance to this research

This is the historical root of the [[Relativized a priori]] thread that the manuscript reaches via the cited [[friedman-2001-dynamics-of-reason]]. For the project, Reichenbach's relativized constitutive principles are the philosophical model for treating a theoretical framework as a *convention/gauge choice*: the constitutive principles that make experience objective within a paradigm are exactly the kind of framework-relative commitments that shift in a "Kuhnian revolution as collective gauge transformation" ([[kuhn-1962-structure]]). They are presupposed to give content to observation yet are not absolute, mirroring how the manuscript's agents adopt local frames that fix the meaning of their beliefs without any frame being privileged — a [[Gauge transformation]] relates them. Reichenbach thus connects the Kantian foundation ([[kant-1781-critique-pure-reason]]) of [[participatory-it-from-bit]] to its account of framework change, sitting alongside [[Cassirer 1910|cassirer-1910-substance-function]]'s constitutive-but-revisable a priori.
