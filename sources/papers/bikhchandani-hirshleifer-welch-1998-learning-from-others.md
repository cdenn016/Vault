# Learning from the Behavior of Others: Conformity, Fads, and Informational Cascades

> [!info] Citation
> Bikhchandani, S., Hirshleifer, D., & Welch, I. (1998). *Learning from the Behavior of Others: Conformity, Fads, and Informational Cascades*. Journal of Economic Perspectives, 12(3), 151-170. DOI: [10.1257/jep.12.3.151](https://doi.org/10.1257/jep.12.3.151).

## TL;DR
This is the authors' accessible, integrative review of the observational-learning and informational-cascade literature that their 1992 paper launched. Written for a broad economics audience, it lays out the basic cascade mechanism in plain terms, distinguishes it carefully from neighboring explanations of conformity, and surveys the empirical and applied reach of the idea across economics, finance, politics, fashion, and even crime. The central conceptual service is taxonomic: it separates *informational* conformity (copying others because their actions reveal information) from *payoff-externality* conformity (copying because doing what others do is directly rewarding, as in network effects or sanctions), and from preference-based herding. It also emphasizes the defining property that follows from the informational account — that cascades are **fragile** and idiosyncratic, because they rest on a thin base of revealed information and can be reversed by a small amount of new public evidence.

## What it establishes
The review crystallizes a taxonomy of mechanisms that all produce look-alike conformity but differ in their welfare and dynamics: (i) **informational cascades**, where rational agents discard private signals once the public history is decisive; (ii) **reputational/payoff** herding, where agents conform to avoid sanctions or because synchronization is itself valuable; and (iii) **direct conformity preferences**. It stresses that only the informational channel yields the signature *fragility and inefficiency* — herds built on little hard information that can flip abruptly — and it catalogs evidence and applications (financial bubbles, political bandwagons, technology adoption, fads) where the cascade reading is apt. The piece functions as the field's canonical map and entry point, clarifying what cascade theory does and does not claim.

## Relevance to this research
This is the authoritative entry-point review for the whole cascade literature and a strong review-tier reference for situating the program's belief-coupling claims against the canonical taxonomy of conformity mechanisms. Its distinction between *informational* conformity and *payoff-externality* conformity is directly useful for the program: the VFE coupling term $\sum_j\beta_{ij}\,\mathrm{KL}(q_i\|\Omega_{ij}[q_j])$ is fundamentally an *informational* alignment force (an agent moves its belief toward what neighbors' beliefs imply), so the program's recoverable herding belongs squarely in the informational-cascade category rather than the payoff-externality one — a distinction worth stating explicitly when positioning the work. The review's emphasis on fragility also gives the program a falsifiable target: any cascade regime it claims to recover should display the same easily-reversed, low-information-base character. Its value is as a synthesizing map and taxonomy rather than a specific mechanism the functional uses.
