# A New Product Growth for Model Consumer Durables

> [!info] Citation
> Frank M. Bass (1969). “A New Product Growth for Model Consumer Durables.” *Management Science* 15(5), 215–227. DOI: [10.1287/mnsc.15.5.215](https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.15.5.215).

## TL;DR

Bass develops and tests a population-level diffusion model for first purchases of consumer durables. Adoption combines an innovation term with an imitation term proportional to prior adoption, yielding a definite differential equation and a corresponding sales trajectory. The paper establishes why a plotted S-curve is not, by itself, evidence for a Bass process: the formal diffusion law and its parameterized hazard must be specified and tested.

## Problem & setting

The paper seeks a quantitative model for the timing of initial purchases of new durable products. It assumes that a consumer's purchase propensity depends both on an external or innovative component and on the number of previous buyers, which represents imitation. The model is evaluated using historical data for eleven consumer durables and is used for a long-range color-television forecast.

## Method

Let $N(t)$ denote cumulative adopters and $m$ the eventual market size. The Bass model specifies the population diffusion equation
$$
\frac{dN}{dt}=\left(p+q\frac{N}{m}\right)(m-N),
$$
where $p$ is the innovation coefficient and $q$ is the imitation coefficient. Equivalently, for the adoption fraction $F=N/m$, the conditional adoption rate is $f(t)/(1-F(t))=p+qF(t)$. The parameters are fitted to observed product-adoption histories and used to predict the magnitude and timing of peak sales.

## Key results

Applied to the eleven durable-goods series, the model gives useful predictions of peak sales and the timing of the peak, and the paper develops a long-range forecast for color television. The result is a specific mechanistic diffusion curve generated by $p$, $q$, and market saturation. Many unrelated cumulative processes can look sigmoidal, so visual resemblance alone does not identify this equation.

## Relevance to this research

Bass is the required comparator whenever the belief-inertia manuscript discusses adoption, diffusion, or S-shaped population trajectories. A formal population equation must precede a Bass-style claim, and its innovation, imitation, and saturation terms must be distinguished from the individual-level dynamics of [[Belief inertia]]. Gauge-transported VFE or second-order belief motion does not automatically aggregate to the Bass equation.
