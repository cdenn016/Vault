# The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory?

> [!info] Citation
> Friston, Karl (2010). "The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory?" *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* 11(2), 127–138. https://doi.org/10.1038/nrn2787

## TL;DR

Friston proposes that any self-organizing system that resists dissipation into disorder must minimize a single quantity — variational free energy, an information-theoretic upper bound on "surprise" (the negative log-evidence of sensory data). Because free energy bounds surprise, minimizing it implicitly maximizes the evidence for the system's internal generative model of its world. The paper argues this one imperative subsumes perception, learning, attention, and action under a common variational-Bayesian objective: perception minimizes free energy by updating beliefs, learning minimizes it by updating parameters, and action ("active inference") minimizes it by changing sensory input to match predictions. It is the canonical, unifying statement of the free-energy principle.

## Problem & setting

Biological agents must maintain themselves within a bounded set of states despite a fluctuating environment. Formally, an agent has sensory states `s` generated by hidden environmental causes, and an internal generative model `p(s, ψ)` over sensations and their causes. The quantity it would like to minimize is surprise `−log p(s)`, the negative log-evidence of its sensations under the model. But surprise is intractable because it requires marginalizing over all hidden causes.

Friston's move is to introduce a recognition density `q(ψ)` — an approximate posterior over causes — and replace surprise with variational free energy, which is an upper bound on surprise. Free energy can be written as the expected negative log-joint minus the entropy of `q`, or equivalently as surprise plus the Kullback–Leibler divergence between `q` and the true posterior. Minimizing free energy with respect to `q` therefore (i) tightens the bound by driving `q` toward the true posterior, and (ii) lowers surprise itself. This is exactly the variational-Bayes decomposition that underlies the [[Evidence lower bound (ELBO)]]: free energy is the negative ELBO.

## Method

The paper is a synthesis rather than a single experiment, organizing several literatures around the free-energy minimum.

Perception as inference: holding the generative model fixed, the brain adjusts the recognition density `q(ψ)` (encoded by neuronal activity representing the sufficient statistics of beliefs) to minimize free energy. Under a Gaussian (Laplace) assumption, this reduces to minimizing precision-weighted prediction errors — connecting directly to hierarchical predictive coding.

Precision and attention: the inverse variances (precisions) that weight prediction errors are themselves optimized; modulating precision is cast as the computational substrate of attention, biasing inference toward reliable signals.

Learning and the M-step: slow minimization of free energy with respect to model parameters (synaptic efficacies) updates the generative model — a variational expectation–maximization picture where fast belief updates are the E-step and slow parameter updates are the M-step.

Active inference: uniquely, free energy is also minimized by acting on the world — the agent selects actions that make sensations conform to predictions, so motor control becomes the fulfillment of proprioceptive predictions rather than a separate optimal-control problem.

## Key results

A single objective — variational free energy / surprise — is shown to be a common currency for perception, attention, learning, and action, unifying the Bayesian-brain, predictive-coding, efficient-coding, and optimal-control traditions. The Laplace approximation reduces free-energy minimization to hierarchical message passing of precision-weighted prediction errors, giving a concrete neuronal implementation via predictive coding. Precision optimization furnishes a principled, free-energy account of attention as gain control on prediction-error units. Active inference dissolves the separation between perception and action: both minimize the same bound, with action closing the loop by resampling the world.

## Relevance to this research

This paper is the conceptual bedrock of the VFE Transformer Program: the model trains on a variational free-energy / ELBO objective, the very quantity Friston elevates to a first principle. Several specific ingredients map directly onto its claims.

The transformer's loss is the negative ELBO (free energy = surprise + KL). Friston supplies the rationale for why minimizing this bound is simultaneously model-evidence maximization and belief-posterior tightening — see [[Variational free energy]] and [[Evidence lower bound (ELBO)]].

The model carries Gaussian beliefs per token and runs an E-step over beliefs and an M-step over parameters. This is precisely Friston's fast-recognition / slow-learning split, i.e. the variational-EM reading of free-energy minimization (cf. [[neal-1998-variational-em]] and [[bogacz-2017-free-energy-tutorial]]).

The architecture's precision-weighted attention and attention-entropy term operationalize Friston's claim that precision weighting is the computational basis of attention; the `gradient_mode "filtering"` echoes the generalized-filtering view of online belief updating. See [[Precision weighting]] and [[Prediction error]].

Treating next-token negative log-evidence as surprise lets the model's training, inference (E-step), and parameter learning (M-step) all be read as one free-energy descent — the unification Friston argues for.

> [!note] Editorial: Friston frames active inference as minimizing free energy through action on the world; an autoregressive transformer has no actuators, so the program inherits the perception-and-learning half of the principle (belief updating + parameter learning) rather than the active-inference loop. The free-energy objective and precision-weighted inference transfer cleanly; embodied active inference does not.
