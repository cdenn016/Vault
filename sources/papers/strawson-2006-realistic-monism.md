# Realistic Monism: Why Physicalism Entails Panpsychism

> [!info] Citation
> Strawson, G. (2006). "Realistic Monism: Why Physicalism Entails Panpsychism." *Journal of Consciousness Studies*, 13(10–11), pp. 3–31. (Lead target article of the *JCS* special issue, with seventeen commentaries. Reprinted in A. Freeman (Ed.), *Consciousness and Its Place in Nature*, Imprint Academic / OUP, 2006; chapter DOI [10.1093/oso/9780199267422.003.0001](https://doi.org/10.1093/oso/9780199267422.003.0001).)

## TL;DR
Strawson argues that a genuinely realistic form of physicalism — one that does not explain away or deny the reality of conscious experience — is committed to panpsychism. The argument proceeds in two steps: first, any non-eliminativist physicalism must accept that experience is a real physical phenomenon; second, given that physics only reveals abstract structural/relational properties of matter and says nothing about intrinsic nature, the only coherent account of how matter gives rise to experience is that experiential properties are present all the way down, even in ultimates (fundamental physical entities). The paper thus defends panpsychism not as a mystical thesis but as the inevitable consequence of taking both physics and phenomenology seriously.

## Problem & setting
The paper addresses the mind-body problem from within a physicalist framework, targeting what Strawson calls "physicSalism" — the faith that physics can in principle fully capture all of concrete reality. Most self-described physicalists implicitly endorse [NE]: that physical stuff is, in its fundamental nature, wholly and utterly non-experiential. Strawson argues that this commitment is incompatible with a second commitment [RP]: that experience is real, concrete, and therefore physical. The tension is sharpened by invoking the Russell-Eddington point that physics discloses only formal-structural, relational properties of the physical, leaving intrinsic nature entirely open.

## Method
The argument is conceptual and analytic rather than empirical. Strawson first distinguishes realistic physicalism (RP) from eliminativist reductions that covertly deny phenomenal consciousness (following Block's "Armstrong-Block reply"). He then examines emergence as the only apparent way to reconcile RP with NE: could experience be a purely emergent property of wholly non-experiential ultimates? He deploys a series of destructive analogies — liquidity from non-liquid molecules (conceivable, because both sides share the same homogeneous P-phenomena vocabulary) versus extension from genuinely unextended point-entities (inconceivable, he argues, as a metaphysical matter) — to show that experiential-from-non-experiential emergence would be analogous to the impossible unextended-to-extended case: "you can get A from non-A for some substitutions for A, but not all." The spatial-from-non-spatial case is offered as a further test; Strawson contends that in every case where one genuinely cannot get A from non-A, experience provides the paradigm case. The Russell-Eddington structural-knowledge argument then closes the case: since physics leaves intrinsic nature underdetermined, the only intrinsic nature we have direct evidence for is experiential (from first-person insight into one's own neural pointer readings), making panpsychism the most Occamical hypothesis.

## Key results
The central philosophical conclusion is that panpsychism — the view that every concrete thing involves experiential being — is the only coherent form of realistic (non-eliminativist) physicalism. Supporting theses include: (1) eliminativist positions (Dennett, Dretske, Tye, Lycan, Rey) covertly deny experience by "looking-glassing" the word "consciousness," making them not genuinely physicalist; (2) the concept of radical emergence of experience from wholly non-experiential substrates is incoherent for the same reason that extension-from-non-extension is incoherent; (3) physics provides only abstract relational-structural knowledge, leaving intrinsic nature open and making non-experiential intrinsic nature an unmotivated assumption. No empirical findings are reported; the contribution is the logical argument establishing panpsychism as entailed by the conjunction of RP and the Russell-Eddington structural-knowledge thesis.

## Relevance to this research
This paper is relevant primarily through the participatory realism / philosophy of mind thread in the research program. The Russell-Eddington structural-knowledge thesis — that physics captures only abstract relational structure, not intrinsic nature — parallels the information-geometric perspective in the VFE framework, where beliefs and gauge-equivariant attention operate on relational (covariant) quantities rather than intrinsic magnitudes. Strawson's argument that the only known intrinsic physical nature is experiential resonates with Wheeler's "it from bit" and the participatory realist position that observation and experience are constitutive of physical reality rather than derivative from it. His critique of radical emergence, specifically the argument that you cannot get experience from wholly non-experiential substrates via combination, is directly relevant to theories (like predictive coding and active inference) that attempt to ground phenomenal experience in functional or information-processing terms. The paper also provides philosophical context for the PIFB ("Participatory It-From-Bit") manuscript thread, where the question of whether experiential states can be identified with information-geometric structures is addressed.
