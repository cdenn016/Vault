# Testing the Limits of Quantum Mechanical Superpositions

> [!info] Citation
> Arndt, M. and Hornberger, K. (2014). "Testing the Limits of Quantum Mechanical Superpositions." *Nature Physics* 10, 271–277. arXiv:1410.0270.

## TL;DR
This review surveys experimental tests of the quantum superposition principle for massive, complex objects — from neutron interferometry and atom interferometry to macromolecule interference and proposed optomechanical schemes. It argues that whether quantum mechanics extends into the macroscopic domain is an open empirical question, not one to be settled by interpretation, and introduces a quantitative measure of "macroscopicity" to compare disparate experiments on a common footing.

## Problem & setting
Quantum mechanics predicts that the superposition principle is universally valid, yet macroscopic objects never appear in superpositions in everyday experience. Standard decoherence theory (Joos, Zurek) explains the effective classical behavior through environmental entanglement, but does so within the unitary quantum framework — requiring interpretational choices. An alternative possibility, taken seriously by the authors, is that quantum mechanics breaks down beyond some mass or complexity scale, as suggested by the difficulty of reconciling quantum theory with general relativity. The paper reviews where the state of the art stands and what experiments could falsify such modifications.

## Method
The review covers several experimental platforms for mechanical quantum superposition: (1) SQUID flux qubits realizing superpositions of macroscopic circulating currents; (2) neutron interferometry with crystal beam splitters achieving 7 cm arm separation; (3) atom interferometry (Raman beam splitters, Bose-Einstein condensates) with coherence times exceeding 2 seconds; (4) macromolecule interference via KDTLI (Kapitza-Dirac-Talbot-Lau interferometer) and OTIMA (time-domain optical ionization grating) schemes, currently holding the mass record at >10,000 amu; (5) proposals for levitated nanosphere interference and optomechanical micromirror superpositions. A central analytical contribution is the "macroscopicity" measure $\mu = \log_{10}(\tau_e / 1\,\text{s})$, where $\tau_e$ is the coherence-time parameter that characterizes how strongly an experiment falsifies minimal classicalizing modifications of quantum mechanics satisfying Galilean invariance, scale invariance, and consistent treatment of identical particles. This allows comparison across entirely different experimental platforms.

## Key results
The macroscopicity scale places current experiments in the range $\mu \approx 5$–$12$: neutron interferometry (1962) at $\mu \approx 6.8$, SQUID persistent-current superpositions at $\mu \approx 4.8$, Talbot-Lau interference of large molecules (PFNS8, 2011) at $\mu \approx 12.1$. Proposed OTIMA experiments with $10^8$ amu gold nanoparticles could reach $\mu \approx 23.3$, which would falsify all current CSL (continuous spontaneous localization) model predictions. The review establishes that competitive macroscopicities are achievable by radically different physical systems, suggesting no single golden experimental strategy dominates.

## Relevance to this research
This paper is relevant primarily through the participatory realism and quantum-foundations thread of the research program (see [[PIFB.tex]] / [[Participatory It-from-Bit]]). The empirical question of whether the superposition principle extends to macroscopic scales bears directly on interpretations of quantum measurement, objective collapse models (CSL, Penrose-Diosi), and the role of observation in constituting physical reality — all themes central to participatory realism. The macroscopicity measure is also a concrete operationalization of the idea that "macroscopicness" is a matter of empirical degree rather than metaphysical kind, relevant to discussions of the quantum-classical boundary and decoherence in active inference / FEP contexts. The Schrödinger-Newton and CSL collapse models discussed here represent the class of theories the program aims to situate epistemically.
