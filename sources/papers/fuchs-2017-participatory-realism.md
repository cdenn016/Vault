# On Participatory Realism

> [!info] Citation
> Fuchs, Christopher A. (2017). "On Participatory Realism." In I. T. Durham & D. Rickles (Eds.), *Information and Interaction: Eddington, Wheeler, and the Limits of Knowledge* (pp. 113–134). Springer. Preprint: arXiv:1601.04360 [quant-ph] (v3, 28 Jun 2016 revision).

## TL;DR
Fuchs argues that QBism — Quantum Bayesianism in its most radical form — should be understood not as instrumentalism or anti-realism but as a species of "participatory realism": the view that reality is more than any third-person perspective can capture, and that agents (observers) are irreducible, non-negligible participants in the construction of actual events. The paper traces this position through John Wheeler's "law without law" and "observer-participancy," defends it against critics who label it non-realist, and presents the Born Rule as a normative, inter-subjectively binding structure that points beyond any individual agent's probability assignments.

## Problem & setting
The central problem is the persistent mislabelling of first-person-perspectival interpretations of quantum mechanics — Copenhagen, QBism, Zeilinger/Brukner, Rovelli relational, Wheeler observer-participancy — as "non-realist" or "instrumentalist." Fuchs argues this conflation ignores a genuine third option between naive object-realism and instrumentalism. Prior art includes Wheeler's writings on "it from bit," Bohr's "phenomenon," William James's "pure experience," and the extensive QBism programme developed with Rudiger Schack, Carlton Caves, David Mermin, and Marcus Appleby. The occasion for the paper is a correspondence with Adan Cabello whose "Map of Madness" chart initially categorised all these views as "non-realist."

## Method
The paper is primarily conceptual and philosophical, organised as an annotated correspondence. Key technical moves include: (1) invoking Einstein's distinction between "subjective factor" and "objective factor" in the Schilpp volume to show that quantum states, time-evolution maps, and measurement outcomes living in the subjective factor does not imply that the normative Born Rule does — the Born Rule lives in the objective, impersonal factor; (2) presenting the SIC-POVM form of the Born Rule,
$$Q(D_j) = (d+1)\sum_{i=1}^{d^2} P(H_i)\,P(D_j|H_i) - 1,$$
as a normative constraint any agent should strive to satisfy, distinct from particular probability assignments; (3) arguing that Kochen-Specker and Bell theorems, combined with de Finetti subjective probability, force "objective indeterminism" — irreducible randomness that goes further than Zeilinger or Brukner by rejecting the eigenstate-eigenvalue link even at probability 1; (4) proposing "participatory realism" as the unifying label for Wheeler, Zeilinger/Brukner, Rovelli, Bohr, and QBism, with QBism at the logically enforced extreme. Wheeler's 1974 notebook is reproduced as an appendix, identifying "participation" and "undecidable propositions" as the two parents of physics.

## Key results
The main philosophical result is the coinage and defence of the term "participatory realism" as a realist, not anti-realist, stance: reality is more than third-person accounts capture, and agents are constitutive participants in the creation of facts. A key technical corollary is that the Born Rule, expressed as a normative SIC relation, belongs to the "objective factor" in Einstein's sense — it is a rule lying about even when no agents are present to use it — while quantum states themselves do not. The paper establishes that QBism's commitment to subjective Bayesian probability combined with objective indeterminism is a coherent and strongly realist position, not a retreat to instrumentalism. Wheeler's "law without law" is reinterpreted: there are no stochastic laws of nature either; the only "objective" residue is the normative structure (Born Rule, Hilbert space geometry) that any agent must strive to satisfy.

## Relevance to this research
Participatory realism is the philosophical backbone of the PIFB (Participatory It-From-Bit) manuscript, which generalises Wheeler's it-from-bit programme to the active-inference/VFE setting. Several direct connections: (1) The QBist agent's probability assignments updated by the Born Rule are the quantum analogue of the VFE agent's belief distributions updated by variational free energy minimisation — both are normative, first-person prescriptions that nonetheless point to inter-subjective structure. (2) Wheeler's "elementary quantum phenomena" as elementary acts of creation map onto the discrete VFE update steps in the multi-agent active-inference model: each belief update is a local "QBoom." (3) The distinction between the subjective quantum state and the objective normative Born Rule parallels the distinction in the VFE framework between agent-specific posteriors q_i and the shared gauge-equivariant transport structure Omega_ij — the latter is the "objective factor" that agents must agree on. (4) Fuchs's insistence that agents are non-eliminable participators directly motivates the multi-agent FEP model where individual agents' actions reshape the shared generative model, not merely sample from a pre-existing one.
