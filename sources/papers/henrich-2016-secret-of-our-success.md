# The Secret of Our Success: How Culture Is Driving Human Evolution, Domesticating Our Species, and Making Us Smarter

> [!info] Citation
> Henrich, J. (2016). *The Secret of Our Success: How Culture Is Driving Human Evolution, Domesticating Our Species, and Making Us Smarter*. Princeton University Press, Princeton. ISBN 978-0-691-16685-8.

## TL;DR
A synthesis arguing that the distinctive success of *Homo sapiens* rests not on individual raw intelligence but on cumulative culture: a body of know-how, tools, and practices accumulated over generations through high-fidelity social learning that no individual could reinvent in a lifetime. Henrich's central image is the "collective brain" — the proposition that human adaptive capacity is a property of the interconnected population rather than of any single mind, and that the size and connectedness of the social network, together with the fidelity of transmission, set the rate at which culture accumulates and complexifies.

## What it establishes
The book marshals ethnographic, archaeological, and experimental evidence for several linked claims: that humans are obligate cultural learners equipped with attention biases (prestige bias, conformist bias, self-similarity and success bias) that target whom to copy; that cumulative cultural adaptation can produce complex, well-tuned practices whose causal logic individual practitioners do not understand; and that the productivity of the collective brain scales with population size and interconnection, so that demographic collapse or isolation can cause technological *loss*. Gene–culture coevolution then feeds back on biology — culture shaped human anatomy, digestion, and social psychology ("self-domestication"). The argument is verbal and evidential rather than formal; it supplies no equations.

## Relevance to this research
The "collective brain" is precisely a multi-agent system whose population-level intelligence emerges from coupled belief updating, which is the substrate the SocialPhysics program formalizes. It motivates, at the framing level, why coupling beliefs across agents (the inter-agent term of the VFE functional) produces capabilities no single agent holds — the meta-agent / hierarchical-emergence claim of the program rendered as an empirical, anthropological thesis. The dependence of cumulative culture on network size, connectivity, and transmission fidelity is suggestive for how the coupling-graph topology and the precision/attention kernel should govern collective performance. The relevance is strong but framing-level: the book provides motivation and the menu of transmission biases, not machinery the equations import. See [[Cultural evolution and social learning]].
