# What Is It Like to Be a Bat?

> [!info] Citation
> Nagel, Thomas (1974). "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" *The Philosophical Review*, 83(4), 435–450. DOI: [10.2307/2183914](https://doi.org/10.2307/2183914).

## TL;DR

Nagel's classic essay argues that an organism is conscious if and only if there is "something it is like" to be that organism — a subjective, first-person character of experience. He uses the bat, whose echolocatory sensory world is alien to ours, to show that the *what-it-is-like* is essentially tied to a particular point of view and is therefore not capturable by an objective, third-person physical description that abstracts away from any specific perspective. This is the canonical statement of the subjectivity (or "point of view") problem for any reductive theory of mind.

## Problem & setting

The essay targets reductionist physicalism. Nagel grants that mental states may be physical, but insists no extant account of "how" they could be physical takes the *subjective* character of experience seriously. Objective science proceeds by moving away from a species-specific or individual viewpoint toward a description valid from no particular vantage. But conscious experience is constitutively *had from* a point of view; an account that discards the viewpoint discards the very thing to be explained. The bat dramatizes this: we can know everything physical and functional about bat echolocation and still have no grip on what it is like, from the inside, to perceive the world that way.

## Method

Conceptual analysis and thought experiment rather than empirical study. Nagel distinguishes (i) imaginatively projecting *oneself* into a bat's situation, which only yields what it would be like for *me* to behave as a bat, from (ii) grasping what it is like *for the bat*, which is inaccessible because it requires occupying the bat's point of view. He generalizes: facts about experience are accessible only from points of view sufficiently similar to the experiencer's, so they resist the objective, perspective-free idiom of physics. He carefully separates this epistemic/perspectival claim from dualism — he does not argue experience is non-physical, only that we lack the concepts to see how it is physical.

## Key results

The phrase "what it is like to be" becomes the standard criterion for phenomenal consciousness. Nagel establishes that subjectivity is a structural feature — a *point of view* — not merely a residue of incomplete information, and that objective reduction systematically loses it. The essay seeds later formulations of the explanatory gap (see [[levine-1983-explanatory-gap]]) and the hard problem (see [[chalmers-1995-facing-up]]), and it frames the long-running debate over whether a complete physics could ever entail the facts of experience.

## Relevance to this research

This 1974 essay (distinct from the project's separately cited Nagel 1986, *The View from Nowhere*) is the textual source of the manuscript [[participatory-it-from-bit]]'s phrasing about "what it is like to occupy gauge frame $\phi_i$." PIFB's relocation move trades on Nagel's point-of-view criterion directly: where Nagel locates the subjective character in an organism's perspective, the manuscript identifies a perspective with a local frame in the gauge bundle, so that an agent modeled as a section ([[Agents as fibre-bundle sections]]) has a *what-it-is-like* indexed by its frame $\phi_i$. The frame-relativity of registered content then becomes the formal analogue of Nagel's claim that experiential facts are accessible only from a given vantage — a structural cousin of the relativistic, observer-frame picture in [[lahav-neemeh-2022-relativistic-consciousness]] and the participatory ontology of [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]]. The essay also fixes one pole of the project's emerging [[Mathematical consciousness science]] discussion: it states precisely the subjective fact that a structural/relational account ([[tsuchiya-saigo-2016-category-theory-iit]], [[tononi-2016-iit]]) must either capture or be charged with leaving out, which is the threshold PIFB's relocation claims to clear.
