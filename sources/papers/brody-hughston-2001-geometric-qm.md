# Geometric Quantum Mechanics

> [!info] Citation
> Dorje C. Brody and Lane P. Hughston (2001). "Geometric Quantum Mechanics." *Journal of Geometry and Physics* 38(1): 19–53. DOI: [10.1016/S0393-0440(00)00052-8](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0393-0440(00)00052-8).

## TL;DR

Reformulates quantum mechanics as classical Hamiltonian dynamics on the complex projective space of rays, equipped with the Fubini–Study metric. The Schrödinger equation becomes Hamiltonian flow on this Kähler manifold; the symplectic structure gives the dynamics, while the Riemannian (Fubini–Study / [[Fisher information metric|Fisher–Rao]]) part gives uncertainty and distinguishability. Quantum mechanics is thereby presented as a geometric theory whose state space carries both a symplectic and a compatible metric structure.

## Problem & setting

Standard quantum mechanics is written in a linear Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$, yet physical states are rays, not vectors: the genuine state space is the complex projective space $\mathbb{CP}^{n}$ obtained by quotienting out overall phase and normalization. Much of the geometric content of the theory — the distinguishability of states, the speed of evolution, the structure of entanglement — is hidden by the linear formalism. Building on earlier geometric treatments (Kibble, Gibbons, Ashtekar–Schilling, and the authors' own work), Brody and Hughston develop quantum mechanics *intrinsically* on $\mathbb{CP}^{n}$, which is a Kähler manifold carrying simultaneously a symplectic form and a compatible Riemannian metric.

## Method

On $\mathbb{CP}^{n}$ the Hermitian structure splits into a symplectic two-form $\omega$ and the Fubini–Study metric $g$ (a constant multiple of the [[Fisher information metric|quantum Fisher–Rao]] metric). The expectation value $H(\psi)=\langle\psi|\hat H|\psi\rangle$ of the Hamiltonian operator becomes a smooth real *function* on the manifold, and Schrödinger evolution is exactly the Hamiltonian flow generated by $H$ through $\omega$, $\dot{x}^{a}=\omega^{ab}\partial_b H$ — classical Hamiltonian dynamics on a curved phase space. The Riemannian part $g$ measures distinguishability: the variance (uncertainty) of an observable equals the squared metric length of the gradient of its expectation function, so a Heisenberg uncertainty relation becomes a statement about arc length. The authors compute the geodesics and the constant holomorphic sectional curvature of $\mathbb{CP}^{n}$ and extend the construction to mixed states and to the geometry of entanglement.

## Key results

The dynamics (symplectic) and the statistics/distinguishability (metric) of quantum mechanics are unified as the two halves of a single Kähler structure. The dispersion of an observable is the squared Fubini–Study length of its Hamiltonian vector field, giving a purely geometric reading of uncertainty; and the energy dispersion fixes the *speed* at which the state moves through $\mathbb{CP}^{n}$ — the Anandan–Aharonov relation, in which the Fubini–Study distance traversed equals $\Delta E\,t/\hbar$, a geometric time–energy uncertainty and quantum speed limit. The framework reproduces standard quantum predictions while exposing entanglement, measurement, and mixed-state structure as features of the geometry of the state manifold rather than of the linear algebra of operators.
