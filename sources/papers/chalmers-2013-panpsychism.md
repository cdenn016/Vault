# Panpsychism and Panprotopsychism

> [!info] Citation
> Chalmers, David J. (2013). "Panpsychism and Panprotopsychism." *The Amherst Lecture in Philosophy*, 8, 1–35. Available at <https://amherstlecture.org/chalmers2013/>. A revised version appears in G. Brüntrup & L. Jaskolla (Eds.), *Panpsychism: Contemporary Perspectives* (Oxford University Press, 2017).

## TL;DR
Chalmers presents a "Hegelian argument" for panpsychism by synthesizing the conceivability argument against materialism (zombies are conceivable, hence possible, hence phenomenal truths are not grounded in microphysical truths) and the causal argument against dualism (physics is causally closed, so phenomenal properties must be grounded in physical properties). The synthesis — constitutive Russellian panpsychism — resolves the deadlock by identifying microphenomenal properties with the quiddities (intrinsic categorical bases) of microphysical dispositions, so that consciousness plays the very causal roles physics ascribes to fundamental properties. He further distinguishes this view from panprotopsychism, where fundamental entities bear proto-conscious properties that collectively but not individually constitute experience.

## Problem & setting
The hard problem of consciousness creates a dilemma: the conceivability argument (zombies, knowledge argument, explanatory gap) refutes materialism, while the causal-closure argument refutes dualism. Both arguments appear sound, yet they are mutually inconsistent. Chalmers asks whether any positive view can satisfy both simultaneously, building on Russell's observation that physics characterizes structural/relational properties but leaves open their intrinsic categorical bases (quiddities).

## Method
The argument is philosophical and conceptual rather than mathematical. Chalmers distinguishes narrowly physical properties (microphysical role properties, fully characterizable in structural terms) from broadly physical properties (role properties plus their quidditary realizers). He then shows that the conceivability argument establishes at most the possibility of structural zombies — entities that share all relational/structural physics but lack quiddities — without establishing the possibility of categorical zombies (which also share quiddities). The causal-closure argument, recast in terms of broadly physical causal closure, is compatible with phenomenal properties serving as quiddities. Constitutive Russellian panpsychism (type-A version, with a priori entailment from microphenomenal to macrophenomenal truths) therefore evades both horns: it escapes the conceivability horn because categorical zombies are not clearly conceivable, and it escapes the causal horn because microphenomenal quiddities are causally relevant in the way physics requires. The paper also introduces panprotopsychism — fundamental entities bear protophenomenal properties (non-phenomenal individually but collectively constituting phenomenal states via a priori entailment) — as a second candidate synthesis.

## Key results
The central philosophical result is the Hegelian argument: the conjunction of (a) denial of narrow physicalism (from conceivability considerations) and (b) broad physicalism (from causal-closure considerations) is precisely the ground occupied by constitutive Russellian panpsychism. Nonconstitutive and non-Russellian varieties of panpsychism fail because they remain vulnerable to the causal-exclusion horn. Panprotopsychism is distinguished from ordinary type-A materialism by requiring that protophenomenal properties be (i) non-structural and (ii) linked to phenomenal truths via a priori entailment, not merely a posteriori necessitation; this exempts it from the standard epistemic arguments whose target is the structural-phenomenal gap specifically. The paper does not offer a solution to the combination problem (how micro-experiences combine into macro-experience) — that is reserved for a companion article — but frames it as the main residual challenge for panpsychism.

## Relevance to this research
This paper is the primary philosophical grounding for the participatory realism and consciousness-theoretic strand of the research program. The identification of phenomenal properties with quiddities (intrinsic bases of physical structure) resonates directly with the "it from bit" / participatory informational framework being developed in PIFB.tex: if the fundamental ontology is informational or experiential rather than purely structural, then the variational free-energy formalism describing belief updating may have a first-person, experiential interpretation at the level of individual agents or even subsystems. The distinction between constitutive and nonconstitutive panpsychism matters for multi-agent active inference: constitutive Russellian panpsychism offers a route by which individual-agent VFE minimization could, in principle, ground collective or higher-order experiential states (the combination problem is the multi-agent consciousness analogue of the binding problem). The broad/narrow physicalism distinction also parallels the gauge-theoretic distinction between structural (relational, gauge-invariant) and non-structural (gauge-dependent, absolute) quantities in the GL(K) attention framework — an intriguing structural analogy worth tracking even if not a direct mathematical correspondence.
