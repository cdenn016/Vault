# Evolutionary Games and Spatial Chaos

> [!info] Citation
> Nowak, M. A., & May, R. M. (1992). *Evolutionary Games and Spatial Chaos*. Nature 359(6398):826–829. DOI: [10.1038/359826a0](https://doi.org/10.1038/359826a0).

## TL;DR
Nowak and May place prisoner's-dilemma players on a two-dimensional lattice where each cell plays only its immediate neighbors and then copies the strategy of the most successful player in its neighborhood. With no memory, no reciprocity, and no strategic sophistication — players are pure cooperators or pure defectors — cooperation nonetheless survives and persists indefinitely. The deterministic spatial dynamics generate ever-changing, often chaotic, spatiotemporal patterns of cooperator and defector clusters, including symmetric kaleidoscopic growth and "dynamic fractals," demonstrating that spatial structure alone can sustain cooperation that would vanish in a well-mixed population.

## What it establishes
The result is that population structure (network reciprocity) qualitatively changes collective outcomes. Cooperators survive by forming clusters whose interior members harvest mutual-cooperation payoffs faster than boundary defectors can exploit them; whether clusters grow or shrink depends on the temptation parameter $b$ (with $R=1$, $S=P=0$ in the simplest payoff scaling). For a range of $b$ the lattice settles into persistent coexistence with fluctuating but statistically stationary cooperator fraction, rather than the all-defect fixation of the mean-field case. Local interaction plus local imitation, with no further assumptions, is enough.

## Relevance to this research
This is the canonical demonstration that population structure changes collective outcomes, directly parallel to the program treating agents as nodes coupled by gauge-transported KL on a graph. The spatial prisoner's dilemma is a strong neighborhood reference for structure-dependent collective dynamics — the survival of cooperation as a structural rather than strategic effect mirrors how the program expects coupling topology to shape consensus and clustering — though it uses deterministic lattice imitation rules rather than VFE gradient flow, so the link is methodological-conceptual rather than a shared functional.
