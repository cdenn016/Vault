# Two Dogmas of Empiricism

> [!info] Citation
> Quine, W. V. (1951). "Two Dogmas of Empiricism." *The Philosophical Review*, Vol. 60, No. 1, pp. 20–43. http://www.jstor.org/stable/2181906

## TL;DR

Quine attacks two foundational dogmas of logical empiricism: the analytic/synthetic distinction (the idea that some truths are grounded in meanings alone, independently of fact) and reductionism (the idea that each meaningful statement is equivalent to some logical construction over terms referring to immediate experience). He argues that both dogmas are ill-founded, that the attempt to define analyticity through synonymy, definition, or semantical rules is circular, and that the boundary between speculative metaphysics and empirical science dissolves once the dogmas are relinquished. The positive upshot is a thoroughgoing holism — the "web of belief" — and a shift toward pragmatism.

## Problem & setting

Logical empiricism, as represented by Carnap and the Vienna Circle tradition, rested on a sharp cleavage between analytic truths (true by virtue of meaning alone — "No bachelor is married") and synthetic truths (contingently grounded in fact), and on the reductionist program of translating meaningful statements into sense-datum language. Quine challenges both. Prior attempts (Kant, Leibniz, Hume) to ground analyticity were noted as the historical background. The paper engages critically with Carnap's state-descriptions, his notion of explication, and his semantical rules as attempted grounding of the analytic/synthetic distinction.

## Method

Quine proceeds by conceptual analysis and reductio. For analyticity: he traces the chain of attempted definitions — analyticity → synonymy → definition → interchangeability *salva veritate* → semantical rules — showing that each move either presupposes analyticity itself or fails to escape circularity. The interchangeability criterion requires an intensional language presupposing analyticity; semantical rules merely relabel the problem. For reductionism: he invokes Duhem-style holism. Statements face the tribunal of experience not individually but as a corporate body — a "web of belief" in which adjustments upon recalcitrant experience can be made anywhere by revising other beliefs, including those of logic itself. No statement is immune from revision, and none faces experience in isolation.

## Key results

The analytic/synthetic distinction is shown to be a dogma without clear empirical or philosophical content: every attempt to define analyticity either smuggles in the concept itself or replaces it with an equally unexplained term (synonymy, semantical rule). The radical thesis is holism: the unit of empirical significance is the whole of science, not individual statements. As a corollary, the boundary between natural science and speculative metaphysics is blurred — any statement can be held true by revising other parts of the web, and any statement can in principle be revised. The paper ends endorsing pragmatism: choice among theories is guided by pragmatic considerations (simplicity, fecundity, conservatism), not by a principled analytic/synthetic cut.

## Relevance to this research

Quine's holism and rejection of a sharp fact/meaning boundary bear on the philosophical foundations of participatory realism and the observer-dependent physics program that motivates the VFE research. Participatory accounts of quantum measurement (Wheeler's "it from bit") require a nuanced view of how theoretical posits and observational reports are coupled — Quinean holism supports the claim that no observation statement is theory-free and that the "boundary" between observer and observed is a pragmatic posit, not a metaphysical given. The paper is a canonical reference for the philosophy of science background underlying discussions of participatory realism, epistemic frameworks, and the status of mathematical structures in physical theory (relevant to [[Participatory Realism]] and [[PIFB]] manuscript). The rejection of reductionism also resonates with the VFE program's treatment of beliefs as irreducible Gaussian tuples rather than point estimates reducible to data.
