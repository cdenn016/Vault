# An Integrative Theory of Intergroup Conflict

> [!info] Citation
> Henri Tajfel and John C. Turner (1979). *An integrative theory of intergroup conflict*. In W. G. Austin & S. Worchel (Eds.), *The Social Psychology of Intergroup Relations* (pp. 33–47). Brooks/Cole, Monterey, CA.

## TL;DR
This chapter sets out Social Identity Theory, the claim that a substantial part of the self-concept derives from membership in social groups, and that people are motivated to achieve a positive social identity by favourably differentiating their in-group from relevant out-groups. Drawing on the minimal-group experiments — in which even arbitrary, content-free group assignments produce in-group favouritism in reward allocation — Tajfel and Turner argue that mere categorization into "us" and "them" is sufficient to generate intergroup bias, without any conflict of material interests. The theory explains intergroup discrimination, in-group cohesion, and the tendency of group members to accentuate similarities within and differences between groups.

## What it establishes
The mechanism runs through categorization, identification, and comparison: individuals categorize the social world into groups, identify with their own, and seek a positive distinctiveness for it through intergroup comparison. This produces accentuation effects — perceived within-group similarity and between-group difference are exaggerated — and a drive toward in-group favouritism. The result is that influence and alignment are not uniform across a population but are *structured by group boundaries*: agents assimilate to in-group members and differentiate from out-group members, even absent any objective reason to do so.

## Relevance to this research
Social Identity Theory underpins why coupling in the multi-agent model is selective and group-structured rather than uniform. Agents preferentially align with the in-group and differentiate from the out-group, which maps onto block / community structure in the coupling graph $\beta_{ij}$ and onto the formation of echo chambers and emergent meta-agents (coarse-grained clusters) in the Ouroboros tower. The accentuation effect — exaggerated between-group divergence — is the qualitative signature of polarization that a coupling functional with both attractive (in-group) and repulsive or vanishing (out-group) interactions would produce. This is strong context for the community-detection and polarization aspects of the program, though the theory motivates the *structure* of coupling rather than supplying a specific energy term.
