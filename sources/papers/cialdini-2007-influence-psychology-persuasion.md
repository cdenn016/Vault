# Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion

> [!info] Citation
> Robert B. Cialdini (2007). *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (revised edition). Harper Business / Collins (original edition 1984 as *Influence: Science and Practice*).

## TL;DR
Cialdini's popular synthesis distills the empirical persuasion and compliance literature into six broad principles that reliably increase the likelihood of saying yes: reciprocity (returning favours), commitment and consistency (acting in line with prior commitments), social proof (taking others' behavior as a guide to one's own), authority (deferring to credible experts), liking (yielding more to those we like), and scarcity (valuing what is rare or threatened). The book illustrates each principle with field examples and laboratory findings, framing them as cognitive shortcuts that compliance practitioners exploit.

## What it establishes
The contribution is a parsimonious phenomenological taxonomy rather than a formal model. Each principle names a regularity in how social context biases decisions: social proof captures the descriptive-norm pull toward what similar others do; commitment/consistency captures the inertia of prior public positions; authority and liking modulate whose input is weighted heavily. Together they catalogue the *levers* of influence — the situational cues that raise or lower the effective weight an agent places on a social signal.

## Relevance to this research
The book catalogues the empirical levers of social influence — especially "social proof" and "consistency" — that any agent-level influence model should be able to express. Social proof is a direct descriptive-norm analogue of the belief-coupling term (weighting neighbours' positions), and consistency is a phenomenological cousin of belief inertia / prior anchoring. The honest framing: this is a practitioner synthesis, not load-bearing theory; it is useful as a phenomenology *checklist* against which to ask whether the VFE coupling terms can reproduce each lever, rather than as a source of equations or mechanisms the functional actually uses. Authority and liking, for instance, would correspond to heterogeneous, asymmetric coupling weights, which the bare softmax-of-KL attention does not by itself supply.
