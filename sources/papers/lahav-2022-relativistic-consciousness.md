# A Relativistic Theory of Consciousness

> [!info] Citation
> Lahav N and Neemeh ZA (2022). "A Relativistic Theory of Consciousness." Frontiers in Psychology 12:704270. https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2021.704270

## TL;DR
The paper proposes that phenomenal consciousness is a relativistic rather than absolute phenomenon — it is observable from the first-person cognitive frame of reference but not directly from third-person frames, analogously to how velocity is frame-relative in special relativity. By applying an equivalence principle between a conscious human and a functionally identical "zombie" AI system, the authors prove via the principle of relativity that zombies cannot exist and that phenomenal consciousness is identical to phenomenal judgment-representations in the first-person frame. This dissolves the hard problem without positing private, immaterial qualia or an eliminativist illusion.

## Problem & setting
The hard problem of consciousness (Chalmers 1996) asks why functional information processing gives rise to subjective phenomenal experience (qualia), and why those experiences appear private and inaccessible to third-person science. Both naturalistic dualism (qualia as a fundamental, irreducible constituent) and illusionism (qualia as cognitive illusion) assume consciousness is an absolute property — either one has it or does not, independently of the observer. Prior theories such as IIT (Tononi), global workspace theory (Baars/Dehaene), and attention schema theory (Graziano) address the functional aspect but leave the explanatory gap between public functional states and private phenomenal experience unresolved.

## Method
The authors develop a conceptual and mathematical argument structured around two moves drawn from physics:

1. **Equivalence principle of consciousness**: Modelling a cognitive system as a three-tier information processing tuple S = (T, P, C) — sensation T, perception P, and functional consciousness C (the module that generates phenomenal judgment-representations) — they formalise Alice (conscious human) and ALICE (a functionally identical zombie AI) as inhabiting the same cognitive frame of reference because their dynamics and representations agree on all measurements. Applying the relativistic principle (laws of physics must have the same form in all admissible frames of reference that agree on all measurement outcomes), the authors conclude the two systems are physically equivalent. Because phenomenal consciousness is physical (broad physicalism), inference from Alice's frame to ALICE's yields that ALICE must also have phenomenal consciousness — zombies are impossible (Eq. 11–18).

2. **Identification of qualia with phenomenal judgment-representations**: A second, more detailed argument (Eq. 19–35) avoids explicit reliance on broad physicalism. By tracking how Alice's first-person frame uses a quale directly as input to her language module (Eq. 29) while the same linguistic output is also produced by the functional consciousness subsystem (Eq. 23), and combining these via the equivalence transformation (delta function, Eq. 26), the authors derive an identity between the quale and the phenomenal judgment-representation in the first-person frame: $\vec{c}^\mu_{x_t} = \vec{Q}^\mu_{x_t}$ (Eq. 35). This shows qualia are vectors in the state space of the cognitive system — observable from within (first-person frame) but not directly substitutable into another system's subsystems (Eq. 36–41), explaining the apparent privacy without positing immaterial properties.

3. **Third- versus first-person frames**: The reason Bob cannot directly measure Alice's quale is that her phenomenal judgment-representation is a neural state in Alice's state space; when Bob's sensation subsystem encounters it, it measures only the physical coordinates $\sum_m \alpha_m \hat{e}_m$ and creates a new, different quale of that measurement (Eq. 39–41). This is formally analogous to frame-dependent measurements of velocity.

## Key results
The main theoretical result is the equivalence principle for consciousness (Eq. 11–18, 35): any cognitive system with functional consciousness (phenomenal judgment-representations) necessarily has phenomenal consciousness from its first-person cognitive frame. Zombies are logically impossible given broad physicalism and the principle of relativity. The apparent privacy of qualia is explained without dualism — qualia are not private, just frame-dependent; the transformation between first- and third-person frames is well-defined (delta function in the case of equivalent cognitive systems). The authors show that a first-person phenomenological report provides legitimate scientific data by being isomorphic to third-person measurements, advocating interdisciplinary collaboration between phenomenologists and cognitive neuroscientists.

## Relevance to this research
The relativistic theory of consciousness is directly relevant to the **participatory realism / "it from bit"** thread of the research program. Its core claim — that consciousness is observer-relative in a way formally analogous to physical relativity — resonates with the participatory ontology position (Wheeler's "it from bit", Fuchs' QBism) in which physical reality is co-constituted by the observer's participation rather than existing absolutely. The identification of qualia with phenomenal judgment-representations (Eq. 35) is an instance of the more general principle that observables are relational — the same move the VFE/active inference framework makes when grounding perception in belief-updating rather than passive sensing.

The three-tier cognitive model S = (T, P, C) maps naturally onto the VFE hierarchy: sensation corresponds to the likelihood/observation layer, perception to belief formation (the q-layer), and the functional consciousness module C to the higher-order inference that minimises free energy over representations. The frame-dependent observability of qualia is a structural parallel to gauge dependence in the GL(K) attention mechanism: different "observers" (gauge frames) see different coordinate representations of the same underlying belief geometry, with transformation laws mediating between them.

This paper is also relevant to the multi-agent active inference framework: the equivalence between Alice's and ALICE's frames under the relativistic principle is structurally analogous to gauge equivalence between agents sharing a common prior — different agents in different "cognitive frames of reference" nonetheless agree on measurement outcomes because they are related by an admissible transformation.
