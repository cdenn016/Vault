# Dimensional Reduction in Quantum Gravity

> [!info] Citation
> Gerard 't Hooft (1993). "Dimensional reduction in quantum gravity." Salamfestschrift, in *Conf. Proc. C930308*: 284–296. Preprint: [arXiv:gr-qc/9310026](https://arxiv.org/abs/gr-qc/9310026).

## TL;DR

't Hooft argues that, because black-hole entropy scales with horizon *area* rather than volume (Bekenstein–Hawking), the number of independent quantum degrees of freedom in any region of space is bounded by its boundary area in Planck units, not by its volume. He proposes that a $(d+1)$-dimensional gravitational world can therefore be fully described by data living on its $d$-dimensional boundary — the founding statement of the **holographic principle**.

## Problem & setting

Counting the fundamental degrees of freedom of quantum gravity. Naive volume-extensive counting overcounts wildly compared to what black-hole thermodynamics permits, signaling a deep dimensional reduction in the true theory.

## Method

A degree-of-freedom counting argument: the maximum entropy (hence information) storable in a region is set by the largest black hole that fits, whose entropy is one quarter its surface area. The conclusion is that physics in the bulk is encoded holographically on the boundary.

## Key results

The holographic bound on degrees of freedom and the holographic principle, subsequently sharpened by Susskind and formalized covariantly by [[bousso-2002-holographic-principle]]; realized concretely in AdS/CFT.

## Relevance to this research

This is the founding holographic-principle proposal that gives quantitative teeth to "it from bit": physical degrees of freedom are bounded and counted by an information measure on a boundary. It sits directly under the [[Emergent spacetime and holography]] theme that PIFB ([[participatory-it-from-bit]]) surveys, and supplies the degrees-of-freedom-counting backbone for the [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]] reading in which informational capacity, not a presupposed continuum, sets what physics can hold. It pairs with [[wheeler-1990-it-from-bit]] as the physics-side formalization of the informational-ontology slogan.
