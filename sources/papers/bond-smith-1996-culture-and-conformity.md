# Culture and Conformity: A Meta-Analysis of Studies Using Asch's Line Judgment Task

> [!info] Citation
> Rod Bond and Peter B. Smith (1996). *Culture and conformity: A meta-analysis of studies using Asch's (1952b, 1956) line judgment task*. Psychological Bulletin, 119(1), 111–137. DOI: 10.1037/0033-2909.119.1.111.

## TL;DR
Bond and Smith aggregate 133 Asch-type line-judgment studies conducted across 17 countries to quantify how conformity varies with experimental and cultural factors. They find that conformity increases with the size of the unanimous majority (with diminishing returns past three or four) and with the proportion of confederates, and that conformity is reliably higher in collectivist cultures than in individualist ones. They also document a temporal decline in measured conformity in the United States over the decades since Asch, suggesting the effect is sensitive to broader cultural climate.

## What it establishes
The meta-analysis converts the qualitative Asch effect into quantitative effect sizes and moderator relationships. The dependence of conformity on majority size $N$ is monotonic but saturating; the cultural moderator shows that the value a society places on group harmony versus individual autonomy scales the effective strength of normative pressure. By pooling across studies the paper provides population-level estimates of how strongly the social signal weighs against private judgment, and how that weighting shifts with group size and cultural context.

## Relevance to this research
This supplies quantitative effect sizes — conformity as a function of majority size, and as a function of cultural individualism — that could parameterize or validate the VFE social-coupling strength. In particular, the saturating dependence of conformity on $N$ is an empirical target for how the aggregate coupling weight $\sum_j \beta_{ij}$ should scale with the number of agreeing neighbours, and the collectivist/individualist contrast is a candidate calibration for the global coupling temperature or the prior-conformity strength. The honest scope: this is a calibration and validation source for the conformity regime, not a theoretical result; it tells the model what numbers to hit, not what the mechanism is.
