# Field Theory in Social Science: Selected Theoretical Papers

> [!info] Citation
> Kurt Lewin (Dorwin Cartwright, ed.) (1951). *Field Theory in Social Science: Selected Theoretical Papers*. Harper & Brothers, New York.

## TL;DR
This posthumous collection of Lewin's theoretical papers founds the dynamical, force-field view of psychology and group dynamics. Lewin frames behavior as motion within a "life space" — a representation of the person together with their psychologically relevant environment — driven by a field of psychological forces. His program is to treat individual and group behavior with the conceptual apparatus of physics: forces, valences, tensions, and equilibria, all defined in a topological/vectorial representational space rather than literal physical space. Group dynamics, social change, and resistance to change are analyzed as field phenomena, including the well-known unfreezing–change–refreezing account of how stable group equilibria shift.

## What it establishes
The organizing principle is Lewin's equation $B = f(P, E)$: behavior is a function of the person and the environment taken together as the life space, not of either alone. Within the life space, regions carry valences (attractions and repulsions) that generate psychological forces, and observed behavior is the resultant of these forces — motion toward equilibrium in a force field. Quasi-stationary social equilibria are maintained by opposing driving and restraining forces, so change is understood as altering this force balance rather than simply pushing harder. This is a qualitative, geometric dynamics of behavior, articulated in the language of vectors and fields but without a closed-form equation of motion.

## Relevance to this research
Lewin is the conceptual root of treating belief change as motion under forces in a representational space — the same metaphor the VFE program formalizes precisely. The overdamped flow $\dot{\mu} \propto -\nabla F$ is gradient descent in a force field, and the underdamped ansatz $M\ddot{\mu} + \gamma\dot{\mu} + \nabla F = 0$ is literally a force balance with inertia, friction, and a potential gradient. Lewin's "force field" and quasi-stationary equilibrium are the prose ancestors of the VFE force balance and its fixed points. This is strong as intellectual lineage but, in honesty, not a direct equation source: Lewin supplies the picture (behavior as motion in a field of forces) while the VFE functional supplies the actual potential $F$, the metric/mass, and the equations of motion.
