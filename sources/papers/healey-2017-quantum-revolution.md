# The Quantum Revolution in Philosophy

> [!info] Citation
> Healey, R. (2017). *The Quantum Revolution in Philosophy.* Oxford University Press. ISBN: 9780198714057.

## TL;DR

Healey develops a *pragmatist* interpretation of quantum theory: the quantum state is neither a description of physical reality (anti-realist about the wavefunction) nor a single agent's pure personal credence (anti-QBist on subjectivity). Instead, quantum theory furnishes *authoritative, objectively correct prescriptions* for how any suitably situated agent should apportion credence to claims about physical magnitudes. Born-rule probabilities are not subjective whims but objectively prescribed degrees of belief that any rational agent in the same physical situation ought to adopt. The book thus occupies a middle ground between QBist personalism and state-realism, retaining objectivity (the prescriptions are correct for everyone) while denying that the quantum state represents a feature of the world.

## Problem & setting

The book targets the gap between two extremes in quantum foundations: realist interpretations that reify the wavefunction (many-worlds, Bohm, collapse) and the QBist view that the state is one agent's personal Bayesian credence. Healey argues both miss the function of quantum theory, which is to give *advice* — prescriptions for credence and for the significance of magnitude claims — grounded in objective features of the physical situation rather than in an underlying ontic state.

## Method

Drawing on pragmatist philosophy of language and inferentialist accounts of content, Healey reconstrues quantum states, Born probabilities, and magnitude claims in terms of their inferential and prescriptive roles. Part I applies this prescriptive interpretation to the measurement problem, nonlocality, and the EPR/Bell setting; Part II draws out consequences for explanation, causation, objectivity, and representation. The argument is philosophical rather than formal, but it is disciplined by close attention to what the formalism licenses an agent to assert.

## Key results

Healey shows that the standard "paradoxes" (measurement, nonlocality) dissolve once the quantum state is read prescriptively rather than descriptively, and that one can keep *objectivity without ontic states*: the prescriptions are correct for all agents in a given situation even though no wavefunction-of-the-world is posited. The position preserves much of the QBist dissolution of the measurement problem while restoring an intersubjective, non-personal sense in which quantum advice is binding.

## Relevance to this research

Healey marks out exactly the position the project's [[participatory-it-from-bit]] needs to navigate toward: objectivity-as-binding-prescription-for-all-agents, between QBist personalism ([[fuchs2014-qbism-locality|fuchs-2014-qbism]], [[fuchs-schack-2013-bayesian-coherence]]) and full state-realism. The project's notion of objectivity is *consensus among coupled agents* — not one agent's private credence, yet not a view from nowhere either — which is structurally the pragmatist middle ground Healey articulates. In the project's terms, the "prescription any agent ought to adopt in this situation" is the belief an agent would hold after its variational dynamics have equilibrated against the shared coupling field ([[Multi-agent variational free energy]], [[Variational free energy]]); intersubjective bindingness corresponds to beliefs that agree up to [[Parallel transport]] between frames, with disagreement quantified by [[Holonomy]]. Healey supplies the philosophical vocabulary (prescription, objective-but-not-ontic) that lets the project state its [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]] commitments without collapsing into either solipsism or naive realism.
