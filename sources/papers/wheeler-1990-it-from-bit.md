# Information, Physics, Quantum: The Search for Links

> [!info] Citation
> Wheeler, J. A. (1990). "Information, Physics, Quantum: The Search for Links." In W. H. Zurek (Ed.), *Complexity, Entropy, and the Physics of Information*, Santa Fe Institute Studies in the Sciences of Complexity, pp. 309–336. Redwood City, CA: Addison-Wesley.

## TL;DR

Wheeler argues that physical reality — every particle, every field, even the spacetime continuum — derives its existence, meaning, and function from binary answers to yes-or-no questions: bits. Under the slogan **"it from bit,"** the act of observation (posing a question to nature and registering the answer) is constitutive of the observed phenomenon rather than merely revealing a pre-existing fact. The essay advances a programmatic thesis that information is ontologically prior to things, and that physics must one day be reconstructed from elementary observer-participatory acts of distinction.

## Problem & setting

The essay appears in the immediate aftermath of the quantum information revolution and responds to three deep open questions Wheeler frames as: "How come existence?", "How come the quantum?", and "How come 'one world' out of many observer-participants?" Prior foundational programs — Copenhagen, many-worlds, hidden variables — either bracket the measurement problem or multiply ontology. Wheeler instead proposes to dissolve it by placing information, not matter or fields, at the base of physics. He surveys the then-nascent connections between Shannon information theory, quantum measurement, black-hole thermodynamics (Bekenstein entropy), and the self-referential structure of the universe as a "self-excited circuit" that calls itself into being through observational acts.

## Method

The contribution is conceptual and programmatic rather than formal. Wheeler advances the thesis through three interlocking moves. First, the **ontological inversion**: "every it derives its existence from yes-or-no questions, from binary choices, from bits." Second, the **no-phenomenon-without-observation principle** ("no phenomenon is a phenomenon until it is an observed/registered phenomenon"), which generalises Bohr's insistence on classical registration apparatus into a universal constitutive role for observation. Third, the **"no tower of turtles" move**: rejecting an infinite regress of underlying substrate, Wheeler locates the bottom-level in elementary acts of distinction — the irreducible click of an apparatus that records a binary answer — rather than in a continuum or in fields. He draws analogies to delayed-choice experiments (in which a photon's past trajectory is determined retroactively by the present choice of measurement apparatus), which Wheeler reads as evidence that the observer's choice participates in constituting the history being probed.

## Key results

The essay does not derive formal theorems or report numerical results; its contributions are foundational and interpretive.

- **It from bit** — the thesis that every physical quantity ultimately draws its significance from information: bits registered by physical devices. Information is prior to energy, matter, and spacetime.
- **The participatory universe** — the claim that observers are not passive spectators: by choosing which question to ask and registering the answer, the observer participates in bringing the observed phenomenon into definite being. This is Wheeler's sharpest break with classical realism.
- **Self-excited circuit** — the universe as a totality participates in its own coming-into-being through the cumulative registered observations of all observer-participants across cosmological time. There is no external "creation." The circuit is self-referential.
- **Delayed-choice thought experiments** — presented as empirical evidence that the quantum past is not fixed until a present-moment apparatus choice forces a definite history into being, underscoring the constitutive rather than revelatory role of measurement.
- **Bekenstein–Hawking entropy** — cited as the clearest case where information (surface area in Planck units) is the fundamental quantity, subordinating thermodynamic entropy and geometry to an informational count.
- **Research agenda** — a set of open questions connecting information theory, quantum gravity, cosmology, and the origin of physical law, framed as the program that a future "physics of information" must address.
