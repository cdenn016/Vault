# Two Dogmas of Empiricism

> [!info] Citation
> Quine, W.V.O. (1951). "Two Dogmas of Empiricism." *The Philosophical Review*, 60(1), 20–43. https://doi.org/10.2307/2181906

## TL;DR
Quine attacks two foundational assumptions of logical empiricism: first, the analytic/synthetic distinction (the claim that some truths hold by meaning alone); second, reductionism (the claim that individual statements have their own empirical content in isolation). He argues instead that our beliefs face experience as a corporate body — the whole web of belief is what confronts the tribunal of experience, not individual sentences one at a time.

## Problem & setting
Logical empiricism in the tradition of Carnap and Ayer held that (i) analytic truths (true by meaning) are sharply separable from synthetic truths (true by fact), and (ii) each meaningful statement can in principle be translated into a verifiable sense-datum report. Quine challenges both dogmas, arguing that no satisfactory criterion for analyticity has ever been given that does not presuppose itself, and that the verification theory of meaning is incoherent if applied sentence by sentence.

## Method
The argument is conceptual and logical. Quine traces the circularity of attempts to define analyticity through synonymy, definition, interchangeability salva veritate, and semantic rules — each definition either presupposes the very notion it tries to explain or collapses the distinction. He then introduces the holism thesis: the unit of empirical significance is the whole of science rather than any individual sentence. Any statement can be held true come what may if we make sufficiently drastic adjustments elsewhere in the system; conversely, any statement can be revised. There is no privileged class of truths immune from empirical revision.

## Key results
The central conclusions are: (1) the analytic/synthetic distinction does not carve nature at its joints and has no clear, non-circular definition; (2) statements do not individually have empirical content — they confront experience only as a holistic web (the "Duhem–Quine thesis"); (3) the boundary between ontology and empirical theory is pragmatic rather than principled, and we should choose the ontology that best simplifies the total theory. Logical and mathematical truths differ from peripheral empirical claims only in their centrality within the web, not in kind.

## Relevance to this research
Quine's holism is directly relevant to the participatory realism and it-from-bit strand of the research program (see `PIFB.tex` and related manuscripts). The claim that beliefs face experience as a corporate body maps naturally onto the VFE framework, where minimization of variational free energy is a global, holistic operation over an entire hierarchy of belief distributions — not a sentence-by-sentence verification. The web-of-belief metaphor resonates with the multi-agent belief-coupling terms in the free energy functional (the β_ij KL transport terms), where no agent's belief is updated in isolation but always within the context of the coupled system. For the participatory realism manuscript, Quine's pragmatic ontology — entities are whatever simplifies the total theory — provides a classical precursor to the participatory/constructive account of physical ontology that the PIFB framework develops. The dissolution of the analytic/synthetic boundary also informs the blurring of "model" and "world" that participatory realism exploits.
