# Philosophy and the Scientific Image of Man

> [!info] Citation
> Sellars, Wilfrid (1962). "Philosophy and the Scientific Image of Man." In Robert Colodny (ed.), *Frontiers of Science and Philosophy* (pp. 35–78). Pittsburgh: University of Pittsburgh Press. Reprinted in *Science, Perception and Reality* (Routledge & Kegan Paul, 1963).

## TL;DR

Sellars distinguishes the *manifest image* (the framework of persons, intentions, colors, and ordinary objects in terms of which humans have always conceived themselves) from the *scientific image* (the framework of imperceptible particles and theoretical posits), and poses the problem of how to fuse them into a single "stereoscopic" view. The two images are rival but complementary conceptions of "man-in-the-world," and philosophy's task is their reconciliation.

## Relevance to this research

Sellars frames, in canonical form, the very two-images problem that [[participatory-it-from-bit]] proposes to address *geometrically*: the gap between the agent-level, participatory description (manifest: agents, beliefs, frames) and the formal scientific description (gauge structure, sections, connections). The manuscript's wager is that the fibre-bundle / gauge picture supplies the "stereoscopic fusion" — agents modeled as [[Agents as fibre-bundle sections]] are simultaneously manifest participants and scientific-image structure, related by [[Gauge transformation]].
