# Moderate Structural Realism about Space-Time

> [!info] Citation
> Esfeld, Michael, and Lam, Vincent (2008). "Moderate Structural Realism about Space-Time." *Synthese* **160**(1): 27–46. DOI: [10.1007/s11229-006-9076-2](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11229-006-9076-2).

## TL;DR

Esfeld and Lam carve out a *moderate* ontic structural realism that places objects and relations on the same ontological footing: objects are not eliminated (against radical OSR) but they have no intrinsic identity over and above the relations in which they stand (against object-first realism). They apply this to spacetime, arguing that spacetime points in general relativity are individuated only by the metric relations among them — and, that the position is naturally expressed in the *fibre-bundle* formulation of gauge theories, where points carry no identity independent of the relational/connection structure laid over them.

## Problem & setting

The structural-realism debate had polarized between Worrall's epistemic version ([[worrall-1989-structural-realism]]) and the radical, object-eliminating ontic version of French and Ladyman ([[french-ladyman-2003-remodelling]]). The radical version faces the objection that relations seem to require relata. Esfeld and Lam take general-relativistic spacetime — where the hole argument and general covariance already cast doubt on points as primitive individuals — as the testbed for a middle position that keeps objects without making them metaphysically prior to structure.

## Method

Conceptual analysis of the ontology of spacetime in two formulations of general relativity: the standard tensor formulation and the fibre-bundle formulation. In each, they argue that spacetime points exist but are individuated wholly by their metric/relational embedding — there are no bare "this-points" with primitive thisness. The fibre-bundle picture is the cleaner vehicle: base-manifold points and the structures (connections, sections) over them are mutually constituting, with no element bearing intrinsic identity. Objects and relations are thus ontologically co-fundamental ("on the same footing").

## Key results

- **Moderate OSR.** Objects and relations are equally fundamental; objects are real but lack intrinsic identity, being individuated only relationally. This blunts the "no relations without relata" objection while keeping the anti-haecceitist payoff of OSR.
- **Relational individuation of spacetime points.** Points in GR are individuated by metric relations, not by primitive identity — a structuralist reading of general covariance and the hole argument.
- **Fibre bundles as the natural formalism.** The position finds its most natural and explicit expression in the gauge-theoretic / fibre-bundle formulation, where the relevant ontology is sections-and-connection rather than self-standing points.

## Relevance to this research

This is the source that comes closest to a literal statement of the manuscript's pan-agentic metaphysics. The project models [[Agents as fibre-bundle sections]] over a base manifold, with beliefs transported by a connection ([[Gauge transformation]], holonomy); Esfeld and Lam argue, on independent philosophy-of-physics grounds, that exactly this fibre-bundle structure is where a defensible structural realism lives, and that the "points" (here, agents) are individuated only relationally — they have no intrinsic, frame-independent identity. That is precisely the manuscript's claim that an agent's belief is a frame-dependent section and only the gauge-invariant relational content is objective. The "moderate" twist — objects real but relationally individuated — matches the project better than the radical object-eliminating reading, because the program does carry concrete agents (sections) while denying them any privileged, view-from-nowhere state. It thus refines [[Structural realism]] for the project and links the [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]] thread of [[participatory-it-from-bit]] directly to the gauge-theoretic core; cross-cluster with gauge-theory because the bundle formalism is the shared substrate.
