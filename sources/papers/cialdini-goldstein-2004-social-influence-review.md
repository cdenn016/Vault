# Social Influence: Compliance and Conformity

> [!info] Citation
> Robert B. Cialdini and Noah J. Goldstein (2004). *Social influence: Compliance and conformity*. Annual Review of Psychology, 55, 591–621. DOI: 10.1146/annurev.psych.55.090902.142015.

## TL;DR
This Annual Review article is the authoritative modern synthesis of the social-influence literature, organizing decades of conformity and compliance research around three core human motivations: the goal of **accuracy** (forming correct perceptions and acting effectively), the goal of **affiliation** (building and maintaining social relationships), and the goal of **maintaining a positive self-concept**. The authors integrate classic findings (Asch conformity, Sherif norm formation, the Cialdini persuasion principles) and contemporary work into a goal-based framework, arguing that whether a given influence attempt succeeds depends on which motivational goal is active.

## What it establishes
The review's contribution is organizational rather than a single new model: it shows that the heterogeneous influence phenomena (social proof, reciprocity, commitment/consistency, authority, descriptive vs. injunctive norms) can be parsimoniously explained by which of the three superordinate goals an influence cue engages. Accuracy-driven influence corresponds closely to informational social influence (others as evidence), while affiliation- and self-concept-driven influence map onto normative pressures. The article also surveys the moderators — relationship closeness, group membership, public vs. private response — that tune the strength of each channel.

## Relevance to this research
This is a single authoritative entry point that ties together the empirical conformity and compliance literature the VFE program is meant to recover. It serves as the review-of-record for grounding claim (1) — that the model reproduces classical social-influence phenomena as limits of one functional — and is the natural source for the human-side citations a manuscript needs when motivating the coupling terms. Its accuracy-versus-affiliation split parallels the informational-versus-normative decomposition that maps onto distinct VFE terms (evidential belief coupling versus prior/entropy conformity pressure). It is strong as a survey anchor but is a review rather than load-bearing formal theory; it supplies phenomenology and citations, not equations.
