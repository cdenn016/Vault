# Materialism and Qualia: The Explanatory Gap

> [!info] Citation
> Levine, Joseph (1983). "Materialism and Qualia: The Explanatory Gap." *Pacific Philosophical Quarterly*, 64(4), 354–361. DOI: [10.1111/j.1468-0114.1983.tb00207.x](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1468-0114.1983.tb00207.x).

## TL;DR

Levine coins the term **explanatory gap** to name a specifically epistemic deficiency in psychophysical identity claims. Even granting that "pain is C-fiber firing" may be metaphysically true, the identity statement leaves something *unexplained* that ordinary scientific identities (e.g. "heat is molecular kinetic energy") do not: it fails to make intelligible *why* that physical state should feel the way it does, or feel like anything at all. The gap is between the explanatory power we expect from a reductive identity and the explanatory power psychophysical identities actually deliver.

## Problem & setting

Levine distinguishes his point from Kripke's modal argument against materialism. Kripke argued that mind–body identities, if true, must be necessary, yet appear contingent, and concluded they are likely false. Levine accepts that the materialist can resist the metaphysical conclusion, but argues a residue remains at the level of *explanation*. A good theoretical identity should let the physical description fully account for the macroscopic phenomenon: knowing the molecular story about heat tells you why heat does everything heat does. By contrast, the full physical–functional story about pain does not similarly account for its qualitative feel; the conceptual room for "why this feel, or any feel?" stays open. That open room is the explanatory gap.

## Method

Comparative conceptual analysis of theoretical identity statements. Levine contrasts the inferential closure of paradigm reductions (heat/kinetic energy, water/H2O) with the inferential openness of psychophysical reductions (pain/C-fiber firing), isolating the qualitative character of experience as exactly the term over which the physical account fails to be explanatorily complete. He frames the gap as compatible with materialism being *true* while still being a genuine cost — an epistemic, not necessarily ontological, problem.

## Key results

The essay supplies the standard vocabulary for one of the central problems in philosophy of mind. "Explanatory gap" becomes the canonical name for the failure of physical descriptions to render conscious quality intelligible, and the locution is later folded into Chalmers's "hard problem" (see [[chalmers-1995-facing-up-consciousness|chalmers-1995-facing-up]], [[chalmers-1996-conscious-mind]]). Levine's careful separation of the epistemic from the metaphysical reading is what lets the gap function as a constraint on theories that any candidate account must address.

## Relevance to this research

Levine's term is the precise object the manuscript [[participatory-it-from-bit]] claims to *relocate* rather than close. PIFB's relevance hinges on naming what it is doing in Levine's own vocabulary: the manuscript does not assert it has explained why a gauge frame feels like anything, but that the gap, once expressed in the project's geometry, moves from a brute physical-state-to-quale step to a question about frame-relative registered content within the [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]] ontology. Holding the note to Levine's careful distinction keeps the manuscript honest — a relocation of where the gap sits is not a closure of it, and the editorial discipline of this source is to refuse any conflation of the two. The gap also marks the explanatory threshold for the project's [[Mathematical consciousness science]] thread: structural theories such as [[tononi-2016-iit]] and the category-theoretic reading in [[tsuchiya-saigo-2016-category-theory-iit]] are precisely attempts to span (or dissolve) Levine's gap, and PIFB positions itself against that backdrop. This note pairs with [[nagel-1974-what-is-it-like]] as the two foundational statements of the subjective-character / explanatory-deficiency problem the program engages.
