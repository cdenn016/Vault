# Influence of a Consistent Minority on the Responses of a Majority in a Colour Perception Task

> [!info] Citation
> Serge Moscovici, Elisabeth Lage, and Martine Naffrechoux (1969). *Influence of a consistent minority on the responses of a majority in a colour perception task*. Sociometry, 32(4), 365–380. DOI: 10.2307/2786541.

## TL;DR
This is the founding "blue-green" study of minority influence. Groups judged the colour of unambiguously blue slides while a planted minority of two confederates consistently called them green. When the minority was *consistent* (always answering green), a small but reliable fraction of the majority's responses shifted toward green, and — more strikingly — the majority's perceptual threshold for distinguishing blue from green was durably altered, detectable even on a later private after-task. An inconsistent minority (green on only some trials) produced essentially no influence. The study establishes that a numerical minority can move a majority, and that *behavioral consistency* is the decisive condition.

## What it establishes
The core finding inverts the usual conformity asymmetry: influence does not flow only from many to few. A consistent minority introduces a stable, conflicting response pattern that the majority cannot easily dismiss, producing both manifest response change and a latent, internalized shift in perception (conversion) that outlasts the interaction. The contrast between consistent and inconsistent minorities isolates consistency — not numbers — as the active variable, and the persistence of the after-effect distinguishes minority "conversion" from majority "compliance." The result is a standing challenge to any model in which influence is purely a function of the size of the opposing camp.

## Relevance to this research
Minority influence is a stress-test for any consensus model. A naive averaging or overdamped flow with size-weighted coupling washes out a small minority, yet consistent minorities here persist and even convert the majority. The honest framing is that this is partly an *open challenge* to the VFE program: can the dynamics reproduce asymmetric minority persistence? A plausible mechanism within the model is that a confident, consistent minority carries low-$\Sigma$ / high-precision beliefs, giving its agents large effective coupling pull (small KL to themselves, sharp likelihood) and, in the underdamped reading, large effective inertial mass, so they resist being averaged away and instead drag the majority's belief over time. Whether the functional actually yields conversion (a durable shift after the minority is removed) rather than transient compliance is a concrete falsifiable test, not an established result.
