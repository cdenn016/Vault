# Psychedelics, Meditation, and Self-Consciousness

> [!info] Citation
> Millière R, Carhart-Harris RL, Roseman L, Trautwein F-M and Berkovich-Ohana A (2018). "Psychedelics, Meditation, and Self-Consciousness." *Frontiers in Psychology* 9:1475. https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2018.01475

## TL;DR
This paper bridges the neuroscience of meditation and psychedelic research by systematically comparing their phenomenological and neurophysiological effects on self-consciousness. The authors argue that self-consciousness is a multidimensional construct encompassing narrative, agentive, and somatosensory aspects, and that "self-loss" or "ego dissolution" can take several distinct forms rather than constituting a unitary phenomenon. They survey evidence that both modalities disrupt the default mode network (DMN) and alter narrative, bodily, and spatial self-consciousness — but in quantitatively and qualitatively different ways.

## Problem & setting
The growing scientific literature on meditation (contemplative neuroscience) and on classical psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD, DMT, 5-MeO-DMT) had largely developed in parallel, despite extensive phenomenological and neurophysiological overlap. The paper aims to synthesize these two bodies of work, focusing on alterations of self-experience. Prior art includes Fox et al. (2016) — a meta-analysis of 78 neuroimaging studies of four meditation styles — and the Carhart-Harris group's fMRI studies of psilocybin and LSD, as well as phenomenological frameworks from Gallagher (2000), Seth (2013), and Damasio (1999).

## Method
The paper is a theoretical and empirical review. It integrates neuroimaging meta-analyses, psychometric questionnaire data (5D-ASC/5D-OAV scales), neurophenomenological studies (MEG combined with microphenomenological interview), and qualitative first-person reports (including unpublished online survey data by R.M.). The core conceptual move is a decomposition of self-consciousness into (a) narrative aspects (de se thought, autobiographical memory, mental time travel), (b) multisensory aspects (body ownership, bodily awareness/interoception, spatial self-location), and (c) non-dual / pure consciousness states. This taxonomy is mapped against available evidence for each induced alteration type.

## Key results
Focused Attention (FA) and mindfulness meditation deactivate key DMN nodes (mPFC, PCC), consistent with suppression of mind-wandering and self-referential thought. Psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD, ayahuasca) produce comparable decreases in DMN activity and integrity, and additionally increase between-network connectivity (DMN–task-positive network) and global connectivity. Neural correlates of drug-induced ego dissolution (DIED) include decreased DMN integrity, decreased PHC–RSC connectivity, increased DMN–TPN functional connectivity, increased entropic brain activity, and altered insular and temporoparietal junction (TPJ) connectivity. Non-dual awareness (NDA) meditation decreases the anticorrelation between DMN and task-positive networks — a pattern also observed under psilocybin. Experienced meditators (but rarely novices) can inhibit body ownership and spatial self-location; psychedelics (especially DMT and 5-MeO-DMT) can completely suppress bodily awareness and spatial self-location. The paper calls for caution against conflating temporary states of self-loss with "selflessness" as a stable behavioral trait.

## Relevance to this research
This paper is most directly relevant to the participatory realism and philosophy-of-mind threads in the broader research program. Within the VFE / active inference framework, the DMN is plausibly interpreted as encoding high-level self-priors (the "narrative self" as a hierarchical prior over autobiographical context), and its disruption by psychedelics or meditation corresponds to a collapse of the prior–posterior gap in self-referential inference. The paper's multidimensional taxonomy of self-consciousness maps onto the layered belief hierarchy of the VFE model (narrative self ↔ hyper-priors; bodily awareness ↔ low-level sensory beliefs). The observation that weakened top-down priors (reduced binocular rivalry switching under psilocybin; attenuated mismatch negativity under LSD) mediate ego dissolution resonates with the VFE model's emphasis on prior strength as a gauge coupling — high-coupling priors rigidly constrain posterior beliefs, while reduced coupling allows larger posterior excursions away from the prior, producing experiences of boundary dissolution. The NDA and pure consciousness discussions are directly relevant to the PIFB (participatory it-from-bit) manuscript's treatment of consciousness and self.
