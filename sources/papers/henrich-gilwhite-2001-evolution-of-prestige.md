# The Evolution of Prestige: Freely Conferred Deference as a Mechanism for Enhancing the Benefits of Cultural Transmission

> [!info] Citation
> Henrich, J., & Gil-White, F. J. (2001). "The evolution of prestige: Freely conferred deference as a mechanism for enhancing the benefits of cultural transmission." *Evolution and Human Behavior* **22**(3), 165–196. DOI: [10.1016/S1090-5138(00)00071-4](https://doi.org/10.1016/S1090-5138(00)00071-4).

## TL;DR
A theory of prestige as an adaptation for social learning, sharply distinguished from dominance. Henrich and Gil-White argue that once humans became reliant on acquiring skills by copying others, selection favored a psychology that identifies the most competent models and *freely confers deference* on them — attention, gifts, and compliance — in exchange for proximity and learning opportunities. Prestige is thus an information-goods market: deference is the price learners pay for access to high-quality cultural information, and prestige hierarchies are a byproduct of distributed copying decisions rather than coercion.

## What it establishes
The paper assembles ethnographic and psychological evidence that prestige and dominance are cognitively and behaviorally distinct status systems with different emotional and postural signatures, and that the prestige system is organized around the logic of selective social learning. Because learners cannot always directly evaluate skill, they rely on cues — including the deference others already show a model — producing a self-reinforcing dynamic in which attention concentrates on a few high-skill individuals. The model predicts copying that is biased toward success and competence, the spread of even incidental traits of prestigious models (a runaway/"prestige-bias" amplification), and the rational allocation of limited learning attention to the most informative sources.

## Relevance to this research
Prestige bias is a concrete, asymmetric rule for allocating social-learning attention — heavier coupling toward high-status, high-skill sources — which a heterogeneous attention/coupling kernel $\beta_{ij}$ in the multi-agent VFE model must be able to represent. It grounds the claim that influence weights are not uniform across neighbors: the softmax attention should be able to concentrate on a small set of informative agents the way deference concentrates on prestigious models. The "free conferral in exchange for information" framing also resonates with the precision-weighted account of why an agent down-weights its own prior to absorb a trusted neighbor's belief. Strong, specification-level relevance to the structure of the coupling kernel, though prestige here is an evolutionary-psychological mechanism rather than a derived term of the functional.
