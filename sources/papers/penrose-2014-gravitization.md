# On the Gravitization of Quantum Mechanics 1: Quantum State Reduction

> [!info] Citation
> Penrose, Roger (2014). "On the Gravitization of Quantum Mechanics 1: Quantum State Reduction." *Foundations of Physics*, 44, 557–575. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10701-013-9770-0

## TL;DR
Penrose argues that the principles of general relativity should modify quantum mechanics — "gravitizing" QM rather than quantizing gravity — and that this modification resolves the measurement paradox via objective gravitational state reduction. Quantum superpositions of states with significant mass displacement are predicted to be unstable, decaying spontaneously with lifetime τ ≈ ℏ/E_G, where E_G is the gravitational self-energy of the difference between the two superposed mass distributions. This is the Diósi-Penrose (DP) proposal for objective wavefunction collapse.

## Problem & setting
Standard quantum mechanics harbors a fundamental inconsistency between its two operative principles: unitary evolution U (continuous, deterministic, linear) and state reduction R (discontinuous, probabilistic). This "measurement paradox" (Leggett's terminology, preferred by Penrose over "measurement problem") demands explanation. Most approaches treat R as an approximation arising from U; Penrose instead treats U as the approximation, proposing a nonlinear gravitational correction that yields R as a limiting description. The companion paper [16] addresses the "gravitization of quantum mechanics 2" (on the OR proposal and its extensions).

## Method
The core proposal, developed jointly with Diósi, identifies a fundamental energy uncertainty E_G associated with any quantum superposition of two mass distributions. When a massive object is placed in superposition of two spatially distinct locations, the two branches correspond to two distinct space-time geometries. From the Einsteinian (general-relativistic) perspective, these belong to inequivalent quantum field theory vacua, making their superposition formally illegal in the sense of QFT (inner products between states in different Hilbert spaces diverge). Penrose estimates the resulting energy uncertainty via the gravitational self-energy of the mass difference distribution:

$$E_G = \text{(gravitational interaction energy to separate two coincident copies of the mass to their superposed locations)}$$

computed within Newtonian gravity. Invoking the Heisenberg time-energy uncertainty relation then gives a superposition lifetime:

$$\tau \approx \frac{\hbar}{E_G}$$

For a uniform solid sphere of radius a and mass m displaced by distance b, E_G approaches ~(2/3) of its asymptotic value already at b ~ a (one diameter), making the formula largely insensitive to large displacements. The principle of equivalence is invoked more rigorously as a second motivation: the Newtonian and Einsteinian wavefunctions differ by a phase factor containing a t^3 term (coefficient proportional to g^2), implying different positive-frequency definitions and hence different vacua — the Galilean limit of the Unruh effect.

## Key results
The main result is the lifetime formula τ ≈ ℏ/E_G for spontaneous collapse of mass-superposition states, with E_G the Newtonian gravitational self-energy of the difference mass distribution. All R processes, including deliberate measurements (where mass movement inside the detector triggers collapse) and environmental decoherence (where entangled environmental mass displacement drives collapse), are subsumed under this single gravitational mechanism, providing a clear objective ontology for state reduction without appeal to unobservable degrees of freedom. The 1975 Colella-Overhauser-Werner experiment is cited as confirming that the equivalence principle is respected by quantum mechanics in the relevant regime.
