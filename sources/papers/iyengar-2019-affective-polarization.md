# The Origins and Consequences of Affective Polarization in the United States

> [!info] Citation
> Iyengar, S., Lelkes, Y., Levendusky, M., Malhotra, N., & Westwood, S. J. (2019). *The Origins and Consequences of Affective Polarization in the United States*. Annual Review of Political Science 22:129-146. DOI: [10.1146/annurev-polisci-051117-073034](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-polisci-051117-073034).

## TL;DR
An authoritative review that distinguishes **affective polarization** — the tendency of partisans to dislike and distrust the opposing party and to feel warmly toward their own — from **ideological/issue polarization**, the divergence in policy positions. The authors argue affective polarization is rooted in partisanship as a social identity rather than in growing issue disagreement, survey how it is measured (feeling thermometers, social-distance and trust items), trace its causes (identity, partisan sorting, hostile media, elite cues), and document its consequences for interpersonal trust, discrimination, and democratic functioning.

## What it establishes
The central conceptual move is to separate two quantities that are often conflated. Issue polarization is the spread or group separation of policy positions $\theta_i$; affective polarization is the gap between in-party and out-party affect, typically the feeling-thermometer difference $A_i = T_i^{\text{in}} - T_i^{\text{out}}$. The review marshals evidence that $A_i$ has risen sharply even where issue positions $\theta_i$ have moved little, locating the driver in social-identity processes (in-group favoritism, out-group derogation) rather than in substantive disagreement, with partisan sorting and a hostile information environment as amplifiers.

## Relevance to this research
This disambiguates which polarization the program is actually predicting and thereby guards against overclaiming: the VFE flow naturally describes distance and dispersion in belief means $\mu_i$ — issue/ideological polarization — whereas affect is a separate axis the basic functional does not directly model. The social-identity mechanism it foregrounds is, however, a candidate ingredient for the program's prior and coupling structure: in-group/out-group affect could enter as an identity-dependent sign or magnitude on the attention weight $\beta_{ij}$ (repulsion from out-group neighbors), which is also what a backlash result like Bail et al. would require. Honestly, this is strong calibration context adjacent to the core dynamics rather than machinery the functional currently uses.
