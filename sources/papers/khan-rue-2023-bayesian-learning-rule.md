# The Bayesian Learning Rule

> [!info] Citation
> Khan, M. E., & Rue, H. (2023). The Bayesian Learning Rule. Journal of Machine Learning Research, 24(281), 1-46. arXiv:2107.04562.

## TL;DR

A remarkably broad swathe of machine-learning algorithms — ridge regression, Newton's method, the Kalman filter, gradient descent, RMSprop, Adam, dropout, expectation-maximization, Laplace approximation, and variational inference — turn out to be special cases of a single update that the authors call the *Bayesian learning rule*. The rule is natural-gradient descent on the natural parameters of an exponential-family approximation to the posterior. Its organizing trick is the natural-to-expectation parameter duality of exponential families: the [[Natural gradient]] of an objective with respect to the natural parameter equals the *ordinary* gradient with respect to the dual (expectation) parameter, so the Fisher-preconditioned step can be computed without ever forming or inverting the [[Fisher information metric]]. Choosing a posterior family and an approximation to the natural gradient then recovers each named algorithm as a particular instance, exposing what each method is implicitly assuming about the posterior and explaining why second-order and adaptive methods carry the curvature information they do.

## Problem & setting

The point of departure is the variational Bayesian objective: rather than seek a point estimate of parameters $\theta$, fit an approximate posterior $q(\theta)$ to minimize the variational free energy
$$ \mathcal{L}(q) = \mathbb{E}_{q}\!\left[\ell(\theta)\right] + \mathbb{E}_{q}\!\left[\log \frac{q(\theta)}{p(\theta)}\right], $$
where $\ell(\theta)$ is the (negative log-)loss and $p(\theta)$ the prior, so that $\mathcal{L}$ is the expected loss plus a $\mathrm{KL}$ regularizer against the prior. Prior art had already established two ingredients separately. Amari's [[Natural gradient]] (see [[amari-1998-natural-gradient]]) showed that descent on a curved statistical manifold should premultiply the Euclidean gradient by the inverse Fisher metric, giving a reparameterization-invariant step. And natural-gradient variational inference — developed by Khan and collaborators in the years preceding this synthesis — had shown that for exponential-family $q$ the natural-gradient step takes an especially clean form. The contribution of this paper is to take that clean form as a *primitive learning rule* and demonstrate that the standard algorithm zoo falls out of it under explicit, identifiable approximations, assuming throughout that the posterior approximation belongs to an exponential family in minimal representation.

## Method

Restrict $q$ to a minimal exponential family with natural (canonical) parameter $\lambda$, sufficient statistics $T(\theta)$, and expectation (mean / dual) parameter $\mu = \mathbb{E}_q[T(\theta)]$. The map $\lambda \leftrightarrow \mu$ is the Legendre duality generated by the log-partition function, and it is exactly the duality between the two flat affine connections of information geometry. The Bayesian learning rule is the natural-gradient step on the natural parameter,
$$ \lambda_{t+1} \;=\; \lambda_t \;-\; \rho_t\, \tilde{\nabla}_{\lambda}\, \mathcal{L}(\lambda_t), $$
where $\tilde{\nabla}_\lambda = F(\lambda)^{-1}\nabla_\lambda$ is the Fisher-preconditioned gradient. The mechanism that makes this practical is the identity, central to the paper,
$$ \tilde{\nabla}_{\lambda}\,\mathcal{L} \;=\; F(\lambda)^{-1}\nabla_{\lambda}\mathcal{L} \;=\; \nabla_{\mu}\,\mathcal{L}, $$
the natural gradient with respect to $\lambda$ equals the *plain* gradient with respect to the dual parameter $\mu$. The Fisher metric never has to be assembled or inverted; one differentiates the objective in expectation coordinates instead. Concretely, the update reads off the expectation-parameter gradient and moves the natural parameter along it,
$$ \lambda_{t+1} \;=\; \lambda_t \;-\; \rho_t\, \nabla_{\mu}\, \mathcal{L}(\mu_t). $$

For a Gaussian posterior $q = \mathcal{N}(m, S^{-1})$ the natural parameters are $(S\,m,\ -\tfrac12 S)$ and the expectation parameters are $(m,\ m m^\top + S^{-1})$, so substituting the Gaussian into the rule yields coupled updates for the mean and precision in which the precision accumulates the expected Hessian of the loss and the mean takes a Newton-like step preconditioned by that precision. The algorithm zoo emerges by two independent choices: the posterior family, and the approximation to $\nabla_\mu \mathcal{L}$. A Gaussian posterior with the exact expected Hessian gives a Newton / Laplace-style update; a delta (Dirac) posterior collapses the rule to ordinary gradient descent; replacing the full precision with a diagonal running average of squared gradients produces RMSprop, and adding the first-moment average produces Adam; a Bernoulli posterior recovers dropout; the linear-Gaussian state-space instance is the Kalman filter; and retaining the full variational objective gives variational inference and expectation-maximization. Each algorithm is thus tagged with the posterior assumption and curvature approximation it silently makes.

## Key results

The principal result is unificational rather than a benchmark number: the paper derives the named algorithms — ridge regression, Newton's method, the Kalman filter, gradient descent, RMSprop, Adam, dropout, EM, Laplace approximation, and variational inference — as explicit special cases of the single rule, with each derivation making transparent which posterior family and which natural-gradient approximation it corresponds to. This furnishes a common ledger on which adaptive and second-order optimizers can be compared, and a constructive recipe for new algorithms by varying the two choices. The evidence is mathematical and derivational; the paper is a synthesis and unification rather than an empirical contribution, and it does not rest its case on a leaderboard. Where it cites empirical support, that support comes from the constituent prior works (for example the Adam-from-Bayes and natural-gradient VI papers) rather than from new large-scale experiments introduced here. The strength of the claim is therefore in the breadth and exactness of the reductions, not in reported accuracy figures.

## Relevance to this research

This paper turns Amari's natural gradient (see [[amari-1998-natural-gradient]], [[Natural gradient]]) from a geometric principle into the program's actual learning rule. The VFE transformer maintains, per token, a Gaussian belief tuple $(\mu, \Sigma, \phi)$ and updates it by minimizing variational free energy; the Bayesian learning rule is precisely the closed-form, Fisher-preconditioned Gaussian update that such a belief revision should be — natural-gradient descent on the natural parameters of an exponential-family posterior, with the step read off the expectation-parameter gradient. The natural-to-expectation duality it exploits is the same dual-affine structure that underlies the program's information geometry ([[Information geometry and natural gradient]], [[Fisher information metric]]), and it explains why the belief update can be computed without explicitly inverting a Fisher metric: the inversion is absorbed into the change of coordinates between $\lambda$ and $\mu$. It also frames the architecture's E-step and any Adam-style M-step optimizer as two instances of one rule operating on different posterior families, and it directly informs the claim, in the [[VFE Transformer Program]], that iterative VFE minimization over Gaussian beliefs ([[Variational free energy]]) performs a principled Bayesian update rather than an ad hoc gradient step.
