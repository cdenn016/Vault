# Whatever next? Predictive brains, situated agents, and the future of cognitive science

> [!info] Citation
> Clark, Andy (2013). "Whatever next? Predictive brains, situated agents, and the future of cognitive science." *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 36, 181–253. https://doi.org/10.1017/S0140525X12000477

## TL;DR
Clark argues that brains are fundamentally prediction machines that implement hierarchical generative models to minimize prediction error across a bidirectional cascade of cortical processing. This framework — hierarchical predictive processing (HPP) — is proposed as a unifying account of perception, action, attention, and cognition, in which top-down predictions attempt to "explain away" bottom-up sensory signals and only residual errors propagate forward. The paper critically surveys the evidence, challenges, and broader philosophical implications of this view, concluding it offers the best current candidate for a unified science of mind and action.

## Problem & setting
The central problem is understanding how the brain, enclosed within a "black box," can infer the causal structure of the world from only the patterns of change in its own internal states. Classical feedforward accounts are insufficient; the brain must do something more — using prior knowledge to actively predict incoming sensory signals. Clark surveys the hierarchical predictive coding tradition from Helmholtz through Rao and Ballard (1999) and Friston (2005, 2010), including its extension to motor action (active inference), and asks whether this constitutes a genuinely unified computational architecture of mind.

## Method
This is a theoretical target article (published with open peer commentary in *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*), not an empirical or computational study. Clark synthesizes the hierarchical predictive processing literature, drawing on:

- Rao and Ballard's (1999) bidirectional predictive coding model of visual cortex, in which backward connections carry predictions and forward connections carry only prediction errors.
- Friston's free-energy formulation, in which prediction error minimization is a consequence of minimizing an information-theoretic free energy that upper-bounds surprisal: $F \geq -\log p(o | m)$.
- The "action-oriented" extension in which motor commands are generated by proprioceptive prediction errors self-suppressed through movement rather than neuronal signaling, unifying perception and action under a single predictive imperative.
- Precision-weighting as the computational account of attention: gain on error units is modulated by estimated signal reliability, controlling the relative influence of prior expectations versus incoming data.

The duplex architecture posits two functionally distinct unit populations at each level: representation units encoding conditional expectations of perceptual causes, and error units encoding precision-weighted prediction errors, with forward connections carrying error and backward connections carrying predictions.

## Key results
The paper is theoretical; its main contributions are:

1. A systematic synthesis demonstrating that hierarchical predictive processing provides a unified computational account of perception, action, attention, learning, and bi-stable phenomena (e.g., binocular rivalry explained as a double-well energy landscape in which no single hypothesis fully explains the driving signal).
2. The account of attention as precision-weighting: attention modulates gain on error units, implementing a Bayesian reweighting of bottom-up evidence versus top-down prior expectations.
3. A defense of the "Bayesian Brain" hypothesis: behavioral evidence that humans approximate Bayesian optimality across perception and motor control domains (motion illusions, cue integration, color constancy) is interpreted as indirect support for HPP as the implementing mechanism.
4. Non-classical receptive field effects, repetition suppression, and biphasic response dynamics all receive natural explanation as prediction-error signals rather than feature detectors.
5. The "dark room" objection — that prediction-error minimization predicts organisms should seek a sensory-impoverished environment — is addressed by noting that itinerant exploration is itself predicted given species-typical prior expectations about navigating a changing world.

## Relevance to this research
This paper is the canonical introduction to predictive processing / active inference as a unified framework, with direct relevance to this research program in several respects. The free-energy minimization framework that Clark surveys is the biological and theoretical precedent for the gauge-theoretic VFE transformer: the VFE model's belief update dynamics (iterative KL minimization across the hierarchy $h \to s \to p \to q \to o$) are a structured, gauge-equivariant generalization of the prediction-error minimization scheme Clark describes. The precision-weighting account of attention — gain modulation on error units — maps directly onto the attention weights $\beta_{ij}$ in the VFE free energy, where the softmax temperature $\tau$ plays the role of inverse precision and the entropy term $\tau \beta_{ij} \log(\beta_{ij}/\pi_{ij})$ ensures a proper stationary point. The hierarchical generative model structure (hyper-priors $h$, model-level priors $s$, beliefs $q$) parallels the HPP hierarchy with empirical Bayes. The paper's discussion of "explaining away" and the duplex error/representation unit architecture is the conceptual ancestor of the VFE's separation of belief means, variances, and prediction-error signals. The extension to action-oriented active inference (Friston et al. 2009, 2010) is the biological motivation for the multi-agent extension in the MAgent model.
