# Statistical distance and Hilbert space

> [!info] Citation
> William K. Wootters (1981). "Statistical distance and Hilbert space." *Physical Review D* 23(2): 357–362. DOI: [10.1103/PhysRevD.23.357](https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevD.23.357).

## TL;DR

Wootters shows that the natural notion of *statistical distance* between two quantum states — defined operationally as the number of distinguishable intermediate states, i.e. how many standard deviations of measurement separate them — coincides exactly with the angle between the corresponding rays in Hilbert space. The geometry of distinguishability, built from pure statistics (counting how reliably an observer can tell states apart), reproduces the Hilbert-space inner-product geometry. This identifies the [[Fisher information metric|Fisher–Rao]] statistical metric with the Fubini–Study metric on quantum state space.

## Problem & setting

Two probability distributions over measurement outcomes are "far apart" if few samples suffice to distinguish them. Wootters formalizes this *statistical distance* for the outcome distributions generated by quantum measurements and asks how it relates to the abstract Hilbert-space distance between the underlying state vectors, which is otherwise an unmotivated mathematical postulate.

## Method

He defines statistical distance via the distinguishability of neighboring distributions — the Fisher–Rao arc length of the path of measurement statistics — and maximizes it over choices of measurement. For pure quantum states the optimal measurement yields a statistical distance equal to $\arccos|\langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle|$, the Fubini–Study angle between the rays. The Hilbert-space geometry thereby acquires an operational, information-theoretic justification: the inner product is not arbitrary but is fixed by how distinguishable states are under measurement.

## Key results

1. Quantum statistical distance (maximal Fisher–Rao distinguishability over measurements) equals the angle between Hilbert-space rays.
2. The Hilbert-space metric is derivable from the statistics of distinguishing states, giving an operational/epistemic grounding to the formalism.
3. Establishes the conceptual root of the modern quantum-information-geometric distinguishability metric (later sharpened into the quantum Fisher / SLD metric by [[braunstein-caves-1994-quantum-fisher]]).

## Relevance to this research

This paper is the historical root of the distinguishability metric that [[participatory-it-from-bit]] *pulls back*. PIFB's program treats the geometry of an agent's belief space as primary and asks what physical structure it induces; Wootters provides the founding demonstration that a core piece of physics — the Hilbert-space geometry of quantum states — is itself the pullback of a purely statistical, observer-defined distinguishability metric. That is precisely the participatory move PIFB generalizes: physical geometry as the shadow of how an agent can tell its states apart. The Fisher–Rao statistical distance Wootters uses is the same [[Fisher information metric]] the project employs throughout, and the identification "statistical distance = geometric angle" is the conceptual template for PIFB's signature/complexification arguments and for any quantum extension.
