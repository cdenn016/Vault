# Explaining Culture: A Naturalistic Approach

> [!info] Citation
> Sperber, D. (1996). *Explaining Culture: A Naturalistic Approach*. Blackwell, Oxford. ISBN 978-0-631-20045-1.

## TL;DR
The founding statement of the "epidemiology of representations" / cultural-attraction program. Sperber argues that culture should be explained naturalistically as the distribution of mental and public representations across a population, and — against meme-style replication accounts — that cultural items are almost never faithfully copied. Each act of transmission is a reconstruction: a receiver infers a representation from a public artifact (an utterance, a gesture, an object), and because inference is shaped by shared cognitive dispositions, repeated transmission *transforms* representations, pulling them toward psychological "attractors" rather than preserving them intact.

## What it establishes
The central move is to replace replication with *transformation toward attractors*. Cultural stability is then not high-fidelity copying but the convergence of many independent, lossy reconstructions onto the same cognitively privileged forms — attractors fixed by perceptual, memory, and inferential biases (and by ecological factors). On this view a "cultural type" is a cluster of similar representations maintained by an attractor in representational space, and explaining a culture means identifying the psychological and ecological factors that create and position these attractors. The framework is causal-cognitive rather than mathematical; it reframes the unit and the mechanism of cultural inheritance and sets up the contrast with selectionist transmission models.

## Relevance to this research
The cultural-attraction view treats each transmission as a transformative inference toward an attractor, which is conceptually the same move as treating an agent's posterior belief as pulled toward a prior attractor during VFE minimization — the self-coupling term $\alpha\,\mathrm{KL}(q_i\,\|\,p_i)$ is the formal version of an attractor exerting a reconstructive pull on a received belief. It is honestly *adjacent* rather than load-bearing: Sperber supplies no equations and the attractor notion here is qualitative, but it is the intellectual source of the "transformative versus preservative" debate that Henrich & Boyd (2002) later formalized and that underlies the program's belief-recreation framing. Its main role in this neighborhood is to motivate why beliefs are reconstructed-under-priors, not transmitted verbatim. See [[Belief inertia]].
