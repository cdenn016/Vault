# How the Laws of Physics Lie

> [!info] Citation
> Cartwright, Nancy (1983). *How the Laws of Physics Lie*. Oxford: Oxford University Press / Clarendon Press. ISBN: 9780198247043.

## TL;DR

Cartwright argues that the fundamental laws of physics, precisely because they are explanatorily powerful and broad, are *not literally true* of the world: they describe idealized models, not actual messy systems. The fundamental laws "lie" in that they hold only of the abstract objects in our models; what is true of real systems are the local, hedged *phenomenological* laws. Explanatory power and truth pull in opposite directions — the more unified and fundamental a law, the further it is from describing any concrete situation.

## Problem & setting

Standard scientific realism infers from the explanatory and predictive success of fundamental laws to their (approximate) truth. Cartwright attacks the inferential step. She distinguishes *fundamental* (theoretical, covering, abstract) laws from *phenomenological* (concrete, situation-specific) laws and argues that only the latter can be literally true, because the fundamental laws apply exactly only to idealized model systems that abstract away from interfering factors.

## Method

Case studies from physics (electromagnetism, quantum damping, the use of ceteris paribus clauses, the role of approximation and model-building in deriving predictions). She analyzes how physicists actually derive empirical results — through layers of idealization, approximation, and model construction — to show that the fundamental law never directly contacts the concrete phenomenon; bridge models and corrections do the real descriptive work.

## Key results

- **Laws as idealizations.** Fundamental laws are true of models, not of the world; they trade descriptive accuracy for breadth and unifying power.
- **Phenomenological vs. fundamental laws.** Truth migrates to the concrete, hedged laws; the fundamental laws function as organizing tools.
- **Anti-realism about laws, realism about entities/causes.** Cartwright keeps causal and entity realism while denying that the covering laws are literally true.

## Relevance to this research

Cartwright is the close neighbor to the manuscript's "laws as grammar" reading. If physical laws are idealizing tools that organize and license model-building rather than literal truths about a mind-independent world, then they function much like a *grammar*: a framework that structures what can be coherently said/inferred within a domain, not a mirror of substance. That dovetails with the project's [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]] stance and with [[cassirer-1910-substance-function|Cassirer 1910]]'s function-over-substance — laws are functional, framework-constituting devices, not descriptions of intrinsic natures. It also supports the broader anti-correspondence epistemology the manuscript shares with [[vanfraassen-1980-scientific-image|van Fraassen 1980]] and the underdetermination reading of [[quine-1951-two-dogmas]]: success does not certify literal truth. In the program's terms, an agent's "laws" are the grammar of its belief-updating, gauge-relative and instrumental, not a privileged readout of the world.
