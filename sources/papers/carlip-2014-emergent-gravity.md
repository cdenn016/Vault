# Challenges for Emergent Gravity

> [!info] Citation
> Carlip, S. (2014). "Challenges for Emergent Gravity." Studies in History and Philosophy of Modern Physics. arXiv:1207.2504 [gr-qc].

## TL;DR
Carlip surveys the principal obstacles that any model of emergent gravity must overcome to reproduce the observational underpinnings of general relativity. The paper classifies emergent models into two broad types — those in which fundamental degrees of freedom live in a pre-existing background environment (type I) and those in which spacetime itself is emergent (type II) — and argues that recovering local Lorentz invariance, the equivalence principle, diffeomorphism invariance, and background independence simultaneously is extremely difficult. No current ad hoc approach is likely to succeed without some deeper organizing principle yet to be identified.

## Problem & setting
General relativity enjoys enormous observational support, yet its quantization remains intractable, motivating the hypothesis that gravity is a collective, "emergent" phenomenon arising from more primitive degrees of freedom. Examples range from analog models, composite graviton proposals, and pregeometric approaches (type I) to quantum graphity, group field theory, and Wheeler's "it from bit" (type II). The paper asks what constraints observational successes of GR place on any such model, collecting challenges that were known individually but not assembled in a single place.

## Method
The analysis is conceptual and theoretical rather than computational. Carlip works from the Ehlers-Pirani-Schild (EPS) construction to identify the minimal assumptions — local Lorentz invariance, universality of free fall, absence of a second-clock effect, absence of nondynamical background structures, and compatibility of conformal and projective structures — that force a metric description of gravity. He then examines each requirement as a constraint on emergent models: (i) Lorentz invariance and the problem of multiple effective metrics for distinct excitations; (ii) the equivalence principle and the spin-2 uniqueness requirement (Feynman/Weinberg soft-graviton theorem); (iii) gravitational self-coupling and the Kraichnan argument; (iv) diffeomorphism invariance in both its roles (background independence and projection onto spin-2); (v) the van Dam-Veltman-Zakharov discontinuity for massive or approximately invariant models; and (vi) the Weinberg-Witten theorem constraining composite gravitons in Lorentz-invariant theories.

## Key results
The paper establishes that each of the following constitutes a serious, largely unresolved obstacle for emergent gravity models. (1) Unless the background environment is itself Lorentz invariant, distinct excitation modes generically acquire distinct effective light cones, yielding Finsler rather than Lorentzian geometry; recovering a single speed of light typically requires fine tuning or an ad hoc discrete symmetry. (2) The equivalence principle demands a single massless spin-2 field coupling universally to all matter; composite graviton proposals generically produce multiple candidates and must impose extra symmetry to lift the surplus to Planck-scale masses. (3) Gravitational self-coupling — gravity's coupling to its own energy at the same universal strength — requires nonlinear consistency beyond the linearized approximation. (4) The Weinberg-Witten theorem rules out a massless spin-2 particle carrying nonzero charge under a Lorentz-covariant conserved stress-energy tensor; type I models must therefore either break Lorentz invariance at short scales, invoke nonlocality, ensure no spin-2 fields exist below the emergence scale, or rely on the gauge-invariance loophole. (5) An approximate diffeomorphism invariance is qualitatively different from exact invariance: a small graviton mass leads to the vDVZ discontinuity and Boulware-Deser ghosts that require very special actions to avoid. (6) Type II models face the prior challenge of demonstrating that a smooth, diffeomorphism-invariant spacetime emerges at all before any of the above applies.

## Relevance to this research
This paper is peripherally relevant to the participatory/it-from-bit strand of the research program. Wheeler's "it from bit" is explicitly catalogued as a type II emergent-gravity model, and Carlip's critique of it — that recovering Lorentz invariance, the equivalence principle, and diffeomorphism invariance from purely informational or combinatorial degrees of freedom remains an open and extremely difficult problem — provides a useful calibration for participatory-realist interpretations that invoke quantum information as ontologically primary. For the VFE transformer research specifically, the paper is background context rather than a technical input: the gauge structure in GL(K) attention is an internal information-geometric gauge (acting on belief-state representations), not a spacetime gauge, so the Weinberg-Witten and EPS constraints analyzed here do not directly constrain that framework. However, Carlip's discussion of how approximate symmetries (e.g., near-diffeomorphism invariance) can be qualitatively misleading resonates with the project's insistence on maintaining exact gauge equivariance rather than accepting it only perturbatively.
