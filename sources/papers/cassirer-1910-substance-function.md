# Substanzbegriff und Funktionsbegriff (Substance and Function)

> [!info] Citation
> Cassirer, Ernst (1910). *Substanzbegriff und Funktionsbegriff: Untersuchungen über die Grundfragen der Erkenntniskritik*. Berlin: Bruno Cassirer. English translation: *Substance and Function and Einstein's Theory of Relativity*, trans. W. C. Swabey and M. C. Swabey (Chicago: Open Court, 1923).

## TL;DR

Cassirer's foundational neo-Kantian treatise argues that the basic concepts of modern physics and mathematics are not abstractions of shared substantial properties from particulars (the "substance" or *Substanzbegriff* tradition running from Aristotle through empiricist abstraction theory) but *functional* concepts (*Funktionsbegriff*): laws of serial order, relations, and functional dependencies that constitute their objects rather than being read off from them. Objectivity, on this view, is not correspondence to a thing-in-itself but invariance under the system of functional relations — a position that anticipates, almost verbatim, the modern reading of physical objectivity as gauge-invariance. It is the single most load-bearing uncited philosophy-of-science source standing behind the manuscript's invariance-as-objectivity move.

## Problem & setting

The book targets the empiricist theory of concept formation, on which a general concept (e.g. "metal," "mass") is obtained by noticing the common marks of a class of objects and discarding the rest. Cassirer argues this picture cannot account for the concepts that actually do scientific work — number, point, energy, function — which are introduced as relational structures and ordering principles, not as residues of shared sensible content. The polemical target is the metaphysics of substance: the idea that knowledge bottoms out in self-subsistent things bearing intrinsic properties.

## Method

Cassirer proceeds by conceptual-historical analysis across mathematics (the concept of number, the development of the function concept, projective geometry's treatment of points as intersections of relations), the exact sciences (the constitution of physical magnitudes as systems of measurement), and chemistry. In each case he shows that the object is fixed by its position in a relational system: a point is determined by the relations it enters, a magnitude by its place in a measurement structure, a physical quantity by the invariants of the relevant transformation group. The result is a constructive, Kant-inflected epistemology in which the *a priori* is the form of serial order the mind imposes to make objective experience possible — but a form that is historically revisable rather than fixed.

## Key results

- **Function over substance.** Scientific concepts are functional/relational, not abstractive; the relata are constituted by the relations, not vice versa. This is the historical seed of ontic structural realism (see [[french-ladyman-2003-remodelling]], [[Structural realism]]).
- **Objectivity as invariance.** What is objective in a domain is what stays invariant across the admissible transformations of the descriptive framework — the conceptual ancestor of the slogan "the objective content of a theory is its gauge-invariant content."
- **Constitutive but revisable a priori.** The framework that makes experience objective is presupposed (a priori) yet changes with the growth of science, anticipating the relativized a priori later developed by Reichenbach and Friedman ([[Relativized a priori]]).

## Relevance to this research

The manuscript [[participatory-it-from-bit]] explicitly names "Cassirer's neo-Kantian variant" of structural realism but cites no source for it; this note supplies and grounds that reference. Cassirer is the deepest precedent for the project's identification of *objective content with gauge-invariant content*: where the manuscript treats an agent's belief as a frame-dependent section and the physically meaningful quantities as those invariant under [[Gauge transformation]], Cassirer had already argued that objectivity just is invariance under the admissible transformations of a relational framework. His "function over substance" is the direct conceptual ancestor of [[Structural realism]] (structure prior to relata) and dovetails with modeling [[Agents as fibre-bundle sections]]: an agent has no substance-like intrinsic state, only a position fixed by its relations to other sections and the connection that transports between them. His constitutive-but-revisable a priori prefigures the [[Relativized a priori]] thread and the manuscript's treatment of theoretical frameworks as conventions that can shift under collective revision (the Kuhnian-revolution-as-gauge-transformation move). This places Cassirer alongside [[wheeler-1990-it-from-bit]] and the [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]] sources as the constructive-idealist root of the program's epistemology.
