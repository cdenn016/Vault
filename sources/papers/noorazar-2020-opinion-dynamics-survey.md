# Recent Advances in Opinion Propagation Dynamics: A 2020 Survey

> [!info] Citation
> Noorazar, H., Vixie, K. R., Talebanpour, A., & Hu, Y. (2020). *Recent Advances in Opinion Propagation Dynamics: A 2020 Survey*. The European Physical Journal Plus 135, 521. DOI: [10.1140/epjp/s13360-020-00541-2](https://doi.org/10.1140/epjp/s13360-020-00541-2). arXiv:2004.05286.

## TL;DR
A broad, up-to-date survey that maps the entire opinion-dynamics landscape, organizing both discrete and continuous models into a single taxonomy. It covers the foundational averaging models (DeGroot, Friedkin-Johnsen), the discrete-state imitation and spin models (voter, Sznajd, majority-rule), and the continuous bounded-confidence family (Hegselmann-Krause, Deffuant-Weisbuch), together with their many network-structured and noisy variants. Beyond cataloguing models it surveys the analytical tools, the role of network topology, and emerging application-driven problems such as the spread of misinformation and the effect of stubborn or zealot agents.

## What it establishes
Rather than a single result, the paper's contribution is a structured map. It groups models by their state space (binary or finite discrete versus continuous opinion) and by their update mechanism (full averaging, confidence-gated averaging, pairwise imitation, majority rule, persuasion). It traces how each family handles the central questions of the field — when does the population reach consensus, when does it polarize into a few clusters, and when does it fragment — and how those outcomes shift on heterogeneous and dynamic networks. It also flags the open frontiers the field was turning to circa 2020: the influence of online network structure, the dynamics of fake news and information disorder, control and intervention, and the integration of empirical data with these stylized models.

## Relevance to this research
This is the most current broad survey of the opinion-dynamics field, giving the program a one-stop taxonomy of the discrete and agreement models belief-inertia claims to subsume in its overdamped limit, and a checklist of the collective behaviours (consensus, clustering, fragmentation) a unifying VFE functional should reproduce. Its catalogue of open problems — network effects, stubborn agents, misinformation — frames where the gauge-theoretic VFE framing might add value beyond reproduction. The survey is reference/orientation material rather than machinery the functional itself uses, but it is the directly requested authoritative landscape document.
