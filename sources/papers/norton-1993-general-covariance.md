# General Covariance and the Foundations of General Relativity: Eight Decades of Dispute

> [!info] Citation
> Norton, John D. (1993). "General Covariance and the Foundations of General Relativity: Eight Decades of Dispute." *Reports on Progress in Physics*, 56(7), 791–858. https://doi.org/10.1088/0034-4885/56/7/001

## TL;DR
Norton surveys eighty years of controversy over the meaning and status of general covariance in general relativity, from Einstein's original formulation through the Kretschmann objection to modern debates about background independence. The paper defends a substantive reading of general covariance — not merely a notational or formal freedom — grounded in the concept of background independence: a theory is genuinely generally covariant when it contains no fixed, non-dynamical spacetime structures. The review also clarifies the relationship between general covariance and gauge invariance proper, showing these are distinct concepts that have frequently been conflated.

## Problem & setting
Einstein elevated general covariance to a foundational principle of general relativity, but Kretschmann (1917) objected that any theory can be cast in generally covariant form by introducing auxiliary variables, making the principle a mere notational requirement with no physical content. This "deflationary" horn — general covariance as a trivially satisfiable formal property — competed throughout the twentieth century with "substantive" readings that sought to recover genuine physical content. The paper surveys this dispute across its full historical sweep, covering Einstein's hole argument, the Kretschmann objection, Cartan's geometric reformulation, and the emergence of modern background-independence criteria.

## Method
The paper is a historical and conceptual analysis, not a new mathematical derivation. Norton proceeds by systematic review of primary sources and reconstructs the evolving positions of Einstein, Kretschmann, Hilbert, Weyl, Cartan, and later commentators. He distinguishes carefully between (a) formal general covariance — the property of being expressible in coordinate-free or covariant notation, which any theory can achieve — and (b) substantive general covariance — the absence of fixed background spacetime structures, a property that genuinely distinguishes general relativity from Newtonian gravity and special relativity. The hole argument is analyzed as demonstrating that general covariance (in sense (a)) does not by itself determine spacetime points as physically individuated entities.

## Key results
The central finding is that Kretschmann's deflationary objection applies to formal general covariance but not to the substantive, background-independence reading. Under the substantive reading, general relativity is genuinely distinguished from theories like Newtonian mechanics written in arbitrary coordinates because GR contains no non-dynamical geometrical objects. Norton also establishes that general covariance and gauge invariance — while related through diffeomorphism freedom — are conceptually distinct: gauge invariance in the physicist's sense involves redundancy of description for a fixed background, whereas general covariance in the substantive sense denies the existence of such a background. The paper concludes that Einstein's principle, properly interpreted as background independence, retains genuine physical content.
