# Using Category Theory to Assess the Relationship Between Consciousness and Integrated Information Theory

> [!info] Citation
> Tsuchiya, N., Taguchi, S., & Saigo, H. (2016). "Using category theory to assess the relationship between consciousness and integrated information theory." *Neuroscience Research*, 107, 1–7. DOI: [10.1016/j.neures.2015.12.007](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neures.2015.12.007).

## TL;DR

The authors propose **category theory** — specifically the language of objects, morphisms, functors, and the Yoneda lemma — as the right mathematical tool for relating phenomenal experience to Integrated Information Theory's formal structures. The central methodological claim is that what fixes a quale is not any intrinsic, standalone property but the *relationships* it bears to all other qualia. Category theory, which characterizes objects entirely by their morphisms (their pattern of relations), is therefore the natural formalism for a relational/structural theory of consciousness.

## Problem & setting

IIT identifies experience with a cause–effect structure, but the mapping between that mathematical structure and the felt quality of experience is left informal. Tsuchiya and colleagues ask what mathematical discipline could make "two experiences are similar in such-and-such a way" precise. They argue that ordinary set-theoretic or feature-list approaches mischaracterize qualia, because a quale's character is exhausted by how it stands to other qualia (red relative to other colors, this pain relative to other pains), not by an isolable internal essence.

## Method

The paper imports the categorical viewpoint: model qualia as objects in a category whose morphisms encode experiential relationships (similarity, contrast, transformation). The **Yoneda lemma** is the key lever — it states that an object is determined up to isomorphism by the totality of morphisms into (or out of) it, i.e. by its complete relational profile. Applied to consciousness, this licenses the claim that fixing all relations fixes the phenomenal character. The authors sketch how IIT's cause–effect structures might be recast functorially so that the structure-to-experience map becomes a structure-preserving correspondence rather than a stipulation.

## Key results

The contribution is programmatic: a proposal for a category-theoretic bridge between IIT's formalism and phenomenology, with the Yoneda principle as the formal embodiment of phenomenal structuralism (an object's identity is its web of relations). It opens a research line — later developed in the categorical-consciousness literature — toward proving, rather than asserting, that relational structure determines experiential quality.
