# Experimental and Theoretical Approaches to Conscious Processing

> [!info] Citation
> Dehaene, S. & Changeux, J.-P. (2011). "Experimental and Theoretical Approaches to Conscious Processing." *Neuron*, 70(2), 200–227. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2011.03.018

## TL;DR
This review synthesizes neuroimaging, electrophysiology, and single-cell recording studies to identify the neural correlates of conscious access — the transition from nonconscious to reportable subjective experience. The authors then compare these signatures against major theories of consciousness and describe in detail the Global Neuronal Workspace (GNW) model, in which conscious access occurs via "ignition": a self-amplifying, reverberating, distributed cell assembly spanning prefrontal and parieto-temporal cortices that broadcasts a single coherent representation brain-wide. Clinical implications for anesthesia, coma, vegetative state, and schizophrenia are discussed.

## Problem & setting
How does information cross the threshold from nonconscious processing to conscious reportability? Prior work demonstrated rich subliminal processing (priming, motor biasing, semantic extraction) yet clear behavioral and neural limits to what nonconscious stimuli can achieve. The paper asks what distinguishes the neural fate of stimuli that are consciously perceived versus those that remain subliminal or preconscious, using minimal stimulus-level contrasts: masking, attentional blink, psychological refractory period, inattentional blindness, and threshold detection.

## Method
The paper is a review organized around three convergent methodologies: (1) behavioral and neuroimaging (fMRI) studies establishing whole-brain signatures; (2) time-resolved EEG/MEG and intracranial depth recording (iEEG) in epileptic patients providing temporal and spatial precision; and (3) explicit computational simulations of the GNW architecture using spiking-neuron networks with realistic membrane dynamics, layered cortical columns, and AMPA/NMDA-differentiated feedforward vs. feedback pathways. The GNW model proposes that a subset of cortical pyramidal neurons with long-range axons (dense in prefrontal, cingulate, and parietal regions) forms a "neuronal workspace" that, once ignited, sustains a self-reinforcing assembly and broadcasts the winning representation globally.

## Key results
Converging evidence across paradigms and modalities identifies four consistent neural signatures of conscious access, all appearing late (>300 ms) and simultaneously:
1. Late, broadly distributed positive ERP component (P3b), generated by distributed prefrontal, parietal, and hippocampal sources.
2. Large increases in high-gamma (50–100 Hz) local-field potential power and long-distance beta-band (13–30 Hz) phase synchrony.
3. "Ignition": a sudden, all-or-none, nonlinear amplification of activity across a prefronto-parietal network — absent for subliminal stimuli despite similar early sensory responses up to ~200 ms.
4. Increased bidirectional but bottom-up-dominant Granger causal flow from sensory to frontal areas.

GNW simulations reproduce these signatures: weak stimuli produce only a feedforward wave that dies out in higher areas, while threshold-crossing stimuli trigger NMDA-mediated reverberating ignition, yielding distributed sustained activity, gamma-band oscillations, and the P3b-like slow cortical potential. Spontaneous GNW ignitions (without external input) explain mind-wandering and interference with incoming stimuli (attentional blink). MS patients with white-matter damage show elevated masking thresholds correlating with structural damage in the dorsolateral prefrontal white matter and occipito-frontal fasciculus, providing causal support.

## Relevance to this research
The GNW ignition dynamic — a stochastic phase transition in a recurrent long-range network where information is "globally broadcast" once a threshold is crossed — is a candidate architectural analogy for how VFE minimization in the gauge-equivariant transformer achieves a form of "global availability": the belief-coupling term $\sum_{ij} \beta_{ij} \mathrm{KL}(q_i \| \Omega_{ij} q_j)$ plays the role of inter-area reverberant connections, and the softmax attention distribution $\beta_{ij}$ is the analog of GNW's selective ignition (one coherent representation selected, the rest suppressed). The serial bottleneck and all-or-none character of conscious ignition maps onto the competitive dynamics of VFE minimization where a single low-VFE configuration wins. The GNW's distinction between parallel feedforward (nonconscious) and reverberant global (conscious) processing parallels the VFE distinction between local (within-layer) belief updates and long-range (cross-layer) gauge-transported coupling.
