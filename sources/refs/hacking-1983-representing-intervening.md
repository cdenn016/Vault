# Representing and Intervening: Introductory Topics in the Philosophy of Natural Science

> [!info] Citation
> Hacking, Ian (1983). *Representing and Intervening: Introductory Topics in the Philosophy of Natural Science*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press. ISBN: 9780521282468.

## TL;DR

Hacking's book splits the philosophy of science into two halves — *representing* (theory, realism, reference, incommensurability) and *intervening* (experiment, observation, measurement, instrumentation) — and argues that twentieth-century philosophy of science overweighted the first and neglected the second. Its signature move is *entity realism*: we are warranted in believing in the unobservable entities (electrons, positrons) that we can reliably *manipulate* to interfere with other parts of nature — "if you can spray them, they are real" — even while remaining agnostic about the high-level theories that describe them. Along the way it gives a careful, deflationary treatment of the *theory-ladenness of observation*, distinguishing the strong thesis (all seeing is theoretical) from the weaker, defensible claim that observation reports are saturated with prior commitment, and it makes experiment "have a life of its own" independent of theory-testing.

## What it establishes

The book's first half surveys and tempers the representational debates of its era: scientific realism versus anti-realism, the incommensurability and theory-ladenness arguments of Kuhn and Feyerabend, reference and meaning across theory change, and the underdetermination of theory by data. Hacking's verdict there is cautious — the arguments *about representation* leave the realism question stubbornly undecidable, because they turn on how words hook onto the world. The decisive turn comes in the second half, on *intervening*: he argues that the realism question is settled not at the level of theory but at the level of the laboratory. When an entity is used as a *tool* — when experimenters routinely build apparatus that exploits its causal powers to investigate something else — the case for its reality no longer rests on inference to the best explanation but on engineering practice. This is *entity realism*: belief in entities, detached from belief in the theories ostensibly about them. The companion thesis is the autonomy of experiment: experimental knowledge is not merely theory's handmaiden but has its own creation, observation, and self-vindication ("the creation of phenomena"), and observation itself is a skill, more theory-laden in its *reports* than in its *practice*.

## Why the project cites it

This note enters the wiki for the program's [[Gauge-Theoretic Multi-Agent VFE Model]] through its philosophy-of-science panel, where Hacking supplies two load-bearing arguments under the [[Structural realism and philosophy of science]] theme. The first is the *theory-ladenness of observation*: in the multi-agent VFE setting each agent's "observation" is not raw data but a likelihood read through that agent's own beliefs, priors, and gauge frame ([[Agents as fibre-bundle sections]]), so what an agent sees is constituted by what it already holds. Hacking's careful separation of the strong (untenable) from the weak (defensible) theory-ladenness thesis is exactly the calibration the debate panels need when they invoke *confirmation bias* — an agent whose prior shapes its evidence is the program's formal echo of theory-laden observation, and the philosophy-of-science lens cites Hacking to keep that claim disciplined rather than sweeping. The second is *experimental realism / intervention*: Hacking's "you believe in what you can manipulate" criterion gives the panels a non-representational standard of reality that pairs naturally with the program's active, interventionist reading of inference — beliefs earn their warrant by being acted on (used to predict, transport, and re-gauge), not merely by representing. This is the realist counterweight the panels set against the anti-realism of [[vanfraassen-1980-scientific-image]] and against the *structural* realism of [[worrall-1989-structural-realism]]: where Worrall locates the durable content of a theory in its mathematical structure, Hacking locates it in manipulable entities, and the program's [[Structural realism]] commitments are sharpened by holding the two in tension.

> [!note] Editorial:
> The mapping from Hacking's "theory-ladenness" to the VFE model's prior-conditioned likelihood, and from his manipulation criterion to the model's interventionist use of beliefs, is an interpretive bridge drawn by the project's debate panels — it is an analogy the program leans on, not a claim Hacking makes about probabilistic inference. Hacking's entity realism is also in some tension with the program's broadly *structural*-realist leanings (entities vs. structure as the locus of the real); the panels use that tension productively rather than resolving it.
