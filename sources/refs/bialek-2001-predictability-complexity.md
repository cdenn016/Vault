# Predictability, Complexity, and Learning

> [!info] Citation
> William Bialek, Ilya Nemenman, and Naftali Tishby (2001). "Predictability, Complexity, and Learning." *Neural Computation* 13(11): 2409–2463. DOI: [10.1162/089976601753195969](https://doi.org/10.1162/089976601753195969). Preprint: arXiv:physics/0007070.

## TL;DR

This paper defines the **predictive information** — the mutual information between the past and the future of a time series, $I_{\mathrm{pred}}(T) = I(\text{past},\, \text{future of length } T)$ — and argues it is the right universal measure of a signal's complexity. The point is its *growth rate*: predictive information grows at most sub-extensively (slower than linearly) in the observation window, and the sub-extensive part measures how much there is to learn. For data generated by a model with $K$ continuous parameters, the predictive information grows like $\tfrac{K}{2}\log T$, tying complexity directly to the number of parameters being learned.

## What it establishes

- Predictive information as a reparameterization-invariant complexity measure, with extensive and sub-extensive components.
- The sub-extensive growth law $\sim \tfrac{K}{2}\log T$ for $K$-parameter model classes, recovering MDL/stochastic-complexity counting from an information-theoretic argument.
- A classification of learning problems by how their predictive information diverges, separating learnable from unlearnable structure.

## Why the project cites it

PIFB's ([[participatory-it-from-bit]]) generative story is the hierarchy $h \to s \to p \to q \to \text{observations}$, in which higher levels supply the priors and hyper-priors that lower levels predict. Predictive information is the natural currency for that hierarchy: each level exists to capture the sub-extensive, learnable structure that lets it predict the level below, and the cross-scale shadow priors are precisely a mechanism for passing predictive information downward. The $\tfrac{K}{2}\log T$ counting law connects the manuscript's capacity story to its belief dimension $K$ — the same $K$ that appears in the inverse-$K$ scaling claim — giving an information-theoretic reading of why representational capacity should scale with the number of belief parameters. This ties the multi-scale tower of [[Meta-agents and hierarchical emergence]] to the [[Information bottleneck]] lineage (the authors are the IB authors), and to [[Renormalization-group flow of beliefs]], where coarse-graining discards extensive detail and keeps the sub-extensive predictive part.
