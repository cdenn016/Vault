# Mathematical Methods of Classical Mechanics

> [!info] Citation
> V. I. Arnold (1989). *Mathematical Methods of Classical Mechanics*, 2nd edition. Graduate Texts in Mathematics, Vol. 60. Springer, New York. ISBN 978-0-387-96890-2. DOI: 10.1007/978-1-4757-2063-1.

## TL;DR

The canonical text on the geometric (symplectic) formulation of classical mechanics: Lagrangian mechanics and variational principles, Legendre transform, Hamiltonian mechanics on symplectic manifolds, Poisson brackets, canonical transformations, integrable systems and action-angle variables, and mechanics on Lie groups (the rigid body as geodesic flow on $SO(3)$). It is the reference for treating dynamics as flow on a manifold generated by an energy function.

## What it establishes

- The symplectic-geometry foundations of Hamiltonian dynamics: phase space as a symplectic manifold, Hamilton's equations as a vector field $X_H$ defined by $\iota_{X_H}\omega = dH$, and the Poisson-bracket algebra of observables.
- The Euler–Lagrange / Hamiltonian correspondence via the Legendre transform, Noether's theorem linking symmetries to conserved momenta, and the reduction of systems with symmetry.
- Mechanics on Lie groups and homogeneous spaces, where motion is geodesic flow of a left- or right-invariant metric (the Euler–Arnold equations).

## Why the project cites it

Where the program models belief dynamics as a *Hamiltonian* flow — momentum/inertia terms on the belief manifold, energy-conserving updates, and the [[Hamiltonian belief dynamics]] picture in which beliefs and their conjugate momenta evolve by Hamilton's equations on the $GL(K)$/SPD phase space — Arnold is the geometric-mechanics reference. The Euler–Arnold view of motion on a Lie group as geodesic flow of an invariant metric connects directly to the frame fiber's evolution under the chosen `omega_metric` (cf. [[milnor-1976-left-invariant-metrics]] on which invariant metrics exist), and Noether's symmetry–conservation correspondence is the classical analog of the gauge-invariance the program builds in. Cited as the foundational source for the symplectic/Hamiltonian formalism behind belief-inertia and momentum-augmented dynamics, supporting [[participatory-it-from-bit]].

> [!note] Editorial: Cited for the geometric-mechanics framework, not a specific result; the correspondence to belief dynamics is interpretive (treating the inference flow as a Hamiltonian/geodesic flow on a manifold).
