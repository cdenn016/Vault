# Computational Capacity of the Universe

> [!info] Citation
> Seth Lloyd (2002). "Computational capacity of the universe." *Physical Review Letters* 88(23): 237901. DOI: [10.1103/PhysRevLett.88.237901](https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevLett.88.237901). Preprint: [arXiv:quant-ph/0110141](https://arxiv.org/abs/quant-ph/0110141).

## TL;DR

Treating every physical system as registering and processing information, Lloyd computes hard upper bounds on the total computation the observable universe could have performed over its history: of order $10^{120}$ elementary logical operations on roughly $10^{90}$ bits (or $10^{120}$ bits if gravitational degrees of freedom are counted). The bounds follow from the Margolus–Levitin limit (energy bounds operation rate) and from holographic/entropy bounds on information storage. The universe, in this reading, is a physical computer of finite capacity — the most literal form of "it from bit."

## What it establishes

- Physically grounded limits on the universe's information storage and processing, derived from energy, time, and holographic entropy bounds.
- A quantitative version of the computational-universe thesis: physics *is* information processing, with bounded resources.
- The information-capacity counterpart to 't Hooft–Bousso holographic bounds, framed operationally as computation rather than entropy.
