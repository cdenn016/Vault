# The Logic of Scientific Discovery

> [!info] Citation
> Popper, Karl (1959). *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*. London: Hutchinson. English translation (by the author) of *Logik der Forschung* (Vienna: Springer, 1934, imprint dated 1935).

## TL;DR

Popper rejects induction as the logic of science and proposes *falsifiability* as the criterion that demarcates empirical science from metaphysics, pseudoscience, and pure mathematics. A theory is scientific only insofar as it *forbids* something — it must rule out at least one possible observation (a "potential falsifier"), so that it could in principle be refuted by experience. Confirmations are cheap and easy to find for almost any theory; what counts is surviving severe attempts at refutation. Theories are never verified, only *corroborated* (provisionally accepted after passing risky tests) or refuted, and knowledge grows by bold conjecture followed by ruthless elimination of error. Popper also defends realism about the aim of science and treats theories as genuine claims about the world rather than mere instruments.

## What it establishes

- **The demarcation criterion.** Falsifiability, not verifiability or inductive confirmation, separates empirical science from non-science. A statement that is compatible with every conceivable observation says nothing empirical.
- **Asymmetry of verification and falsification.** No finite body of observations can verify a universal law, but a single genuine counter-instance can refute it; science exploits this logical asymmetry (a modus-tollens engine) rather than seeking inductive support.
- **Potential falsifiers and empirical content.** A theory's empirical content is the class of observation-statements it excludes. The more a theory forbids, the higher its content, its testability, and its informativeness — so Popper inverts the usual preference, favouring improbable, high-content conjectures.
- **Corroboration, not confirmation.** Passing severe tests *corroborates* a theory (a report on its past performance) without raising its probability of being true; corroboration is explicitly not a measure of inductive support.
- **Conventional element in basic statements.** Even "basic" observation statements are accepted by decision and remain revisable; there is no incorrigible empirical bedrock, which blocks a naive foundationalism while preserving objective testability.

> [!note] Editorial:
> The 1959 English edition is the standard cited text, but the substantive content dates to the 1934/35 *Logik der Forschung*; the demarcation-by-falsifiability thesis is therefore Popper's, predating the post-positivist debate it later anchored. The naive single-refutation picture is softened by later authors — Lakatos's research-programme model and Kuhn's paradigm dynamics both argue that lone anomalies rarely sink a theory in practice — a tension this program's philosophy panel weighs rather than resolves here.

## Why the project cites it

Popper is the canonical source for the principle that *a scientific claim must forbid something*, and the program invokes it as the standard for its own pre-registered falsifiers. The gauge-theoretic VFE work makes empirical and structural commitments — that beliefs transport under a genuine [[Gauge transformation]] (equivariance that breaks measurably when learned connection or mixer toggles drift from identity), that invariant relational structure is what is conserved across frames ([[Structural realism]]) — and Popperian discipline demands these be stated as risky, refutable predictions with named potential falsifiers, not as post-hoc confirmations. The adversarial debate panels operationalize exactly this: the red-team's job is to construct potential falsifiers and subject load-bearing identities to severe tests, so that surviving claims are *corroborated* in Popper's sense rather than merely illustrated by cherry-picked confirmations. Within the program's [[Structural realism and philosophy of science]] theme, Popper sits at the strict-falsificationist end of the lineage: [[kuhn-1962-structure|Kuhn 1962]] and the Lakatosian research-programme view temper the naive one-refutation picture, while structural realism ([[worrall-1989-structural-realism|Worrall 1989]], [[ladyman-ross-2007-every-thing-must-go]]) and the social-epistemology corrective in [[longino-2002-fate-of-knowledge|Longino 2002]] supply what survives and who adjudicates the surviving. The gauge-theory audit lens and the philosophy-of-science debate expert cite this entry as the demarcation backstop against which the program's empirical claims are held.
