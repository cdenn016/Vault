# Steps to an Ecology of Mind

> [!info] Citation
> Bateson, G. (1972). *Steps to an Ecology of Mind*. New York: Ballantine Books. (Collected essays; cybernetics, systems theory, and the epistemology of mind.)

## TL;DR

Bateson's collected essays defining information cybernetically as **"a difference that makes a difference"** — a unit of information is not a thing but a distinction that produces an effect somewhere in a system. This relational, effect-defined notion of information is a direct philosophical ancestor of the "it from bit" ontology, locating meaning in differences and relations rather than in substances.

## What it establishes

Across essays on cybernetics, learning, and ecology, Bateson argues that mind, information, and meaning are properties of *patterns of difference* propagating through systems, not of isolated objects. His slogan — information is "a difference that makes a difference" — frames the elementary informational act as a registered distinction with downstream consequence, an explicitly relational and observer-involving conception of what counts as a bit.

## Why the project cites it

[[participatory-it-from-bit]] invokes Bateson for the **information ontology behind "it from bit"**: the manuscript's participatory thesis — that physical and belief structure is built from registered distinctions among agents — descends from Bateson's claim that information is constituted by difference-that-makes-a-difference. In the project this becomes the relational reading of belief coupling, where what matters is the *difference* between an agent's belief and a (transported) neighbor's, measured by $\mathrm{KL}(q_i \| \Omega_{ij} q_j)$ in [[Multi-agent variational free energy]]: a difference that makes a difference to the free energy and hence to the dynamics. Bateson is the cybernetics-side companion to [[wheeler-1990-it-from-bit]], supplying the philosophical lineage for treating the bit — the registered distinction — as ontologically prior.
