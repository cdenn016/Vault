# The Fate of Knowledge

> [!info] Citation
> Longino, Helen E. (2002). *The Fate of Knowledge*. Princeton, NJ: Princeton University Press.

## TL;DR

Longino's central work in social epistemology argues that scientific knowledge is irreducibly *social*: objectivity is not the achievement of an individual mind purged of bias but a property of *communities* that subject claims to transformative critical discourse under shared norms (venues for criticism, uptake of criticism, public standards, tempered equality of intellectual authority). Knowledge is constituted by the critical interaction of many situated knowers, dissolving the dichotomy between the "rational" and the "social" in science.

## What it establishes

- **Social objectivity.** Objectivity is a feature of communities, secured by effective critical interaction, not of isolated rational agents; bias is filtered intersubjectively.
- **Four norms for critical communities.** Public venues, uptake of criticism, publicly recognized standards, and tempered equality of intellectual authority are the conditions under which collective inquiry yields knowledge.
- **Rational/social rapprochement.** The social character of science is not a defect to be quarantined but the very mechanism of its objectivity.
