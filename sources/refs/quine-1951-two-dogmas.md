# Two Dogmas of Empiricism

> [!info] Citation
> Quine, W. V. (1951). "Two Dogmas of Empiricism." *The Philosophical Review* **60**(1): 20–43. DOI: [10.2307/2181906](https://doi.org/10.2307/2181906).

## TL;DR

Quine attacks two pillars of logical empiricism: the *analytic/synthetic distinction* (the idea that some truths hold by meaning alone, independent of fact) and *reductionism* (that each meaningful statement is equivalent to a logical construction from sense-experience). Rejecting both yields *confirmational holism*: our beliefs face the "tribunal of sense experience" not individually but only as a corporate body — a "web of belief" where no statement is immune to revision and any can be held true if we adjust enough elsewhere. This underwrites the *underdetermination* of theory by evidence.

## What it establishes

- **Collapse of analytic/synthetic.** No principled line between truths of meaning and truths of fact; all statements are in principle empirically revisable.
- **Web of belief / holism.** Statements confront experience only collectively; the "field of force" can accommodate a recalcitrant experience by revising any of many beliefs.
- **Underdetermination.** Multiple, logically incompatible total theories can fit the same evidence; experience does not uniquely fix theory (developed further in his 1975 *On Empirically Equivalent Systems of the World*).

## Why the project cites it

Quine's web-of-belief and underdetermination theses are exactly what the manuscript proposes to *formalize* in its multi-agent variational setting. The program represents an agent's commitments as a coupled belief structure updated globally under [[Variational free energy]] minimization — a quantitative "field of force" in which observations adjust the whole web rather than isolated propositions. Underdetermination becomes the existence of many belief configurations (gauge-related or otherwise) compatible with the same data, which is precisely the no-unique-true-description backbone of [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]] shared with [[putnam-1981-reason-truth-history|Putnam 1981]] and [[vanfraassen-1980-scientific-image|van Fraassen 1980]]. The holistic revisability also meshes with the [[Relativized a priori]] / paradigm-shift picture ([[reichenbach-1920-relativity-apriori]], [[kuhn-1962-structure]]): no constitutive principle is absolutely fixed. Quine 1975 ("On empirically equivalent systems of the world," *Erkenntnis* 9:313–328, DOI 10.1007/BF00178004) sharpens the underdetermination thesis and may be cited alongside.
