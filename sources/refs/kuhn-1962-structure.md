# The Structure of Scientific Revolutions

> [!info] Citation
> Kuhn, Thomas S. (1962). *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*. Chicago: University of Chicago Press. (2nd ed. 1970 with "Postscript"; 50th-anniversary ed. 2012.)

## TL;DR

Kuhn's account of scientific change replaces the cumulative-progress picture with a cycle of *normal science* (puzzle-solving within an accepted *paradigm*), *crisis* (accumulation of anomalies the paradigm cannot absorb), and *revolution* (a discontinuous shift to a new paradigm). Competing paradigms are *incommensurable*: they carry different problems, standards, and even meanings for shared terms, so the choice between them is not settled by neutral algorithm. After a revolution, practitioners in some sense "work in a different world."

## What it establishes

- **Paradigms and normal science.** Mature science proceeds within a shared framework that fixes problems, methods, and exemplars; most science is puzzle-solving inside it.
- **Anomaly, crisis, revolution.** Persistent anomalies trigger crisis and eventual paradigm replacement — a discontinuous, non-cumulative shift.
- **Incommensurability.** Successive paradigms lack a common measure; observation is theory-laden and meanings shift across the divide, so there is no neutral observation language to adjudicate.
- **Gestalt-switch character of revolutions.** The world the scientist sees changes with the paradigm — a constitutive, framework-relative view of scientific perception.

## Why the project cites it

The manuscript's subsection "Kuhnian revolutions as collective gauge transformations" rests directly on this book. The program's central analogy: a *paradigm* is a shared frame/gauge fixing the meaning of agents' beliefs, normal science is belief-updating *within* a fixed connection, and a *revolution* is a collective, discontinuous re-gauging — a global [[Gauge transformation]] across the community of [[Agents as fibre-bundle sections]] that resets which quantities are coordinated to experience. Incommensurability becomes the absence of a single privileged frame relating pre- and post-revolution descriptions, the philosophy-of-science face of the project's no-view-from-nowhere commitment in [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]]. Kuhn's framework-relativity also instantiates the [[Relativized a priori]] ([[reichenbach-1920-relativity-apriori]], [[friedman-2001-dynamics-of-reason]]): paradigms supply constitutive principles that shift across revolutions.
