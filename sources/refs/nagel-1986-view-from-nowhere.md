# The View from Nowhere

> [!info] Citation
> Nagel, Thomas (1986). *The View from Nowhere*. New York: Oxford University Press.

## TL;DR

Nagel examines the tension between the *subjective* (the world as it appears from a particular point of view) and the *objective* (the attempt to conceive the world from no particular standpoint — the "view from nowhere"). He argues that objectivity is a *matter of degree*, achieved by progressively detaching from one's own perspective, but that the project of a complete view from nowhere is unattainable: some facts (notably consciousness and the first-person) are essentially perspectival and cannot be captured by any fully external description. The subjective standpoint is ineliminable.

## What it establishes

- **Objectivity as detachment, by degrees.** We become more objective by transcending our particular viewpoint, but never reach a standpoint-free totality.
- **Limits of the view from nowhere.** A fully objective, perspective-free conception of reality is in principle incomplete; some facts are irreducibly first-personal.
- **Ineliminable subjectivity.** Any complete account of the world must accommodate, not abolish, the situated perspective.

## Why the project cites it

Nagel furnishes the manuscript's headline epistemic-limits claim: there is *no view from nowhere*. The program builds this directly into its formalism — there is no privileged, frame-independent description of an agent's beliefs; every account is from some local frame, and objectivity is approached only by what is invariant across frames under [[Gauge transformation]], never by occupying a standpoint outside all of them. This is the conceptual heart of [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]] and dovetails with [[Putnam 1981|putnam-1981-reason-truth-history]]'s "no God's-eye view," [[Longino 2002|longino-2002-fate-of-knowledge]]'s social objectivity, and the constitutive-perspective lineage from [[Kant 1781|kant-1781-critique-pure-reason]]. Nagel also frames the subjective/objective gap that [[Sellars 1962|sellars-1962-philosophy-scientific-image]]'s manifest/scientific-image problem expresses and that the manuscript's gauge geometry attempts to reconcile.
