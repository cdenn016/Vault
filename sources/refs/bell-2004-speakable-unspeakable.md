# Speakable and Unspeakable in Quantum Mechanics

> [!info] Citation
> Bell, J. S. (2004). *Speakable and Unspeakable in Quantum Mechanics: Collected Papers on Quantum Philosophy*, 2nd ed. (with an introduction by Alain Aspect). Cambridge University Press, Cambridge. ISBN 978-0-521-52338-7. (1st ed. 1987.)

## TL;DR

Bell's collected foundational papers, including the derivation of the **Bell inequalities** and the analysis of local hidden variables, the critique of "measurement" as a fundamental notion, and the "beables" program (what a theory says *is*, as opposed to what is merely observable). The volume is the canonical primary source for why local, observer-independent, pre-assigned values cannot reproduce quantum correlations.

## What it establishes

The Bell theorem: no local-hidden-variable theory can match the predictions of quantum mechanics for entangled systems, turning a metaphysical dispute into an experimentally decidable one. Alongside it, Bell's sustained critique of the orthodox "shifty split" between system and apparatus, his insistence that a fundamental theory should be about *beables* rather than observers/observables, and his sympathetic readings of de Broglie–Bohm and spontaneous-collapse alternatives.

## Why the project cites it

Bell is the foundational backdrop for the **observer-dependence** thread the program adopts. The program's participatory stance — objectivity as *transported consensus* among coupled agents rather than a pre-given ledger of facts — lives downstream of Bell: the sharpened Wigner's-friend no-go results the program leans on ([[brukner-2018-no-go-observer-facts|Brukner]], [[frauchiger-renner-2018-no-self-description|Frauchiger–Renner]]) are Bell-type arguments lifted to observers, and the [[Observer-dependent facts and Wigner's friend]] concept page traces that genealogy. Citing Bell fixes the precise content the program must respect: it is the nonexistence of *local, observer-independent assigned values* that forces objectivity to be *built* (from inter-agent relations / nonzero [[Holonomy]]) rather than assumed — the central move of the [[Participatory realism and quantum foundations]] theme. Bell's beables-vs-observables distinction also sharpens the program's [[QBism|QBist]]/[[Psi-epistemic view of the quantum state|epistemic]] commitments by marking exactly what an agent-centered reading gives up.

> [!note] Editorial: Cited as the primary source for the Bell theorem and the observer/measurement critique that the program's participatory and Wigner's-friend strands build on; Bell himself favored beable (de Broglie–Bohm / collapse) realism, a contrast the program notes rather than adopts.
