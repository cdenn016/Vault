# Critique of Pure Reason (Kritik der reinen Vernunft)

> [!info] Citation
> Kant, Immanuel (1781/1787). *Kritik der reinen Vernunft* (A/B editions). Riga: Johann Friedrich Hartknoch. Standard English edition: *Critique of Pure Reason*, trans. and ed. Paul Guyer and Allen W. Wood (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1998).

## TL;DR

Kant's *Critique* mounts the "Copernican revolution" in epistemology: rather than our cognition conforming to objects, objects of experience conform to the constitutive structure of cognition. Space and time are not features of things-in-themselves but *a priori forms of intuition* — the mind's own conditions of any possible perception — and the categories of the understanding (causality, substance, etc.) are the conceptual forms that organize sensibility into objective experience. We can know objects only as *phenomena* (appearances structured by these forms), never the *noumenon* (the thing-in-itself).

## What it establishes

- **Phenomenal/noumenal distinction.** Knowledge is confined to appearances as structured by the knowing subject; the thing-in-itself is in principle inaccessible — there is no cognition of a mind-independent world "as it is in itself."
- **Space and time as forms of intuition.** Space and time are not read off the world but are the a priori sensible forms the subject contributes; geometry and arithmetic are synthetic a priori on this basis.
- **Categories and the transcendental deduction.** Objective experience is possible only because the understanding imposes a priori categories; objectivity is a constitutive achievement, not a given.
- **Copernican turn.** The constitution of the object by the conditions of cognition — the seed of all later constitutive/constructivist epistemology.

## Why the project cites it

Kant is the foundation of the manuscript's epistemology. The [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]] stance of [[participatory-it-from-bit]] is at root Kantian: the agent does not passively read a ready-made world but actively constitutes its phenomenal field through its own forms — formalized in the program as the agent's local frame and belief-generating structure, with the noumenon corresponding to whatever is forever beyond any frame-independent access. Kant's phenomenal/noumenal split is the philosophical ancestor of the project's denial of a view-from-nowhere ([[Nagel 1986|nagel-1986-view-from-nowhere]], [[Putnam 1981|putnam-1981-reason-truth-history]]) and grounds the move from Wheeler's *it from bit* ([[wheeler-1990-it-from-bit]]) to a constitutive, observer-dependent ontology. His "forms of intuition" become, in the relativized hands of Reichenbach and Friedman, the [[Relativized a priori]] ([[reichenbach-1920-relativity-apriori]], [[friedman-2001-dynamics-of-reason]]) that the manuscript reads as framework/gauge conventions; and his constitutive turn is the root of [[Cassirer 1910|cassirer-1910-substance-function]]'s function-over-substance and thereby of [[Structural realism]]. Modeling [[Agents as fibre-bundle sections]] under a [[Gauge transformation]] is, in this lineage, a geometric rendering of the Kantian thesis that objectivity is invariance under the constitutive forms an agent must impose.
