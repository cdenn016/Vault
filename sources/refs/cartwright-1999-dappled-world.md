# The Dappled World: A Study of the Boundaries of Science

> [!info] Citation
> Cartwright, Nancy (1999). *The Dappled World: A Study of the Boundaries of Science*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press. ISBN: 9780521643368.

## TL;DR

Cartwright argues that the world is not governed by a single, universal, exceptionless web of fundamental laws but is *dappled* — a patchwork of domains, each with its own local regularities, gaps, and pockets where no precise law obtains. Where her earlier *How the Laws of Physics Lie* attacked the *truth* of fundamental laws, this book attacks their *dominion*: even granting laws their idealized validity, they hold only inside the carefully engineered situations ("nomological machines") that produce stable, repeatable behaviour, and most of nature is not so arranged. The metaphysical upshot is *local realism about capacities* in place of *fundamentalism about laws*: what is real and exportable across contexts are causal capacities (powers, tendencies), while laws are the parochial, context-bound output of specific arrangements. Models are not approximations to one master theory but the primary, irreducible instruments by which science makes contact with concrete systems, and they are stitched together from disparate theories rather than deduced from a unified base.

## What it establishes

The central thesis is *metaphysical nomological pluralism*: the success of physics licenses belief in a plurality of laws each true in its own domain, not in a single fundamental theory whose writ runs everywhere. Cartwright's mechanism for this is the *nomological machine* — a stable configuration of components with determinate capacities, shielded from interference, whose repeated operation is what *generates* a law-like regularity. Laws, on this reading, are not the deep furniture of the world but the *consequences* of having built (or found) such a machine; outside the machine the regularity simply fails, which is why ceteris-paribus hedges are ineliminable rather than cosmetic. From this she draws *entity/capacity realism over law realism*: capacities are the stable, transportable invariants that survive across the patchwork, and laws are the local readouts that capacities produce only when suitably arranged. A recurring case is the contrast between fundamental physics and a worked science like econometrics, where Cartwright shows that exporting a law beyond the machine that grounds it — assuming the regularity holds where the arrangement does not — is precisely the inferential overreach the patchwork picture forbids. The book thereby separates two realist commitments usually run together: realism about the entities and causal powers in play, and realism about the covering laws, keeping the former while deflating the latter.

## Why the project cites it

This note enters the wiki for the program's [[Gauge-Theoretic Multi-Agent VFE Model]] through its philosophy-of-science panel, under the [[Structural realism and philosophy of science]] theme, where Cartwright supplies the *patchwork* reading of how a model "represents." In the multi-agent VFE setting an agent does not carry a single global theory of the world; it carries a belief tuple (mu, Sigma, phi) and a likelihood read through its own gauge frame ([[Agents as fibre-bundle sections]]), and what counts as a regularity for that agent holds only within the local arrangement — the priors, the coupling structure, the transport that connects it to its neighbours. Cartwright's nomological machine is the natural philosophical gloss on this: the model's stable, law-like behaviour is the output of a particular configuration (a particular regime, a particular set of toggles and couplings), not a context-free truth the model has discovered, and exporting it outside that configuration is unwarranted in exactly Cartwright's sense. Her *capacities-over-laws* move also sharpens the panel's structural-realist commitments: where [[worrall-1989-structural-realism]] locates the durable, theory-change-surviving content of science in mathematical *structure*, and [[hacking-1983-representing-intervening]] locates it in manipulable *entities*, Cartwright locates it in transportable *capacities*, and the program's [[Structural realism]] stance is calibrated by holding these three loci in tension rather than collapsing them. This is the same anti-correspondence, anti-fundamentalist epistemology the panels press against the law-realist reading: the model's success does not certify that it has captured one true global law, only that it has built a serviceable local machine. Cartwright 1999 is the distinct sibling of the project's [[cartwright-1983-laws-physics-lie]] note — the earlier book denies the *truth* of fundamental laws, this one denies their *reach* — and the panels cite both, the gauge-theory and philosophy-of-science audit lenses pairing them to argue that an agent's "laws" are the grammar of its situated belief-updating, not a privileged readout of a mind-independent world.

> [!note] Editorial:
> The mapping from Cartwright's *nomological machine* to the VFE model's regime/configuration-bound regularities, and from her *capacities* to the model's transportable invariants under gauge, is an interpretive bridge drawn by the project's debate panels — an analogy the program leans on, not a claim Cartwright makes about probabilistic inference or gauge theory. Her capacity realism also sits in productive tension with the program's broadly *structural*-realist leanings (capacities/powers vs. structure as the locus of the real); the panels use that tension rather than resolving it.
