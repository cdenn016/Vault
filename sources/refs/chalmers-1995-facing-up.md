# Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness

> [!info] Citation
> Chalmers, David J. (1995). "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness." *Journal of Consciousness Studies*, 2(3), 200–219.

## TL;DR

Chalmers's landmark paper draws the distinction between the **easy problems** of consciousness — explaining functions such as discrimination, integration, reportability, attention, and behavioral control, all in principle tractable by cognitive science — and the **hard problem**: explaining why and how physical processing is accompanied by subjective experience at all. The easy problems concern mechanisms; the hard problem concerns *why there is something it is like* to undergo those mechanisms. No amount of functional explanation, Chalmers argues, settles the hard problem, because one can always coherently ask why the functions are accompanied by experience rather than proceeding "in the dark."

## What it establishes

The paper crystallizes the explanatory situation Nagel and Levine had identified into a single, widely adopted frame. It argues that reductive functional explanation systematically leaves an experiential residue, motivates taking experience as a fundamental feature requiring new bridging principles ("psychophysical laws"), and floats the proposal that information might be doubly aspected (physical and phenomenal) — an early gesture toward the panpsychist and information-based views Chalmers later develops. It sets the agenda: a science of consciousness must explain experience, not merely correlate or replace it.

## What it establishes (continued) — the threshold for any theory

Crucially, Chalmers formulates the hard problem so that *any* candidate theory of consciousness can be tested by whether it speaks to it or merely addresses the easy problems. This makes the paper a benchmark: theories of intrinsic information, predictive processing, or relational structure are all judged by whether they engage the why-experience-at-all question or change the subject.

## Why the project cites it

Chalmers names the exact problem the manuscript [[participatory-it-from-bit]]'s *relocation* claims to answer — or, more carefully, to reposition. PIFB does not assert it solves the hard problem; it argues that within the project's gauge-theoretic, participatory ontology the hard problem is relocated from a brute substrate-to-experience step to a question about frame-relative registered content (see [[Explanatory gap]], [[levine-1983-explanatory-gap]], [[nagel-1974-what-is-it-like]]). Citing Chalmers fixes the target precisely: the manuscript must be measured against the easy/hard distinction, and its honesty depends on conceding that a relocation is not a dissolution. The paper also seeds the panprotopsychist self-classification the project adopts via [[chalmers-2013-panpsychism]], and its information-based gesture links to the [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]] lineage from [[wheeler-1990-it-from-bit]]. Within the emerging [[Mathematical consciousness science]] thread, Chalmers 1995 states the standard the project's formalism (gauge frames, transport, [[Variational free energy]]) is ultimately accountable to.
