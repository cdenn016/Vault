# Challenges for Emergent Gravity

> [!info] Citation
> Steven Carlip (2014). "Challenges for emergent gravity." *Studies in History and Philosophy of Modern Physics* 46: 200–208. DOI: [10.1016/j.shpsb.2012.11.002](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.shpsb.2012.11.002). Preprint: [arXiv:1207.2504](https://arxiv.org/abs/1207.2504).

## TL;DR

Carlip critically reviews the emergent-gravity program and catalogues the obstacles any such model must overcome to reproduce general relativity. The central difficulty is that GR's successes rest on "universal" properties — Lorentz invariance, diffeomorphism invariance, and the equivalence principle — that hold for *all* matter to high precision. An emergent theory whose gravity arises from coarse-grained microscopic degrees of freedom must somehow make these symmetries emerge universally and exactly, which is far from automatic; he argues many emergent-gravity proposals have not shown they can.

## What it establishes

- A clear statement of the universality problem: emergent gravity must reproduce Lorentz/diffeomorphism invariance and the equivalence principle for every species of matter, not just for special probes.
- A critical assessment that thermodynamic and entropic derivations, while suggestive, leave open whether they yield genuine *dynamical* gravity with the right universality, rather than kinematic relations.
- A sober counterweight to the optimism of [[jacobson-1995-thermodynamics-spacetime]], [[verlinde-2011-entropic-gravity]], and related programs.
