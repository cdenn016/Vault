# The Scientific Image

> [!info] Citation
> van Fraassen, Bas C. (1980). *The Scientific Image*. Oxford: Clarendon Press / Oxford University Press. DOI: [10.1093/0198244274.001.0001](https://doi.org/10.1093/0198244274.001.0001).

## TL;DR

The founding statement of *constructive empiricism*: the aim of science is *empirical adequacy* — saving the observable phenomena — not literal truth about unobservable entities. To accept a theory is to believe it is empirically adequate and to adopt it as the framework for one's research, while remaining agnostic about the existence of its theoretical posits. Van Fraassen frames acceptance as a *stance* — a cluster of attitudes, commitments, and values — rather than a belief in the theory's literal truth, decoupling the practice of science from metaphysical realism.

## What it establishes

- **Empirical adequacy over truth.** Science aims to be right about the observable; belief in the unobservable is supererogatory.
- **Acceptance vs. belief.** Accepting a theory commits one to its empirical adequacy and to using it, not to believing its theoretical claims true — a pragmatic, voluntarist epistemology.
- **The empirical stance.** Scientific attitude is a *stance* (values + commitments), not a doctrine; this anti-dogmatic, pragmatic framing of how one holds a theory is the work's lasting methodological contribution.

## Why the project cites it

The manuscript explicitly positions its own attitude as a *stance* "in roughly van Fraassen's sense" — accepting the gauge-theoretic VFE framework as a useful, empirically/operationally adequate lens without claiming it is the one literally true metaphysics. This is the methodological backbone of [[participatory-it-from-bit]]: the program advances [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]] not as dogma but as a stance one adopts, sidestepping the demand to certify a view-from-nowhere truth ([[putnam-1981-reason-truth-history|Putnam 1981]], [[nagel-1986-view-from-nowhere|Nagel 1986]]). Van Fraassen's empirical-adequacy criterion also harmonizes with the project's instrumentalist reading of laws-as-grammar ([[cartwright-1983-laws-physics-lie|Cartwright 1983]]) and with [[Structural realism]]'s modest commitments: one need not reify the formalism's objects to use its structure.
