# Conjectures and Refutations

> [!info] Citation
> Popper, Karl R. (1963). *Conjectures and Refutations: The Growth of Scientific Knowledge*. London: Routledge & Kegan Paul. (Collected essays and lectures, several previously published; 2nd ed. 1965, with later corrected reprintings.)

## TL;DR

This collection of essays develops Popper's positive epistemology of *conjectural knowledge*: science advances not by accumulating confirmations but by boldly conjecturing theories that forbid much, then exposing them to severe attempts at refutation. Knowledge grows through the cycle of *problem — tentative theory — error-elimination — new problem*, and a theory's standing is measured by its *corroboration* — how well it has survived sincere efforts to falsify it — never by its probability or by inductive support. The opening essay sharpens the *demarcation* criterion of [[popper-1959-logic-scientific-discovery|The Logic of Scientific Discovery]]: a theory is scientific to the degree that it is falsifiable, that it sticks its neck out and prohibits observable states of affairs, with Einstein's risky light-bending prediction the exemplar against the unfalsifiable "explains everything" schemes of astrology, Marxist history, and psychoanalysis.

## What it establishes

- **Conjecture-and-refutation as the engine of knowledge growth.** All knowledge, scientific and otherwise, advances by the trial-and-error elimination of bold conjectures; there is no inductive inference *to* theories, only deductive testing of their consequences.
- **Demarcation by falsifiability and bold content.** A good theory forbids more, hence is more testable and more informative; the riskier the prohibition, the more scientific the claim. Irrefutability is a vice, not a virtue.
- **Corroboration, not confirmation or probability.** A theory that survives severe testing is *corroborated*, but corroboration is a report on past performance, not a measure of probable truth; high content and low probability go together.
- **Critique of induction and of justificationism.** Hume's problem is conceded and dissolved: we never justify theories, we only criticize them. Rationality is the readiness to subject conjectures to criticism, not the possession of secure foundations.
- **Fallibilism and verisimilitude.** All theories are conjectural and may be superseded; progress is the replacement of false theories by ones closer to the truth (greater *verisimilitude*), measured by surviving more and more severe tests.

## Why the project cites it

The program's adversarial review and debate machinery is, structurally, a conjecture-and-refutation engine, and this is the canonical source for its positive epistemology. Where [[popper-1959-logic-scientific-discovery|The Logic of Scientific Discovery]] supplies the bare demarcation rule ("a claim must forbid something to be scientific"), *Conjectures and Refutations* supplies the dynamics the project actually runs: a theoretical claim is posed as a bold conjecture, then a panel of expert agents mounts the most severe refutation it can, and what survives is reported as *corroborated* rather than proven. This is exactly the proof-status discipline the peer-review and red/blue-debate procedures enforce — classifying each load-bearing claim as proven, derived, suggested, empirical, or merely conjectured, and never upgrading "survived our attacks" into "established as true." The book's insistence that corroboration is a past-tense report, not a probability, is the philosophy-of-science backing for the project's pre-registered falsifiers: a gauge-equivariance identity, a golden-test parity claim, or a scaling prediction earns standing only by forbidding an observable outcome and then surviving the attempt to produce it.

The same stance feeds the [[Structural realism]] commitment of the multi-agent model. Popper's fallibilism and verisimilitude — false theories superseded by ones closer to the truth, with *structure* (the surviving testable content) as what carries forward — is the diachronic counterpart to the structural realist's reply to the pessimistic meta-induction, and pairs naturally with [[worrall-1989-structural-realism]]. Read against [[kuhn-1962-structure]] it marks the project's deliberate tension: the debate panels are Popperian (theories live or die by severe test), while the consensus-formation and paradigm-shift modeling borrows the Kuhnian and reticulated ([[laudan-1984-science-and-values]]) pictures of how communities of agents actually move. The book's diagnosis of irrefutability as pseudo-science is also the principled guard the panels invoke against confirmation-seeking, complementing the psychological account in [[nickerson-1998-confirmation-bias]].

> [!note] Editorial: The "conjecture/refutation" framing of the project's debate and review panels is an interpretive alignment with Popper's methodology, not a claim that the agents implement Popperian logic formally. Popper's own theory of *corroboration* is famously non-probabilistic and was contested (notably by Bayesian confirmation theorists); the project's quantitative falsifiers — likelihoods, posterior comparisons, golden-test tolerances — are closer in machinery to a Bayesian severe-testing account than to Popper's literal degree-of-corroboration. The citation is to the demarcation-and-bold-conjecture ethos, which the program adopts as a working norm.
