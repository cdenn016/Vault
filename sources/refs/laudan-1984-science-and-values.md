# Science and Values: The Aims of Science and Their Role in Scientific Debate

> [!info] Citation
> Laudan, Larry (1984). *Science and Values: The Aims of Science and Their Role in Scientific Debate*. Berkeley: University of California Press.

## TL;DR

Laudan replaces the hierarchical picture of scientific justification (values fixed at the top, then methods, then theories) with the *reticulated model*: aims, methods, and factual claims mutually constrain and adjust one another, with justification flowing in all directions through the network rather than top-down. This dissolves the Kuhnian threat of incommensurable, value-driven paradigm choice by showing how rational debate at one level can be anchored by agreement at others. The book is also a touchstone for the pessimistic-meta-induction debate: the historical record of discarded-yet-successful theories pressures naive convergent realism.

## What it establishes

- **Reticulated model of scientific rationality.** Aims, methods, and theories form a mutually adjusting triad; no level is foundationally fixed. Disagreement at one level is adjudicated via shared commitments at the others.
- **Against hierarchical justification.** Rejects the view that values/aims sit immune at the apex; values themselves are revisable in light of methods and facts.
- **Bearing on pessimistic meta-induction.** Laudan's historical catalogue of successful-but-false theories is a principal source for doubting that success tracks truth (the problem structural realism answers).

## Why the project cites it

Laudan's reticulated model is a natural fit for the manuscript's multi-agent, multi-level account of how scientific consensus forms and shifts. Where Kuhn ([[kuhn-1962-structure]]) makes paradigm change look like a discontinuous gestalt-switch, Laudan supplies the mechanism of *piecemeal, network-distributed* adjustment — a closer analogue to the program's gradual, coupled belief-updating across agents under [[Variational free energy]], where commitments at different levels (aims/priors, methods/dynamics, facts/observations) co-adjust rather than being fixed in a strict hierarchy. This supports the [[Participatory realism (it from bit)]] account of objectivity as a consensus achievement and feeds the pessimistic-meta-induction motivation behind the project's [[Structural realism]] (structure, not object-identity, is what survives the historical churn; cf. [[worrall-1989-structural-realism]]). It also connects to the social-epistemology thread of [[Longino 2002|longino-2002-fate-of-knowledge]].
